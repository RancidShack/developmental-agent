# Category-Level Generalisation in the Threat Layer of a Small Artificial Learner via Cell-Type Signature-Matching

**Author:** Nicholas P M Baker, Synapstak Ltd
**Date:** 25 April 2026
**Status:** Working draft — title, abstract, Section 1

---

## Abstract

A preceding paper (Baker, 2026d) introduced experience-driven persistent threat representation as the architectural response to the unbounded-accumulation problem identified in v0.9 (Baker, 2026c). The threat layer stabilised Phase 3 hazard avoidance at extended run lengths and reduced cumulative cost by 50 to 75 per cent against the v0.9 baseline, while preserving the developmental signatures established in v0.8. One boundary condition was characterised honestly: at cost 5.0 at 20,000 steps, the per-cell-no-generalisation property of the architecture interacted with Phase 3's time budget to produce 5 of 10 Phase 3 cleanness against the otherwise-uniform 8 to 10 of 10 across the matrix. The mechanism was identifiable: agents who encountered novel hazards late in Phase 2 or during Phase 3 itself could not complete the three-entry flag conversion within the remaining time budget. v0.12 is the iteration directed at this finding, with category-level generalisation in the threat layer as the architectural goal and signature-matching with first-entry flagging as the pre-registered mechanism.

The architectural change is contained to a single rule. When the agent enters a hazard cell, the architecture checks whether any other hazard cell has its threat flag set. If yes, the entered cell's flag is set on first entry. If no, the standard three-entry conversion rule from v0.10 and v0.11.2 applies. The signature is cell type alone, not perceptual feature, not adjacency pattern. Richer signatures are reserved for v0.14 and beyond.

Across a pre-registered batch of 180 runs spanning six cost levels and three run lengths, with seeds matched to the v0.11.2 batch (Baker, 2026g), v0.12 produces the pre-registered Category A outcome on all three checks. Phase 3 cleanness at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps condition reaches 10 of 10. Phase 3 cleanness at 160,000 steps reaches 60 of 60 across all six cost levels. Mean attractors mastered at 160,000 steps holds at 5.80 of 6, and the v0.11.2 individuation Category F is not triggered on any of its three signals. The signature-matching mechanism behaves exactly as the pre-registration predicts: across 162 runs in which any hazard was flagged, the strict invariant *first-entry conversions equals hazards-flagged minus one* holds in 162 of 162 cases.

Two findings depart from the pre-registration's anticipated narrative and are reported here as the substantive contribution of the paper. The first concerns the v0.10 outlier itself. The pre-registration assumed the outlier persists in v0.11.2; it does not. v0.11.2 produces 10 of 10 cleanness at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps condition under matched seeds, against v0.10's 8 of 10. The four mastery interventions developed across the v0.11 series appear to suppress the threat-side outlier as a side-effect, presumably by redirecting Phase 2 attention away from the late-hazard-encounter pattern that produced the boundary condition in v0.10. v0.12 therefore provides a deliberate architectural mechanism for what v0.11.2 achieved incidentally; the paper reports both findings and treats them as the more honest reading of what the threat-layer programme has actually established. The second finding concerns the cost economics. Across the matrix, v0.12 pays between 60 and 93 per cent of the cumulative cost v0.11.2 pays under matched seeds. The pre-registration predicted this advantage would shrink as run length increased; the data shows the opposite. At 20,000 steps the cost ratio ranges from 0.72 to 0.93. At 80,000 steps it tightens to 0.66 to 0.81. At 160,000 steps it tightens further to 0.60 to 0.70. The advantage v0.12 has over v0.11.2 grows with run length rather than shrinking, because v0.11.2 continues to discover and pay three-entry cost on novel hazards across the full run length, whereas v0.12 pays one entry's cost per signature-matched discovery after the first.

A pre-registered honesty constraint applies regardless of the operational result. The cell-type-only signature choice means the architecture's success at category-level generalisation cannot be distinguished from its success at unlocking type information the architecture already represented. The v0.12 batch alone is consistent with both readings. The v0.14 and later iterations testing richer signatures (adjacency patterns, environmental markers, perceptual features) are the necessary follow-on for distinguishing them. The architectural claim made in this paper is bounded accordingly.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Programme to date

A small tabular developmental agent occupying a 20×20 structured environment has, across previous papers, demonstrated five behavioural signatures associated with developmental learning, and has progressively acquired the architectural machinery that makes those signatures stable across extended horizons. v0.8 established the signatures themselves: rule adherence, focused autonomy, feature-aligned preference formation, a specialist-generalist trade-off, and full biographical individuation (Baker, 2026a, 2026b). v0.9 decomposed the v0.8 hazard handling into its constituent parts and identified a long-horizon instability: cost-based learning produced bounded distributed knowledge that could not resist erosion by the unbounded preference accumulator at extended run lengths (Baker, 2026c). v0.10 closed this gap on the hazard side by introducing experience-driven persistent threat representation (Baker, 2026d). v0.11 closed it on the mastery side, but did so through a sequence of three amendments that decomposed the architectural goal of "no signal pulling back" into the four code-path interventions it actually required (Baker, 2026e, 2026f, 2026g). v0.11.2 is the inheritance baseline for the present work.

The arc the programme has been tracing is by now visible enough to name. v0.9 identified that the v0.8 architecture lacked persistent storage for any learned categorical knowledge. v0.10 supplied it for hazards. v0.11 supplied it for mastery. v0.12 is the first iteration after both gaps are closed, and the question it addresses is no longer whether the architecture can store categorical knowledge, but whether it can generalise that knowledge across exemplars of the same category.

### 1.2 The v0.10 outlier and what v0.12 was designed to address

The v0.10 paper reported full Phase 3 cleanness across all six cost levels at 160,000 steps, with one boundary condition characterised honestly. At cost 5.0 at 20,000 steps, v0.10 produced 5 of 10 Phase 3 clean runs against the otherwise-uniform 8 to 10 of 10 across the matrix. The mechanism was identified in Section 4 of that paper. The per-cell-no-generalisation property of the v0.10 threat layer requires three entries per hazard cell to convert each cell's flag to its persistent state. Agents who encountered novel hazards late in Phase 2 or during Phase 3 itself could not complete the three-entry conversion within the remaining time budget when the run length was short and the cost was high enough for Phase 3 entries to count as cleanness violations. The boundary effect disappeared at longer run lengths because Phase 3 had more steps available for late-encountered hazards to reach conversion.

The v0.10 paper named the architectural question this finding opens. Would adding category-level generalisation to the threat layer eliminate the boundary condition by allowing late-encountered hazards to be flagged on first entry rather than after three? v0.10 reserved this as a separate research question for a subsequent iteration. The v0.12 iteration is that follow-on work.

The pre-registration committed before any v0.12 code was written specified signature-matching with first-entry flagging on cell-type signature as the architectural mechanism. The choice was settled in the design conversation (Baker, internal record, 25 April 2026) on three grounds. Signature-matching addressed the v0.10 outlier mechanism directly rather than as a side effect. It preserved the experience-driven character of the architecture, since agents must still encounter cells to flag them; the change is that one experience of category-recognition is sufficient where three experiences of specific-cell-learning were previously required for each subsequent cell. And it maintained single-variable-change discipline at the research-question level: v0.12 changes one rule in `update_threat_layer` and inherits everything else from v0.11.2.

### 1.3 Pre-registered commitments and the Category F honesty constraint

The pre-registration (Baker, 2026h) specified the experimental matrix, the metrics, and six interpretation categories. Five of these (A through E) follow the v0.10 / v0.11 pattern: full success, partial success, no improvement, over-flagging, and developmental degradation. The sixth, Category F, is specific to v0.12 and is named here because it shapes what the paper can claim regardless of the operational result.

Category F is the pre-registered honesty constraint on the cell-type-only signature choice. The architecture already represents cell types directly through the `hazard_cells` set in the world's environment specification. The v0.12 architectural change is the rule that allows this existing information to act on flag conversion. If v0.12 succeeds at the operational level — eliminating the v0.10 outlier and producing first-entry flag conversions on signature match — the success is consistent with two architectural readings that the v0.12 batch alone cannot distinguish. The first reading is that category-level generalisation works in this architecture, that one experience of category recognition is sufficient for the architecture to extend learned avoidance to other cells of the same category. The second reading is more deflationary: that the architecture's existing cell-type representation was sufficient for the behavioural change once the rule allowed it to act, and that no genuine generalisation occurred — the architecture simply began using information it had always carried. Both readings are consistent with the v0.12 data.

The pre-registration commits in advance to reporting this honestly. The architectural claim made in this paper is bounded by Category F. Iterations testing richer signatures (perceptual features added to the environment, adjacency patterns across cells, learned features rather than environment-specified types) are the necessary follow-on for distinguishing the two readings, and are reserved for v0.14 and beyond.

The pre-registration also names two scoping decisions that shape what v0.12 does and does not test. The successful-avoidance bonus, considered as a candidate change at design time, is deferred to a subsequent iteration on the grounds that adding it would constitute a second architectural change and break single-variable-change discipline. The signature is cell type alone, not adjacency or perceptual marker, on the grounds that cell-type-only is the simplest test of whether category-level generalisation works at all in this architecture; richer signatures are the natural extension once the simpler version is established.

### 1.4 Findings and their relation to the pre-registration

The paper reports three substantive findings. The first is the pre-registered Category A outcome: full elimination of the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps cleanness gap, full stabilisation at 160,000 steps across all cost levels, and full preservation of the v0.11.2 mastery-and-individuation signatures. The second is the mechanistic confirmation: across all 162 runs in the batch in which any hazard was flagged, the pre-registered strict invariant — that first-entry conversions equal hazards-flagged minus one — holds in 162 of 162 cases. The signature-matching mechanism behaves exactly as the pre-registration predicts, with no edge cases.

The third and fourth findings depart from the pre-registration's anticipated narrative and are the more substantive contribution of the present work.

The third concerns the v0.10 outlier itself. The pre-registration assumed the outlier persists in v0.11.2; it does not. Under matched seeds drawn from the v0.11.2 batch, v0.11.2 produces 10 of 10 Phase 3 clean runs at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps condition. v0.10 at the same matched-seed condition produces 8 of 10. The four mastery interventions developed across the v0.11 series — depleted feature reward, cleared attraction bias, preference reset at banking, blocked preference accumulation post-banking — appear to have suppressed the threat-side outlier as a side-effect, presumably by redirecting Phase 2 attention away from the late-hazard-encounter pattern that produced the boundary condition in v0.10. v0.12 therefore does not eliminate a problem present in v0.11.2; v0.12 provides a deliberate architectural mechanism for an outcome v0.11.2 had already achieved incidentally. The threat-side outlier and its disappearance are characterised in Section 4 of this paper, not as a v0.12 finding but as a finding about the inheritance chain v0.12 sits within.

The fourth concerns the cost economics of the threat layer at extended run lengths. The pre-registration predicted v0.12 would reduce cumulative cost relative to v0.11.2, with the magnitude of the reduction shrinking at longer run lengths because v0.11.2 already pays bounded cost per flagged hazard. The data inverts this prediction. The cost ratio at 20,000 steps ranges from 0.72 to 0.93 across cost levels. At 80,000 steps it tightens to 0.66 to 0.81. At 160,000 steps it tightens further to 0.60 to 0.70. The advantage v0.12 holds over v0.11.2 grows with run length rather than shrinking. The mechanism, identified post-hoc in Section 5, is that v0.11.2 continues to discover and pay three-entry cost on novel hazards across the full run length, while v0.12 pays one entry's cost per signature-matched discovery after the first. At long horizons, where the v0.10 paper reported a mean of 2.22 of 5 hazards flagged per run by 160,000 steps, the difference between three-entry conversion and one-entry conversion compounds substantially.

The Category F honesty constraint applies to the operational findings reported above. The architectural claim — that v0.12 demonstrates category-level generalisation in the threat layer — is bounded by the cell-type-only signature choice. The v0.12 batch is consistent with the deflationary reading (existing type representation acting on the rule change) and the v0.14 and later iterations are required to distinguish them.

### 1.5 Connection to the broader programme

The v0.10, v0.11, and v0.12 iterations together establish the architectural pattern the v0.9 instability identified as missing. Persistent categorical representation closes the unbounded-accumulation gap on the hazard side (v0.10) and the mastery side (v0.11.2). Category-level generalisation extends learned categorical knowledge across exemplars of the same category, at least in the bounded sense the cell-type signature permits (v0.12). What the architecture as it now stands does not have, and what subsequent iterations are designed to address, is end-state target activation — the question of whether the architecture's developmental schedule can be terminated by the architecture itself, on its own evidence about whether learning is complete, rather than by the externally-imposed phase boundaries the v0.8 schedule specifies. This question is reserved for v0.13 (Baker, in preparation).

The methodological discipline that has held throughout the programme — pre-registration before computation, single-variable change per iteration, behavioural preservation audit across iterations, honest reporting of findings as they occur — held during this iteration. The v0.12 implementation completed in a single pass without surfacing implementation-time discoveries that would have required amendment. This is the empirical answer to the question Section 9 of the pre-registration raised: whether tightening the design discipline in advance (settling four open considerations before the pre-registration was drafted) would let v0.12 implement cleanly in one pass. On this evidence, it has. The amendment budget for the iteration remains untouched.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Environment

The environment is identical to that used in v0.8 through v0.11.2 (Baker, 2026a, 2026d, 2026g). A 20×20 grid contains four cell types. Frame cells form the environmental boundary and are impassable. Hazard cells are clustered in two groups, a three-cell cluster at coordinates (5, 8), (5, 9), (6, 8) and a two-cell cluster at (14, 13), (14, 14); they are passable at scalar cost. Attractor cells are six fixed locations at coordinates (3, 3), (16, 3), (9, 10), (4, 15), (15, 16), and (11, 5), at which the agent receives feature reward on entry and which the v0.11 mastery mechanism handles. Neutral cells fill the remainder of the passable interior. The agent starts each run at coordinate (1, 1) and operates under the three-phase developmental schedule inherited unchanged: prescribed acquisition (Phase 1), drive-based integration (Phase 2), and preference-weighted autonomy (Phase 3), with phase transitions at the deterministic completion of the boustrophedon path and at 60 per cent of total run length respectively.

### 2.2 v0.12 architectural specification

The v0.12 architecture inherits v0.11.2 unchanged except for one modification to the flag-conversion rule in the threat layer. The change is contained entirely within `update_threat_layer`.

Under v0.11.2 (and v0.10 before it), when the agent enters a hazard cell, the cell's per-cell entry counter increments by one. When the counter reaches the pre-registered threshold of three, the cell's threat flag is set to one. The conversion is one-way; flags do not clear at any point in the run. Once a hazard cell's flag is set, the action-selection pipeline excludes any action targeting that cell from consideration before Q-value computation occurs.

Under v0.12, when the agent enters a hazard cell, the architecture first checks whether any other hazard cell has its threat flag set. If yes, the entered cell's threat flag is set to one immediately on this entry, the cell is added to the run's flagged-cells set, and the v0.12 metric *first-entry conversions* is incremented. The standard three-entry counter is not modified for signature-matched conversions, since the cell will not be entered again once flagged. If no other hazard cell is flagged, the standard v0.10 / v0.11.2 three-entry conversion rule applies as before.

The implementation diff against `curiosity_agent_v0_11_2.py` is precise. In `update_threat_layer`, before the existing counter-and-threshold check, a category-membership predicate is evaluated:

```python
any_other_flagged = any(
    self.threat_flag.get(h, 0) == 1
    for h in self.world.hazard_cells
    if h != entered_cell
)
```

If the predicate is true and the entered cell is not already flagged, the cell's flag is set on this first entry and the v0.12 timing metrics record the conversion. If the predicate is false, control falls through to the original three-entry rule. The diff is twelve lines of executable logic plus comment lines.

The signature on which matching occurs is cell type alone. The architecture treats all cells in `world.hazard_cells` as members of the same category for the purposes of flag transfer. There is no perceptual feature, no adjacency pattern, no environmental marker. The first hazard cell the agent flags in any run still requires three entries — at the time of its first three encounters, no other hazard cell is flagged, so the predicate evaluates false and the standard rule applies. Every subsequent novel hazard cell the agent encounters after at least one hazard is flagged is converted on first entry.

### 2.3 What v0.12 does not change

All other architectural elements from v0.11.2 are preserved unchanged. The hard-gate action-selection rule remains: any action targeting a flagged cell is excluded from the action-selection pipeline before Q-value computation, primitive bias computation, or epsilon-greedy random selection. The hazard cost mechanism remains: each entry into an unflagged hazard cell delivers the configured hazard cost as a negative addition to the agent's intrinsic reward stream. The mastery layer remains in full: per-attractor visit counter, mastery flag, MASTERY_THRESHOLD = 3, MASTERY_BONUS = 1.0. The four mastery interventions developed across the v0.11 series — zero feature reward post-mastery, cleared attraction bias for mastered cells, preference reset at banking, blocked preference accumulation post-banking — are inherited verbatim. The three-phase developmental schedule, drive composition at each phase, learning rate 0.1, discount factor 0.9, and epsilon 0.1 are inherited verbatim. The pre-wired aversion bias of −5.0 for frame-adjacent actions is retained; hazards have no pre-wired aversion. The single-variable-change discipline holds at the research-question level: v0.12 changes one rule.

### 2.4 Experimental matrix and matched-seed comparison

The experimental matrix is one architecture (v0.12) crossed with six cost levels (0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0) crossed with three run lengths (20,000, 80,000, 160,000 steps) crossed with ten runs per cell, totalling 180 runs. The cost levels and run lengths are retained from v0.10 and v0.11.2 to enable direct comparison across the threat-layer programme.

For each (cost, run length, run index) triple, the random seed is matched to the v0.11.2 batch (Baker, 2026g) at the same condition. The seeds are not regenerated by formula; they are loaded from the v0.11.2 CSV directly. The reason is technical and is named here for the methodological record. Python's `hash()` of a tuple containing strings is randomised per interpreter session because `PYTHONHASHSEED` defaults to a non-deterministic value for security reasons. The same `hash(...)` formula run in two different Python sessions will not produce the same integer seeds. The v0.11.2 batch's seeds are recorded data, fixed in the CSV, and the only way to guarantee matched-seed comparison is to load them. The v0.12 batch runner refuses to execute if `run_data_v0_11_2.csv` is not present in the working directory.

Matched seeding produces a within-seed comparison up to the point at which the threat layer first intervenes differently in v0.12 from v0.11.2. The first hazard cell a v0.12 agent flags converts via the same three-entry path as in v0.11.2 — at the time of those three entries, no other hazard is flagged, so the v0.12 rule reduces to the v0.11.2 rule. Divergence between v0.12 and v0.11.2 begins at the moment the agent enters a second hazard cell after the first has been flagged. From that point on, v0.12 takes one entry to flag the cell where v0.11.2 would have taken three. All differences in cumulative cost, time-to-final-flag, and Phase 3 entries observed between v0.12 and v0.11.2 at matched seeds are attributable to this divergence specifically.

The v0.10 architecture's batch results are also drawn from the v0.11.2 batch CSV, which contains both v0.10 and v0.11.2 architectures at matched seeds. v0.10 is therefore the comparison architecture in this paper for the v0.10 outlier characterisation in Section 4, with the same matched-seed guarantees that hold for v0.11.2.

### 2.5 Metrics

All v0.10 metrics and all v0.11.2 metrics are retained.

From v0.10: hazard entries by phase (Phase 1, 2, 3, total); Phase 3 cleanness, defined as fewer than three hazard entries during Phase 3; full-run cleanness; number of hazard cells flagged per run; time to first flag; time to final flag; cost paid up to final flag; actions gated by threat layer per phase. From v0.11.2: per-attractor visit counts at end of run; mastery sequence (the order in which attractors crossed the mastery threshold); first-banked attractor identity; mean attractors mastered per run; Phase 3 visits to mastered versus unmastered attractors; the three-signal individuation framework (distinct top-attractor count, most-common-mastery-sequence proportion, rank-versus-distance Pearson correlation).

Three metrics are introduced for v0.12. *Time to second flag* records the step at which the second hazard cell's flag was converted, regardless of conversion path. Together with time to first flag, this gives the time-from-first-to-second-flag gap, which is the direct mechanistic signature of the v0.12 architectural change: under v0.11.2 this gap is bounded below by the time the agent takes to enter a second hazard cell three times after the first hazard's flag has converted, while under v0.12 it is bounded below by the time the agent takes to enter any second hazard cell once. *First-entry flag conversions* counts the number of hazard cells whose flag converted on first entry under the signature-matching rule. Under v0.11.2 this metric is zero by construction; under v0.12 the pre-registered prediction is that it equals (hazards flagged − 1) for every run in which any hazard is flagged. *Cost paid on signature-matched hazards* accumulates the cost paid on cells whose flag converted on first entry, bounded at one HAZARD_COST per signature-matched cell by construction.

Per-run numerical data is preserved in CSV format. Aggregate findings are reported per-cost-level and per-run-length below.

### 2.6 Pre-registered interpretation categories

Six interpretation categories are pre-registered in `v0.12-preregistration.md`, Section 7. Five (A through E) follow the v0.10 / v0.11.2 pattern. Category A specifies full elimination of the outlier with full preservation, defined by three checks: Phase 3 cleanness ≥ 9 of 10 at cost 5.0 at 20,000 steps; Phase 3 cleanness ≥ 55 of 60 at 160,000 steps across all six cost levels; mean attractors mastered ≥ 5.5 of 6 at 160,000 steps with the v0.11.2 individuation Category F not triggered. Categories B and C specify partial elimination and no improvement at the outlier condition, with the audits otherwise preserved at Category A thresholds. Category D specifies over-flagging, with three sub-conditions distinguishing different ways the architecture could flag cells incorrectly. Category E specifies developmental degradation, with quantitative thresholds defined against v0.10 and v0.11.2 baselines.

Category F is the v0.12-specific honesty constraint named in Section 1.3 of this paper. It applies whenever the operational result eliminates the v0.10 outlier, regardless of whether that elimination meets Category A thresholds. The architectural claim made in any v0.12 paper is bounded by Category F. The pre-registration commits in advance to reporting this honestly and to identifying the v0.14 and later iterations as the necessary follow-on for distinguishing the architectural reading ("category-level generalisation works") from the deflationary reading ("existing type representation was sufficient once unlocked").

The pre-registration document was committed to the public repository at github.com/RancidShack/developmental-agent before any v0.12 code was written, in accordance with the methodological commitments named in Section 11 of that document.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Headline finding: Category A satisfied on all three checks

At the outlier condition (cost 5.0, 20,000 steps), v0.12 produces 10 of 10 Phase 3 clean runs. The pre-registered Check 1 threshold (≥ 9 of 10) is exceeded; the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps cleanness gap is fully eliminated.

At 160,000 steps, v0.12 produces 60 of 60 Phase 3 clean runs across all six cost levels. The pre-registered Check 2 threshold (≥ 55 of 60) is exceeded; full stabilisation holds across the matrix at the longest run length tested.

The v0.11.2 mastery and individuation findings are preserved at 160,000 steps. Mean attractors mastered is 5.80 of 6 (Check 3a threshold ≥ 5.5 of 6: PASS). On the three-signal individuation framework, none of the signals trigger: distinct first-banked attractors stand at 5 of 6 (signal threshold < 4: NOT triggered); the most common mastery sequence accounts for 10.0 per cent of runs (signal threshold > 40 per cent: NOT triggered); the rank-versus-distance Pearson correlation is −0.057 (signal threshold > 0.7: NOT triggered). The v0.11.2 individuation Category F is not triggered on any signal (Check 3b: PASS).

All three pre-registered checks pass. The outcome is the pre-registered Category A: full elimination of the outlier with full preservation.

### 3.2 The signature-matching mechanism behaves as predicted

Across the 180-run batch, hazards are flagged in 162 runs (47 at 20,000 steps, 56 at 80,000 steps, 59 at 160,000 steps). The pre-registered strict invariant is that *first-entry conversions equals hazards-flagged minus one* for every run in which any hazard is flagged: the first hazard cell flagged in any run requires three entries, since at the time of those three entries no other hazard is flagged; every subsequent flagged cell converts on first entry under the signature-matching rule.

The invariant holds in 162 of 162 runs across the batch (100.0 per cent). At 20,000 steps the invariant holds in 47 of 47 flagged runs; at 80,000 in 56 of 56; at 160,000 in 59 of 59. The architectural mechanism behaves exactly as the pre-registration predicts, with no edge cases.

The mechanistic signature is also visible in the time-to-second-flag distribution. The mean gap between time to first flag and time to second flag, across all runs in which both are recorded, is 1,689 steps at 20,000 steps total run length, 5,384 steps at 80,000, and 5,044 steps at 160,000. The minimum gaps are 1, 1, and 1 step respectively; the maximum gaps are 10,171, 30,766, and 42,381. The minimum-of-one-step finding is the architectural signature in its purest form: in some runs the agent encounters a second hazard cell immediately after the first hazard's third-entry conversion, and the second cell flags on the very next step. Under v0.11.2 the equivalent gap would be bounded below by the time required for three further entries into the second hazard cell.

The cost on signature-matched cells likewise tracks the architectural prediction. The metric counts cost paid on cells whose flag converted on first entry, bounded at one HAZARD_COST per signature-matched cell by construction. Mean values are 1.10 at 20,000 steps total run length, 2.77 at 80,000, and 3.53 at 160,000 (all expressed in cost-units; the absolute magnitude scales with HAZARD_COST). The metric grows with run length because more hazards are encountered at longer horizons, and each post-first hazard contributes one entry's cost rather than three.

The mechanism is identifiable post-hoc and turns on a property of v0.11.2's hazard discovery dynamics that the pre-registration did not weight correctly. v0.11.2 does not stop encountering novel hazards once a few have been flagged. The v0.10 paper reported a mean of 1.37 of 5 hazards flagged per run at 20,000 steps, 1.80 at 80,000, and 2.22 at 160,000 (Baker, 2026d, Table 3). At the longest run length, just over two hazards per run on average are reaching the three-entry conversion threshold, with later-encountered hazards reaching threshold progressively. v0.11.2's hazard-discovery dynamics are similar; the four mastery interventions affect Phase 3 attention but not the rate at which novel hazards are discovered. Each newly discovered hazard cell costs three entries to flag under v0.11.2 and one entry to flag under v0.12 (after the first hazard in the run). At long horizons, where more hazards are discovered, the difference between three-entry conversion and one-entry conversion compounds across more discoveries. The cost advantage v0.12 holds therefore grows with run length, not shrinks.

This is a finding the present work reports without overclaiming its theoretical significance. The mechanism producing the cost reduction is the architectural fact that signature-matched hazards convert on first entry. The finding is that this fact has a substantively measurable behavioural consequence whose magnitude is run-length-dependent in the opposite direction the pre-registration anticipated. Whether the same asymmetry — between continuous-cost-per-discovery learning and one-time-cost-per-discovery learning — operates in biological developmental systems is a separate question this paper does not address.

### 3.4 v0.11.2 mastery inheritance preserved

The v0.11.2 mastery and individuation findings are preserved across the v0.12 batch at the resolution the metrics measure.

Mean attractors mastered grows with run length under v0.12 in the same pattern v0.11.2 produced: 4.52 of 6 at 20,000 steps, 5.42 of 6 at 80,000, 5.80 of 6 at 160,000. The v0.11.2 batch reported 4.62, 5.47, and 5.87 at the matched conditions; the differences (0.10, 0.05, 0.07) are within the variance the v0.11.2 batch itself produced and do not exceed any pre-registered threshold for developmental degradation.

Phase 3 visits to mastered attractors are 356.2, 392.8, and 464.4 mean visits at the three run lengths. Phase 3 visits to unmastered attractors hold at 0.0 across the matrix, matching v0.11.2's pattern of zero post-mastery interest in unbanked cells once mastery banking has occurred. Post-mastery visits to mastered attractors total 1,098, 2,602, and 4,308 mean visits across the run; these are within the bands v0.11.2 produced and confirm that the four mastery interventions remain effective under the v0.12 threat-layer change.

Individuation holds at all three run lengths. At 160,000 steps, distinct first-banked attractors stand at 5 of 6, the most common mastery sequence accounts for 10.0 per cent of runs, and the rank-versus-distance Pearson correlation is −0.057. None of the three v0.11.2 individuation signals trigger at any of the three run lengths tested. The biographical signature that has held throughout the programme — identical agents in identical conditions becoming different agents through their specific developmental trajectories — holds under v0.12 as it held under v0.11.2.

The v0.10 secondary signatures (concentration, attractor visit ratio) are likewise preserved within the bands v0.11.2 produced. Pre-registered Category E (developmental degradation) is firmly not triggered.

### 3.5 Methodological note: the meta-report bug fix

A bug was identified in the v0.12 batch runner's meta-report logic after the initial 180-run batch had completed. The bug affected the textual conclusion of the report's Category A assessment block: a chained `is True` identity check failed when one of its operands was `numpy.bool_` (returned by `np.mean(...) >= 5.5`) rather than Python's `True` singleton, causing the report to conclude "CATEGORY A NOT MET" despite all three pre-registered checks passing in the report's own per-check output. The data in the CSV was unaffected; the bug was contained to two lines in the report-generation block.

The fix was applied to the batch runner: `cat_a_check_3` is now wrapped in `bool(...)` at assignment, and the final conditional uses truthiness rather than identity comparison. The batch was re-run with the patched runner under matched seeds drawn from `run_data_v0_11_2.csv`. The new CSV is byte-identical to the previous CSV (MD5 hash matches across the two outputs). The architecture and seeds are deterministic on this environment to the byte: same code, same seeds, same NumPy and Python versions produce the same 180-run output without variation.

The methodological record is therefore: bug identified after first batch completed; fix applied to the runner; batch re-run under matched seeds; per-row data byte-identical between the two runs; only the corrected report conclusion differs between the two outputs. The byte-identical re-run is a non-trivial reproducibility property of the v0.12 implementation on this environment and is reported here as such. The data of record is the post-fix CSV; the report cited in this paper is the post-fix meta-report.

The v0.12.1 designation was considered for the patched runner and not adopted, on the methodological reasoning named in the lab record: amendment designations in this programme have been reserved for architectural changes (the v0.11.1 preference reset, the v0.11.2 blocked accumulation, each accompanied by a pre-registered amendment document). A textual fix to a report-conclusion conditional does not reach the bar the v0.11 series established for amendment status. The fix is recorded as a bug commit against `curiosity_agent_v0_12_batch.py` and the iteration's amendment budget remains untouched.

## 4. The v0.10 outlier and the v0.11.2 inheritance finding

This section addresses the v0.10 outlier that the v0.12 iteration was designed to eliminate, and the secondary finding that emerged from the matched-seed comparison: that the outlier was already absent from v0.11.2 before v0.12 was applied. The section is structured to characterise both findings honestly. v0.12 does not eliminate a problem present in its inheritance baseline; v0.12 provides a deliberate architectural mechanism for an outcome the inheritance baseline had achieved incidentally. Both observations are findings of the present work.

### 4.1 The v0.10 outlier as the v0.10 paper characterised it

The v0.10 paper (Baker, 2026d, Section 4) reported an outlier at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps cell of its experimental matrix. v0.10 produced 5 of 10 Phase 3 clean runs at this condition against the otherwise-uniform 8 to 10 of 10 across the rest of the matrix. The mean Phase 3 entries at the outlier condition were 1.90, against the otherwise-uniform 0.0 to 0.6 elsewhere. The mechanism was identified through inspection of per-run data: agents who encountered novel hazards late in Phase 2 or during Phase 3 itself could not complete the three-entry flag conversion within Phase 3's time budget when the run length was short and the cost was high enough for Phase 3 entries to count as cleanness violations. Under cost 5.0, Phase 3 entries each contributed 5 units of cost and counted toward the cleanness threshold; under the 20,000-step run length, Phase 3 had only 8,000 steps available for late-encountered hazards to complete their conversion. The boundary effect disappeared at longer run lengths because Phase 3 had more steps available.

The v0.10 paper characterised this finding as a real architectural property of the per-cell-no-generalisation design choice, expressed in its boundary-condition behaviour rather than as a failure of the architecture. The outlier was reported without reshaping. The architectural implication, made explicit in the v0.10 paper's Section 4.4, was that the per-cell mechanism produces variation in time-to-final-flag across runs as a function of the agent's specific exploration trajectory. Some agents encountered all five hazards during Phase 1 and Phase 2; others encountered only some, with the remainder either never approached at all or approached too late to complete conversion. The outlier condition was the regime in which "approached late but at sufficient cost to count" was the dominant pattern across runs.

The v0.12 iteration was, on the pre-registration's reading of this finding, the architectural follow-on for resolving the outlier mechanism. Signature-matching with first-entry flagging would convert late-encountered hazards on first entry rather than after three, eliminating the time-budget pressure that produced the boundary effect. The pre-registration's Category A Check 1 — Phase 3 cleanness ≥ 9 of 10 at the outlier condition — was the operational target.

### 4.2 The matched-seed finding: v0.11.2 already produced 10 of 10

When the v0.11.2 batch (Baker, 2026g) was re-examined alongside v0.12 results at matched seeds, an observation surfaced that the v0.11.2 paper had not flagged. At the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps condition under matched seeds, v0.11.2 produces 10 of 10 Phase 3 clean runs. v0.10 at the same matched-seed condition produces 8 of 10. The v0.10 paper's own batch had reported 5 of 10 at this condition, but that was a separately-seeded run and the 8 of 10 figure under matched seeds is the like-for-like comparison.

The v0.12 batch produces 10 of 10 at the outlier condition. v0.11.2 produces 10 of 10 at the same condition. The pre-registered Category A Check 1 is satisfied by v0.12 — but it would have been satisfied by v0.11.2 alone, had the question been posed at the time the v0.11.2 batch was completed. The architectural mechanism v0.12 introduces is not what eliminated the outlier in this specific cell of the matrix; the four mastery interventions of the v0.11 series had already eliminated it before v0.12 was applied.

This is a finding the v0.12 paper records honestly because the methodological discipline of the programme demands it. The pre-registration was written on the assumption that the v0.10 outlier persists in v0.11.2; that assumption was wrong. The reasoning for the assumption was not unreasonable — v0.11 changed the mastery side of the architecture, not the threat side, so the threat-side outlier had no obvious reason to be affected by v0.11's changes. But the assumption was empirically false, and the matched-seed comparison surfaces the falsity directly.

### 4.3 Why the v0.11 mastery interventions suppress the threat-side outlier

The mechanism by which v0.11.2's mastery interventions affect threat-side cleanness is identifiable, though it was not anticipated at v0.11 design time and is reported here as a post-hoc characterisation rather than as a pre-registered finding.

The v0.10 outlier, as the v0.10 paper characterised it, depends on agents encountering novel hazards late in Phase 2 or during Phase 3 itself. Under v0.10, Phase 2 and Phase 3 attention was strongly drawn to attractor cells through the unbounded preference-and-feature accumulator that v0.11 was designed to bound. As the agent accumulated preference around attractor regions, time spent exploring distant regions of the environment (where some hazard cells sit) decreased. The v0.10 outlier ran on this dynamic: late hazards were late because the agent's attention had concentrated elsewhere, leaving novel hazards undiscovered until late in the run.

v0.11.2's four mastery interventions change this dynamic substantially. Once an attractor is mastered, its feature reward depletes to zero, its primitive attraction bias clears, its preference at banking is reset to zero, and ongoing preference accumulation on its cell is blocked. The mechanism that previously concentrated attention around attractor regions now releases that attention as each attractor is banked. The agent, post-mastery on a given attractor, is no longer drawn back to it; its exploration extends outward. The probability that a novel hazard cell is discovered earlier in Phase 2 — rather than late — increases as a side-effect of the post-mastery exploration extension. Late-encountered hazards become rarer, and the v0.10 outlier's underlying mechanism is correspondingly weakened.

This explanation is consistent with the timing data the v0.11.2 batch reported. Mean time to first mastery at 20,000 steps under v0.11.2 was 514 steps, well within Phase 1's prescribed traversal completion at step 331 plus a brief Phase 2 settling period. By Phase 2's mid-point, multiple attractors are typically banked, their attraction signals depleted, and the agent's exploration has extended into regions where the hazards sit. The hazards are encountered earlier in the run than under v0.10, and the three-entry conversion completes within Phase 3's time budget more reliably.

v0.11.2's mastery interventions therefore do not directly address the per-cell-no-generalisation property of the threat layer. The per-cell mechanism is unchanged under v0.11.2; each hazard cell still requires three entries to flag. What v0.11.2 changes is the agent's exploration pattern, which in turn changes the distribution of hazard-encounter timing. The outlier condition's underlying mechanism — late hazards meeting time-budget pressure — becomes rarer under v0.11.2 not because the architecture handles late hazards better, but because hazards are encountered earlier.

### 4.4 What v0.12 contributes against this baseline

The above account does not weaken the v0.12 contribution; it sharpens what the contribution is. v0.12 provides a deliberate architectural mechanism for the outcome v0.11.2 achieved as a side-effect. This is a meaningfully different claim from "v0.12 fixes a problem v0.11.2 had."

Three observations distinguish v0.12's contribution from v0.11.2's incidental suppression of the outlier.

The first concerns robustness across conditions not yet tested. v0.11.2's elimination of the outlier depends on a behavioural side-effect: the post-mastery exploration extension that pulls hazard discovery earlier in the run. Whether this side-effect operates reliably in environments with different attractor configurations, different hazard placements, or different cost regimes is an empirical question this batch does not answer. v0.12's signature-matching mechanism, by contrast, addresses the time-budget pressure directly through the architectural rule. Late-encountered hazards under v0.12 flag on first entry regardless of why they were encountered late, regardless of what the agent's exploration pattern looked like in Phase 2, and regardless of how mastery dynamics interacted with hazard discovery. v0.12's elimination of the boundary effect is mechanistic where v0.11.2's is contingent.

The second concerns the cost economics characterised in Section 3.3. v0.11.2's incidental suppression of the outlier does not reduce cumulative cost per discovered hazard; v0.11.2 still pays three entries' cost on every flagged hazard. v0.12 reduces this to one entry's cost on every signature-matched hazard. The cost-economics finding is attributable specifically to the v0.12 architectural mechanism and would not have emerged from v0.11.2's incidental dynamics alone.

The third concerns the architectural arc of the programme. v0.11.2's elimination of the outlier was incidental in two senses: it was not pre-registered as a v0.11 prediction, and it was not surfaced as a finding by the v0.11.2 paper. The matched-seed comparison performed in the v0.12 batch surfaces it now as a retrospective observation about what v0.11.2 already accomplished. v0.12 makes this same outcome a deliberate prediction, supported by the strict invariant that holds in 162 of 162 flagged runs. The architectural status of the finding shifts from "an emergent property the programme noticed in retrospect" to "a pre-registered behaviour the architecture demonstrates by design."

### 4.5 The Category F honesty constraint applies

Section 1.3 named Category F as the pre-registered honesty constraint on v0.12's architectural claim. The discussion above does not alter that constraint; it sharpens what the constraint applies to.

If v0.12's success at category-level generalisation reflects the architecture's existing cell-type representation acting on the rule change, rather than genuine generalisation, then the contributions identified in Section 4.4 are substantially weakened. The robustness-across-conditions argument depends on the mechanism being mechanistic; if the mechanism is "the architecture used type information it already carried," then robustness is contingent on environments in which cell types are similarly directly represented, and the apparent generalisation evaporates in environments where cell-type-equivalent information is not pre-given. The cost-economics finding survives in either reading, since the cost reduction is real regardless of why flag conversion accelerates. The architectural-arc argument is partially preserved, since the rule change is a deliberate architectural choice in either reading, but the strength of the claim — that the architecture demonstrates category-level generalisation — is the bounded one.

The richer-signature work reserved for v0.14 and beyond is the necessary follow-on for distinguishing the readings. Until that work is performed, the v0.12 paper's architectural claim is bounded by Category F: the signature-matching mechanism behaves as a category-level generalisation mechanism would behave, on the cell-type signature, in this environment, against this inheritance baseline. Whether it constitutes category-level generalisation in any deeper architectural sense is not determined by the v0.12 batch alone.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Category-level generalisation as architectural principle

The architectural principle the v0.12 result demonstrates is bounded but identifiable. When categorical knowledge is stored persistently, in a representational structure separate from the dynamics that handle graded preferences, a rule that propagates flag status across cells of the same category can implement a form of generalisation. One experience of the category — three entries into the first hazard cell, sufficient for that cell's flag to persist — extends to subsequent cells of the same category on first encounter. The architecture continues to require encounter; signature-matching is not the same as one-shot full generalisation, where any flagged cell would propagate flag status to all unencountered cells of the category. Encounter remains load-bearing. What changes is that one experience of category-recognition replaces three experiences of specific-cell-learning for each subsequent cell.

The principle generalises beyond the threat layer at least in candidate form. Any architectural element holding categorical learned knowledge — mastered attractors, learned hazard categories, future representations of skills, contexts, or social roles — could in principle support a similar signature-matching propagation. v0.11.2 already demonstrates that mastery is stored persistently and that mastered cells produce no signal pulling the agent back; what v0.11.2 does not demonstrate is whether mastery on one attractor propagates to other attractors of the same category. The threat side has now been tested for both persistence (v0.10) and category-level generalisation (v0.12); the mastery side has been tested for persistence (v0.11.2) but not for generalisation. Whether mastery generalises across attractor categories — whether, for instance, the experience of mastering one feature-bearing cell would accelerate mastery of others — is the natural follow-on question on the mastery side. The present paper does not address it.

The boundary the principle meets is the one Category F names. Cell-type signature-matching is consistent with the deflationary reading: that the architecture's existing type representation was the active ingredient and that no genuine generalisation occurred. Both readings produce the same per-run signatures: identical first-entry conversion counts, identical time-to-second-flag distributions, identical cost economics. The v0.12 batch cannot adjudicate between them. What would adjudicate is testing the principle on signatures the architecture does not directly represent — perceptual features added to the environment, adjacency patterns across cells, learned features rather than environment-specified types. If signature-matching continues to work when the signature requires the architecture to construct rather than retrieve the categorical relationship, the deflationary reading loses force. If it stops working under those conditions, the deflationary reading is supported. v0.14 and later iterations are the methodological vehicle for this distinction; the present paper marks the question open.

### 5.2 The cost-economics finding and what it means

The cost ratios reported in Section 3.3 — 0.84 mean across the matrix at 20,000 steps, 0.72 at 80,000, 0.66 at 160,000 — establish that v0.12's advantage over v0.11.2 grows with run length. The pre-registration predicted the opposite trend on what looked at design time like sensible reasoning: v0.11.2 already pays bounded cost per flagged hazard, so v0.12's reduction (one entry rather than three per signature-matched cell) should produce a smaller absolute saving at long horizons where v0.11.2's per-hazard cost is already bounded. The mistake in this reasoning is identifiable post-hoc and is worth naming, because it surfaces a property of the architecture's discovery dynamics that the threat-layer programme has been operating with implicitly.

The pre-registration's reasoning treated the number of hazards discovered per run as effectively constant across run lengths. It is not. v0.11.2 continues to discover novel hazards across the full run length; the v0.10 paper reported a mean of 1.37 of 5 hazards flagged per run at 20,000 steps rising to 2.22 at 160,000, with maximum time-to-first-flag values exceeding 100,000 steps. Some agents discover their first hazard extremely late in the run; others discover all five; the distribution is broad and run-length-dependent. v0.12 inherits this discovery dynamic — the agent's exploration patterns are governed by the v0.11.2 mastery layer, which v0.12 does not modify — and applies its one-entry conversion rule to each new discovery after the first. The number of signature-matched conversions per run therefore grows with run length, and the cumulative cost saving grows with it.

This is a finding worth carrying forward into the broader programme. The threat-layer cost economics suggest that v0.12 instantiates a pattern with a specific behavioural quality: bounded per-discovery cost that compounds favourably as discoveries accumulate. v0.11.2 instantiates a different pattern: bounded per-discovery cost that does not compound, since each new discovery costs the same three entries regardless of how many discoveries have occurred. The threat-layer programme has therefore moved through three distinct cost regimes in three iterations: v0.9 paid unbounded cost per hazard (as the agent kept encountering and re-encountering them across the full run length), v0.10 / v0.11.2 paid bounded cost per hazard (three entries per discovered cell), and v0.12 pays cost that decreases with the order of discovery (three entries for the first, one for each subsequent). What kind of learner an architecture instantiates depends materially on which of these regimes it operates in.

The finding also suggests that the run-length behaviour of an architecture is not a secondary diagnostic. v0.10 already demonstrated that run length matters for the stability of learned avoidance; v0.12 demonstrates that run length matters for the cost economics of categorical learning. Both findings argue that any future architecture in this programme should be evaluated across multiple run lengths as a matter of methodological discipline, not as a robustness check. The architectural quality that emerges at 160,000 steps is meaningfully different from the architectural quality that emerges at 20,000 steps, and which one is reported as the architecture's behaviour depends on which run length is treated as primary.

### 5.3 The v0.11.2-incidental-outlier-elimination as a finding about inheritance

Section 4 characterised the matched-seed observation that v0.11.2 already eliminated the v0.10 outlier before v0.12 was applied. The mechanism, identified post-hoc, is that the four mastery interventions of the v0.11 series change the agent's exploration pattern in a way that pulls hazard discovery earlier in the run, weakening the time-budget pressure that produced the boundary effect under v0.10. This subsection considers what the finding means for the broader programme.

The first observation is methodological. The v0.11 paper did not report this finding because the v0.11 paper's preserved-signatures audit was conducted against v0.10's central signatures (concentration, attractor ratio, mastery, individuation). The threat-side cleanness pattern was checked at the headline level — total Phase 3 cleanness counts — but the cell-by-cell distribution within the matrix was not examined for boundary-condition behaviour specifically. The matched-seed v0.10-versus-v0.11.2 comparison surfaced the cleanness improvement at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps cell only when the v0.12 batch made that specific comparison part of its evaluation. The finding was present in the v0.11.2 batch's CSV from the day it was committed; what was missing was the question that would have surfaced it.

The lesson the programme can take from this is that behavioural preservation audits should include cell-level matrix comparisons rather than headline-level totals alone. v0.11 changed the mastery side and produced findings on the mastery side; v0.11 also produced findings on the threat side as a side-effect, and those findings sat in the data unread for a v0.12 batch to surface. Future iterations may benefit from a more systematic post-batch sweep of all matched-seed cells where any architecture's cleanness counts differ, even when the differences are small and even when the iteration's pre-registered focus is elsewhere. The v0.10 paper's outlier characterisation is itself an example of this discipline operating well; the v0.11.2 paper missed the equivalent finding, and the v0.12 batch surfaced it.

The second observation concerns what the finding implies about the threat-and-mastery architecture as currently constituted. Until the v0.12 batch was completed, the threat-side and mastery-side architectural elements could be discussed independently: threat handling lived in the threat layer, mastery handling lived in the mastery layer, and the two layers interacted only through action selection (mastered cells lose their attraction signal; flagged cells are excluded from action selection). The finding that v0.11.2's mastery interventions affect threat-side cleanness through an exploration-pattern side-effect demonstrates that the layers are not independent in the way the architectural decomposition suggests. The mastery layer's effect on attention extends, via post-mastery exploration, to the rate at which novel hazards are encountered. The architecture's behaviour on the threat side is partially a function of decisions made on the mastery side.

This is not a problem; it is a property. Developmental architectures whose components affect each other through behavioural channels (rather than through direct representational coupling) are presumably more typical than the cleanly-decomposable architectures that single-iteration papers can suggest. What the finding asks of the programme is that future iterations characterise these cross-layer effects deliberately rather than letting them appear as side-effects in subsequent batches. v0.13 (end-state target activation) will need to consider how its modifications affect both the threat and mastery dynamics it inherits; v0.14 (richer signatures) will need to consider how the threat layer's richer-signature behaviour interacts with the mastery layer's exploration patterns. The decomposition the programme has been operating with — threat layer here, mastery layer there, single-variable changes one at a time — remains methodologically sound, but the layers themselves are more entangled in their behavioural consequences than the decomposition's clean separation suggests.

### 5.4 Limits of the present work

Three limits of the present work are worth naming explicitly, beyond the Category F honesty constraint already discussed at length.

The architecture remains tabular and small-scale. The 20×20 grid is sufficient for testing the architectural principle but does not address scale-related questions. Whether signature-matching scales to environments with thousands or millions of states, whether it generalises to non-tabular representations, whether it interacts with function approximators in ways that introduce drift or catastrophic interference — these are questions the present work does not address. The architectural principle is demonstrated in a sandbox; whether it survives migration to environments where representations must be learned rather than tabulated is a separate research question.

The signature in v0.12 is binary and matches on cell-type identity alone. There is no graded match strength, no probabilistic propagation, no decay or revision of flag status, no mechanism for retracting a flag if the agent later determines the signature was misapplied. These choices were deliberate single-variable simplifications to test whether the simplest possible signature-matching architecture works at all before more complex variants are explored. The simplest version works; whether richer variants — graded matching, probabilistic priors, revisable flags — produce different developmental signatures is a question subsequent iterations are reserved to address.

The behavioural preservation finding holds at the resolution the metrics measure. v0.11.2's signatures are preserved in v0.12 at the level of mean attractors mastered, individuation signal counts, Phase 3 visit ratios, and mastery sequence diversity. Finer-grained comparisons — the temporal structure of preference accumulation across phase boundaries, the within-run trajectory of attention shifts as attractors are progressively banked, the individual-agent biographical patterns at the level of step-by-step trajectories — might reveal subtle differences not captured by the aggregate signatures reported here. The conservative reading is that v0.12 preserves v0.11.2's findings at the granularity at which v0.11.2 was characterised, and that finer-grained comparison is a separate research question reserved for future work.

### 5.5 Architectural questions opened

Three architectural questions are opened or sharpened by the v0.12 findings, each of which is plausibly addressable through a separate iteration and is reserved for future work.

The first is the Category F question already named at length: distinguishing genuine category-level generalisation from existing-type-representation-acting-on-the-rule-change. The signature-matching mechanism in v0.12 is operationally clean, but the signature itself is information the architecture already carried. v0.14 (or later) iterations testing richer signatures — perceptual features, adjacency patterns, learned categorical relationships — are the methodological vehicle for the distinction. Until those iterations are completed, the architectural claim made in this paper is bounded.

The second is the mastery-side equivalent of the question v0.12 addresses. v0.11.2 demonstrates persistent categorical mastery representation; whether mastery on one attractor propagates to other attractors of the same category — an experience of one attractor accelerating mastery of others — is an open architectural question. The v0.12 result on the threat side is suggestive that the principle could work on the mastery side, but the present batch does not test it. A subsequent iteration extending signature-matching to the mastery layer would close the symmetry across the two persistent layers.

The third is the question this paper closes provisionally and the v0.13 iteration is reserved to address: end-state target activation. The architecture as it now stands operates under an externally-imposed three-phase developmental schedule, with phase boundaries at deterministic completion of the prescribed Phase 1 path and at 60 per cent of total run length. The schedule is a design choice, not a developmental finding. Whether the architecture can determine its own phase boundaries — terminating Phase 2 when its own evidence indicates that integration is complete, terminating Phase 3 when its own evidence indicates that autonomy has stabilised — is the question v0.13 is designed to test. The v0.11.2 convergence finding (that mean attractors mastered approaches 6 of 6 at 160,000 steps with a clear distributional shape) provides the mastery-side evidence base that v0.13 will need; v0.12's signature-matching mechanism provides a candidate threat-side analogue (when has the architecture learned what it needs to learn about hazards?). v0.13 will need to specify what end-state activation means in this architecture, what evidence the architecture would consult to determine end-state, and what behavioural change would follow from end-state being declared. The present paper marks v0.13 as the natural next iteration in the architectural arc and leaves the design conversation for the v0.13 pre-registration.

### 5.6 The architectural arc of the threat-layer programme

The v0.9, v0.10, and v0.12 papers together trace a four-step architectural arc on the threat side of the architecture. v0.9 identified that the v0.8 architecture's hazard handling combined two distinct elements (environmental wall and pre-wired aversion bias) and decomposed them into their constituent parts. The decomposition revealed that cost-based learning alone produced bounded knowledge that could not resist erosion by unbounded preference accumulation at extended horizons. v0.10 supplied persistent representation as the architectural response to this problem, and demonstrated full stabilisation at extended run lengths with one boundary condition characterised honestly. v0.12 supplied category-level generalisation as the architectural response to the boundary condition, and demonstrated full elimination of the cleanness gap with the cost-economics finding emerging as an unanticipated secondary result.

What remains, on the threat side, is the question of whether the architecture's threat-handling can support the kind of end-state determination that closes a developmental schedule. This question is the v0.13 question, framed in the threat-side terms it would take in this architecture. The answer is presumably structural: the threat layer holds categorical knowledge that the agent has, by definition, been gated against re-encountering. End-state on the threat side might be expressible as the rate at which new hazards are being discovered: when discovery rate falls below a threshold (or, more strongly, when discovery has not occurred for some pre-specified period), the architecture has presumably learned what it needs to learn about hazards in the current environment. v0.13 will need to specify whether and how this kind of meta-learning signal is operationalised; the v0.12 paper notes only that the question is now well-formed in a way it was not before signature-matching was demonstrated.

## 6. Conclusion

The v0.12 iteration tested whether category-level generalisation in the threat layer, implemented as signature-matching with first-entry flagging on cell-type signature, would eliminate the boundary condition characterised in the v0.10 paper. Across a pre-registered batch of 180 runs spanning six cost levels and three run lengths, with seeds matched to the v0.11.2 batch (Baker, 2026g), the iteration produces the pre-registered Category A outcome on all three checks. Phase 3 cleanness at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps outlier condition reaches 10 of 10. Phase 3 cleanness at 160,000 steps reaches 60 of 60 across all six cost levels. The v0.11.2 mastery and individuation signatures are preserved at the resolution the metrics measure. The signature-matching mechanism behaves exactly as the pre-registration predicts: across 162 runs in which any hazard was flagged, the strict invariant *first-entry conversions equals hazards-flagged minus one* holds in 162 of 162 cases.

Two findings depart from the pre-registration's anticipated narrative. First, the v0.10 outlier was already absent in v0.11.2 before v0.12 was applied; v0.11.2 produces 10 of 10 at the matched-seed outlier condition while v0.10 produces 8 of 10. The four mastery interventions developed across the v0.11 series suppress the threat-side outlier as a side-effect of redirecting Phase 2 attention. v0.12 therefore does not eliminate a problem v0.11.2 had; v0.12 provides a deliberate architectural mechanism for an outcome v0.11.2 had achieved incidentally. Second, the cost economics of v0.12 against v0.11.2 invert the pre-registration's predicted run-length trend. The cost ratio tightens with run length rather than loosening, because v0.11.2 continues to discover and pay three-entry cost on novel hazards across the full run length while v0.12 pays one entry's cost per signature-matched discovery. At long horizons, where the v0.10 paper reported a mean of 2.22 of 5 hazards flagged per run by 160,000 steps, the difference compounds substantially.

The architectural claim the present paper makes is bounded by the pre-registered Category F honesty constraint. The cell-type-only signature is information the architecture already represents; the v0.12 result is consistent both with the reading that category-level generalisation works in this architecture and with the deflationary reading that existing type representation was sufficient once the rule allowed it to act. The v0.12 batch alone cannot adjudicate. v0.14 and later iterations testing richer signatures (perceptual features, adjacency patterns, learned categorical relationships) are the necessary follow-on for distinguishing the readings.

The methodological discipline that has held throughout the programme held during this iteration. The pre-registration was committed to the public repository before any v0.12 code was written. The implementation completed in a single pass without amendment. A bug was identified in the meta-report's textual conclusion after the first batch had completed; the runner was patched, the batch was re-run with matched seeds drawn from the v0.11.2 CSV, and the re-run produced byte-identical CSV output, verifying the architecture's determinism on this environment. The bug fix was recorded as a textual correction rather than as a v0.12.1 amendment, on the methodological reasoning that amendment status in this programme has been reserved for architectural changes. The iteration's amendment budget remains untouched.

The threat-layer programme has now moved through four iterations: v0.9 identified the unbounded-accumulation problem; v0.10 supplied persistent representation as the architectural response; v0.11 closed the equivalent gap on the mastery side; v0.12 supplied category-level generalisation. The natural next iteration on the architectural arc — end-state target activation, framed by the v0.11.2 convergence finding and the v0.12 signature-matching mechanism — is reserved for v0.13 (Baker, in preparation).
