# v0.10 Pre-Registration: Experience-Driven Persistent Threat Representation

**Author:** Nicholas P M Baker, Synapstak Ltd
**Date:** 23 April 2026
**Status:** Pre-registration, committed before any v0.10 code is written
**Preceding work:** v0.9 (Baker, 2026c), v0.8 (Baker, 2026a)

---

## 1. Purpose

v0.9 (Baker, 2026c) demonstrated that the v0.8/v0.9 architecture has no persistent storage mechanism for learned hazard knowledge. Cost-based avoidance in `cost_no_aversion` mode produced partial and transient stabilisation that did not hold at extended run lengths. Specifically, Phase 3 cleanness followed a non-monotonic trajectory across 20,000, 80,000, and 160,000 steps: 29/60 → 43/60 → 34/60. The interpretation offered in v0.9 was that bounded cost-based Q-value adjustments are overwhelmed by the unbounded preference accumulator at extended time horizons.

v0.10 tests whether the addition of an experience-driven persistent threat layer resolves this instability. The layer converts repeated hazard entries into categorical, non-decaying prohibitions that operate as hard gates on action selection, preserving learned hazard knowledge against the preference dynamics that erode it in v0.9.

## 2. Central Hypothesis

Adding an experience-driven persistent threat layer to the v0.9 architecture will stabilise Phase 3 hazard avoidance at extended run lengths. Specifically, v0.10 at 160,000 steps will produce Phase 3 cleanness counts substantially higher than v0.9 at 160,000 steps, and the non-monotonic degradation observed in v0.9 will be absent in v0.10.

## 3. Secondary Hypothesis

The broader developmental signatures established in v0.8 and held in v0.9 — Phase 3 concentration, attractor ratio, mastery count, biographical individuation — will be preserved in v0.10. The addition of the threat layer should not disrupt developmental behaviours; it should operate as an architecturally distinct system that handles hazard knowledge without interfering with preference-and-value dynamics.

## 4. Architectural Specification

v0.10 inherits v0.9 unchanged except for the addition of a persistent threat layer and its associated hazard-entry counter.

### 4.1 Base configuration

The v0.10 architecture operates in the equivalent of v0.9's `cost_no_aversion` mode:

- FRAME cells: impassable, pre-wired aversion retained, epsilon filter active for frame-directed actions.
- HAZARD cells: passable at scalar cost, no pre-wired aversion, epsilon filter NOT active for hazard-directed actions.
- ATTRACTOR cells: passable, feature reward on entry (unchanged from v0.8/v0.9).
- NEUTRAL cells: passable, no reward of their own (unchanged from v0.8/v0.9).

All other architectural elements — three-phase developmental schedule, drive composition at each phase, learning rate 0.1, discount factor 0.9, epsilon 0.1, Q-value reset multipliers at phase transitions, preference accumulation, forward model, prediction error windows — are retained from v0.9 without modification.

### 4.2 Persistent threat layer

A new architectural component is added: a per-cell threat flag, binary-valued, stored as a structure indexed by cell coordinates. At agent creation:

- All FRAME cells are pre-flagged (threat_flag = 1).
- All HAZARD, ATTRACTOR, and NEUTRAL cells are unflagged (threat_flag = 0).

### 4.3 Hazard-entry counter

A per-cell entry counter is maintained, stored as a structure indexed by cell coordinates. Initialised to zero for all cells at agent creation. The counter increments by 1 each time the agent enters a HAZARD cell, across all phases (Phase 1, Phase 2, and Phase 3). The counter is active throughout the agent's life.

### 4.4 Flag conversion rule

When the hazard-entry counter for a HAZARD cell reaches 3, that cell's threat flag is set to 1. The conversion is one-way; flags do not clear.

The threshold of 3 is pre-registered as a design decision and will not be varied within this experiment. Variation of the threshold is a separate research question reserved for later work.

### 4.5 Action-selection rule

During action selection, for each candidate action, the destination cell is checked against the threat layer. If the destination cell's threat_flag = 1, the action is excluded from the action selection pipeline entirely — it does not enter Q-value computation, primitive-bias computation, or epsilon-greedy random selection. This constitutes a hard gate.

The hard gate operates in addition to existing v0.8/v0.9 mechanisms:

- FRAME cells are hard-gated from agent creation (consistent with their pre-flagged status).
- HAZARD cells become hard-gated only after 3 entries have converted their flag to 1.
- Before flag conversion, HAZARD cells remain passable at cost (v0.9 `cost_no_aversion` behaviour).

### 4.6 What v0.10 does not change

- Preference accumulation: unchanged. Continues to accumulate learning-progress reward and feature reward per cell.
- Q-value updates: unchanged. Continues standard TD learning with the v0.9 learning parameters.
- Phase transitions and Q-value reset multipliers: unchanged.
- Drive composition at each phase: unchanged.
- The cost mechanism on hazard entry: unchanged from v0.9's `cost_no_aversion` mode. The agent still pays the configured cost each time it enters an unflagged HAZARD cell.

### 4.7 Per-cell learning, no category-level generalisation

The threat layer operates per-cell. There is no mechanism for category-level generalisation: the agent learns each hazard cell independently, requiring N=3 entries per cell for conversion. Conversion of one HAZARD cell's flag to 1 has no effect on the counters or flags of other HAZARD cells. The agent does not acquire a concept of "hazard as a category" from experience with specific hazards; each hazard cell is a separate learning instance.

Variation in time-to-final-flag across runs is therefore expected and reflects the agent's specific exploration trajectory rather than inconsistent learning. Some agents will encounter all five hazard cells early in Phase 2 and reach the stable threat landscape quickly; others will encounter only some hazards during Phase 2 and continue to learn during Phase 3.

Category-level generalisation — a mechanism by which perceptual or positional features of experienced hazards propagate flagging to similar cells — is a separate architectural question reserved for later work. The absence of such a mechanism in v0.10 is a deliberate design choice consistent with the single-variable-change discipline: v0.10 tests persistent representation, not generalisation.

## 5. Experimental Design

### 5.1 Matched comparison

v0.10 is compared against v0.9 at matched conditions. To ensure clean comparison, v0.9 runs are re-executed at matched random seeds rather than comparing against previously-published v0.9 data. This produces within-seed behavioural comparison up to the point at which the threat layer begins operating.

### 5.2 Experimental matrix

| Variable | Values |
|----------|--------|
| Architecture | v0.9 (as published), v0.10 (threat layer added) |
| Cost level | 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0 |
| Run length (steps) | 20,000, 80,000, 160,000 |
| Runs per cost level | 10 |

Total runs: 2 architectures × 6 cost levels × 3 run lengths × 10 runs = 360 runs.

### 5.3 Computational estimate

Based on v0.9's observed compute (approximately three minutes for 140 runs at 20,000 steps, four minutes for 70 runs at 160,000 steps), the full v0.10 matrix is estimated at 25–40 minutes of total compute on a standard personal laptop. The addition of the threat-layer check per action-selection step is expected to add negligible compute overhead because the check is a single indexed lookup.

## 6. Metrics

### 6.1 Metrics retained from v0.8 and v0.9

- Hazard entries by phase (Phase 1, Phase 2, Phase 3, total)
- Phase 3 concentration (max cell visits in Phase 3 ÷ uniform expectation)
- Phase 3 attractor ratio (proportion of Phase 3 visits on attractor cells ÷ chance expectation)
- Mastery count (state-action pairs meeting the pre-registered mastery criterion)
- Top-5 preferences on attractors (count of attractor cells in top-5 preference ranking)
- Top-5 preferences near attractors (count of cells within Manhattan distance 1 of any attractor)
- Individuation (count of distinct primary-attractor choices across the batch)
- Rule violations by total-entries metric (≥3 hazard entries across the run)
- Rule violations by Phase-3 metric (≥3 hazard entries during Phase 3)

### 6.2 New metrics specific to v0.10

- **Number of cells flagged per run.** Count of HAZARD cells whose threat flag was converted to 1 during the run.
- **Time-to-first-flag.** Step number at which the first HAZARD cell's flag was converted. Measures how quickly the threat layer begins operating.
- **Time-to-final-flag.** Step number at which the last HAZARD cell to be flagged in the run had its flag converted. After this point, the agent operates in a stable threat landscape.
- **Total cost paid before stabilisation.** Cumulative cost incurred from hazard entries up to the time-to-final-flag. The "price of learning" for experience-driven conversion.
- **Phase 3 cleanness at 160,000 steps (primary measure).** Count of runs in which Phase 3 hazard entries fell below the pre-registered threshold (< 3). Directly compared against v0.9's 160,000-step result (34/60).

### 6.3 Expected reporting format

Per-cost-level central tendencies and distribution summaries in prose, following v0.8 and v0.9 convention. Per-run numerical data preserved in CSV files in the repository for post-hoc analysis by any interested researcher.

## 7. Pre-Registered Interpretation Categories

Category assignment is based on the primary measure: Phase 3 cleanness at 160,000 steps in v0.10, compared against v0.9's 34/60 baseline.

### Category A — Full stabilisation

v0.10 at 160,000 steps produces Phase 3 cleanness ≥ 55 of 60 runs across all cost levels. The threat layer resolves the v0.9 instability. Strong support for the central hypothesis. The architectural claim of v0.10 is validated; experience-driven persistent representation is a sufficient mechanism for long-horizon stability of learned hazard knowledge.

### Category B — Partial stabilisation

v0.10 at 160,000 steps produces Phase 3 cleanness between 40 and 54 of 60 runs. The threat layer produces meaningful improvement over v0.9 but does not fully resolve the long-horizon instability. Secondary analysis would examine whether the improvement correlates with cost level, with time-to-final-flag, or with other measured properties. The result would motivate v0.11 investigation of the specific mechanism of residual instability.

### Category C — No meaningful stabilisation

v0.10 at 160,000 steps produces Phase 3 cleanness ≤ 39 of 60 runs — i.e., no meaningful improvement over v0.9's 34/60. The threat layer as specified does not resolve the v0.9 instability. The finding would require substantial revision of the architectural interpretation offered in v0.9 and would motivate investigation of alternative mechanisms (different flag-conversion rules, different action-selection integration, different representational structure).

### Category D — Over-flagging

v0.10 flags substantially more cells than expected, with non-hazard cells being flagged or with all hazard cells being flagged within the first 1,000 steps of Phase 2. This would indicate a bug in the implementation or a design flaw in the trigger rule. Requires inspection and revision before interpretation of behavioural findings.

### Category E — Developmental degradation

The secondary developmental signatures (concentration, attractor ratio, mastery, individuation) are substantially reduced in v0.10 compared with v0.9, even if Phase 3 cleanness is improved. Would indicate that the threat layer, while stabilising hazard avoidance, disrupts other developmental behaviours. Motivates re-examination of whether the threat layer is architecturally too aggressive and should be softened (e.g., by changing the hard gate to a strong veto, or by adjusting the flag-conversion threshold).

## 8. Commitments

Three methodological commitments accompany this pre-registration:

First, no v0.10 code will be written before this pre-registration document is committed to the public repository. The timestamp of the commit establishes the priority of the design decisions.

Second, the experimental matrix and metric definitions will not be revised during or after the batch execution. Any revision necessitated by implementation-time discovery will be handled through an explicit amendment document, committed before the amended computation is run, matching the v0.9 amendment discipline.

Third, the findings will be reported as they occur, not reshaped to fit the interpretation categories. If the result falls between categories (as v0.9's 80,000-step result did), the gap will be reported explicitly and the decision tree's ambiguity will be part of the finding.

## 9. Relationship to v0.11

v0.10 establishes the architectural principle that repeated experience can be converted into categorical persistent knowledge through an N-threshold counter-and-flag mechanism. This principle is intended to generalise to v0.11, where the same architectural pattern will be applied to attractor mastery: repeated attractor visits will deplete attractor reward, and once an attractor is exhausted its signature will be banked in a persistent mastery layer. The threshold mechanism, the persistence property, and the architecturally-distinct storage system are all intended to carry forward.

v0.10 is a complete experiment in its own right — its findings do not depend on v0.11 being successful. But the design choices committed here are made with awareness of how they position the architecture for v0.11's extension.
