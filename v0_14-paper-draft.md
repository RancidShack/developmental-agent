# Competency-Gated Content Transformation in a Small Artificial Learner: Hazard Cells Becoming Knowledge Cells at Mastery Thresholds

**Author:** Nicholas P M Baker, Synapstak Ltd
**Date:** 27 April 2026
**Status:** Working draft — Sections 1 through 9

---

## Abstract

A preceding paper (Baker, 2026l, in preparation) established that the architecture's inherited Phase 3 dynamics handle late-appearing feature-reward sources at random locations, with conditional discovery rate of 93.9% at 160,000 steps among activated runs. The same paper reported a second cross-layer behavioural effect — the activated end-state cell altering threat-layer encounter rates without modifying any threat-layer mechanism — and treated cross-layer effects as a phenomenon-class the programme could now name. v0.14 inherits this architecture and extends it with a competency-gated content transformation rule: each of the five hazard cells receives a competency threshold at run start (a permutation over {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}), and when the agent's competency — measured as the sum of inherited mastery flags — reaches a cell's threshold, that cell transitions in type from HAZARD to KNOWLEDGE. The threat flag persists across the transition. The action-selection hard-gate acquires a competency-bounded exception: the gate continues to exclude actions targeting flagged hazard cells but does not exclude actions targeting flagged knowledge cells. Post-transition, the cell carries feature reward and primitive attraction bias parallel to attractor and end-state cells; on three entries the cell banks, applying the four mastery interventions and clearing the persistent threat flag.

Across a pre-registered batch of 180 runs and a v0.14.1-amended replication batch of a further 180 runs at a different permutation offset, with seeds matched to the v0.13 batch (Baker, 2026l, in preparation), v0.14 produces six findings.

The first concerns operational success against the pre-registered Category A thresholds. Conditional first-entry-after-transition rate at 160,000 steps is 96.2% in the original batch and 96.6% in the replication, exceeding the 85% threshold. Three-entry banking rate at 160,000 steps reaches 59 of 60 in the original batch and equivalent figures in the replication, exceeding the 75% threshold; banking rate at 80,000 steps reaches 51 of 60, exceeding the 60% threshold. End-state activation, under the strictly-stricter v0.14 trigger (`all_attractors_mastered AND all_hazards_banked_as_knowledge`), reaches 53 of 60 at 160,000 steps in the original batch and 58 of 60 in the replication, exceeding the 30/60 threshold. Pre-first-transition behavioural preservation against v0.13 passes at 180 of 180 in both batches across all 17 audited fields. The pre-registered Category A check set passes in full; the v0.13 calibration mistake recorded in that paper's Section 5.4 informed the v0.14 thresholds, which were derived from inheritance distributions rather than absolute counts, and the discipline held.

The second finding is the surprise that operational success surfaced. The pre-registered H2 hypothesis predicted that competing feature-rewards from transitioned knowledge cells would *delay* mastery of the still-unmastered attractor cells: `time_to_final_mastery` would lengthen, `attractors_mastered` could decrease. **The data invert this prediction.** In the original batch, v0.14 records higher `attractors_mastered` than v0.13 in 49 of 180 matched-seed runs and lower in 29; the replication produces 47 versus 20. End-state activation, despite v0.14's amended trigger requiring strictly more milestones than v0.13's, fires more often under v0.14 in 39 runs of the original batch versus 21 under v0.13, and 35 versus 17 in the replication. The harder milestone is reached more often. The replication confirms the inversion is structural rather than a one-permutation artefact.

The third finding concerns a flagging artefact in naive cross-batch comparison. The meta-report's `hazards_flagged` field shows v0.13 higher than v0.14 in 154 of 180 runs in the original batch — superficially suggesting that v0.14 flags fewer hazards. The decomposition reveals the artefact: v0.14 banks knowledge cells, and at banking the persistent threat flag clears. A fully-banked v0.14 run records zero `hazards_flagged` and five `hazards_banked_as_knowledge`, while v0.13 at the same seed records five `hazards_flagged`. Both reflect "the agent flagged this cell at some point during the run." When the comparison is corrected — counting `flagged + banked_as_knowledge` as the proper measure of categorical hazard recognition — the direction reverses: v0.14 records more recognised hazards than v0.13 in 147 runs versus 6. The categorical recognition mechanism is not weakened; it is propagated more aggressively.

The fourth finding extends the cross-layer-effects observation the v0.13 paper introduced. v0.14 produces a third cross-layer effect, structurally distinct from the two prior observations: it bidirectionally couples the threat layer and the mastery layer through the competency-gated transition rule. The threat layer's hard-gate exception, opened by mastery-layer competency, alters the agent's action-selection trajectory in ways that accelerate further mastery. The activation-rate inversion is the cleanest demonstration of this coupling: the harder activation milestone fires more often precisely because the architectural extension that makes the milestone harder also opens cells the agent could not previously enter. The phenomenon-class observation the v0.13 paper named on two iterations is now confirmed across three.

The fifth finding emerged from analysis after the H2 inversion was identified and was not pre-registered as a hypothesis. v0.14's mastery sequences diverge from v0.13's at matched seeds in 146 of 180 runs (81%): the two architectures fork at the second attractor banking, the moment the first hazard cell unlocks. Knowledge banking order matches transition order in only 46 of 357 banking events (13%): the agent autonomously prioritises engagement, routinely overriding the order in which cells became architecturally available. The finding connects directly to the feature-aligned preference formation signature established in v0.8 (Baker, 2026a) and demonstrates that signature operating at scale through composed inherited capabilities.

The sixth finding is the per-cell mechanism finding. The original batch produced a striking spike at cell (5, 9) at threshold 1 (mean Δ(attractors_mastered) = +0.649 across 37 runs). The replication weakened this to +0.250 — the original spike was real but inflated by the particular permutation draw. Across 25 (cell × threshold) conditions, 20 persist or strengthen between batches, 3 reverse direction at small magnitudes, 2 weaken in the same direction. Only one cell (14, 13) shows a clean monotone-with-threshold pattern in the replication. The mechanism is broadly distributed across the five hazard cells rather than concentrated at a single bottleneck. The "obstacle removal at the bottleneck cell" reading from the original batch is a partial truth; the full mechanism operates more diffusely.

The pre-registered Category F honesty constraint applies. v0.14 contributes the cell-type transition rule, the action-selection hard-gate exception, the banking rule on knowledge cells, and the amended end-state trigger. Locating (inherited from v0.13's Phase 3 dynamics) and state-checking (the inherited mastery-flag dictionary) are not v0.14 contributions. The H2 inversion finding is consistent with two readings the v0.14 batches cannot distinguish: an obstacle-removal mechanistic reading in which the cell-type transition matters principally because the hard-gate exception removes a Phase 2 / Phase 3 routing constraint, and a developmental reading in which the agent's relationship to formerly-walled-off cells shifts in a way the inherited drives respond to. A v0.15 obstacle-removal-isolation variant — one in which the hard-gate exception fires at competency-threshold-reached without transitioning the cell type — is reserved for subsequent work and named in the discussion for that reason.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Programme to date

A small tabular developmental agent occupying a 20×20 structured environment has, across previous papers, demonstrated five behavioural signatures associated with developmental learning and progressively acquired the architectural machinery that supports those signatures across extended horizons. v0.8 established the signatures themselves: rule adherence, focused autonomy, feature-aligned preference formation, a specialist-generalist trade-off, and full biographical individuation (Baker, 2026a, 2026b). v0.9 decomposed the v0.8 hazard handling and identified an unbounded-accumulation problem at long horizons (Baker, 2026c). v0.10 closed the gap on the hazard side by introducing experience-driven persistent threat representation (Baker, 2026d). v0.11.2 closed it on the mastery side through three amendments that decomposed bounded mastery into the four code-path interventions it actually required (Baker, 2026e, 2026f, 2026g). v0.12 supplied category-level generalisation in the threat layer through cell-type signature-matching (Baker, 2026h, 2026i, in preparation), eliminating the v0.10 boundary condition while preserving v0.11.2's mastery and individuation findings. v0.13 demonstrated that the architecture's inherited Phase 3 dynamics handle late-appearing feature-reward sources at random locations and named cross-layer behavioural effects as a phenomenon-class across two iterations (Baker, 2026l, in preparation).

The arc the programme has traced through these six iterations has a recognisable shape. Each iteration has supplied one piece of categorical knowledge representation, one bounded resolution of an architectural gap, or one demonstration that inherited dynamics handle a new task class. The v0.13 close marked a point at which the architecture's two persistent knowledge layers — threat and mastery — both store categorical information, both bound the unbounded-accumulation problem v0.9 identified, the threat layer additionally generalises across hazard exemplars on first entry, and the locating machinery underneath these layers handles new feature-reward sources at random locations on the all-attractors-mastered signal.

What the architecture had not yet been asked to do, by the close of v0.13, was support cell-type transitions tied to the agent's developmental state. Hazard cells remained hazard cells; attractor cells, once banked, remained banked attractor cells; the end-state cell appeared at the moment of all-attractors-mastered and banked on first entry. The architecture's environment, in the period before v0.14, contained no cell whose type changed mid-run as a function of what the agent had become. The competency-gated content transformation question — whether a hazard cell could become a knowledge cell when the agent's competency was sufficient to engage it as such — opens at this point in the programme arc.

### 1.2 What v0.14 was designed to test

The v0.14 iteration was designed to test three propositions, only the first of which was framed in the pre-registration as the architectural commitment.

The first proposition is the operational claim. If hazard cells transition to knowledge cells at competency thresholds, the inherited locating machinery v0.13 demonstrated should engage with the post-transition knowledge cells as it engages with attractor and end-state cells. The transition introduces a cell whose perceptual properties change mid-run, which is the harder version of the v0.13 task: v0.13 introduced a cell at a random location whose perceptual properties became visible at activation; v0.14 introduces five cells at fixed locations whose perceptual properties change at five distinct moments tied to the agent's accumulating competency. The operational claim, encoded in the pre-registered Category A thresholds, is that the architecture handles this harder task at high reliability.

The second proposition was the H2 hypothesis. The pre-registration's Section 4 named a mechanistic prediction the architectural decomposition implied: transitioned knowledge cells, carrying feature reward and primitive attraction bias parallel to attractor cells, would compete for the agent's attention with not-yet-mastered attractors. The competition would lengthen `time_to_final_mastery` in some conditions and could in extreme cases reduce `attractors_mastered`. The prediction followed from the design's own logic: more feature-rewarding cells available at a given moment means more candidates for the agent's attention, and finite Phase 2 / Phase 3 budget means some candidates will be reached and some will not. The H2 hypothesis was the architectural decomposition's reading of its own behavioural consequence.

The third proposition was the methodological discipline test. The v0.13 calibration mistake (recorded in that paper's Section 5.4) had taught that pre-registered thresholds against an iteration's operational success should be tied to inheritance distributions rather than to absolute counts. v0.14 was the first iteration to apply that lesson prospectively in the pre-registration: Category A thresholds were derived from the v0.13 batch's distributions on the relevant metrics, with the transition-and-banking thresholds calibrated against the maximum number of cells the architecture could plausibly handle in a finite run length. The discipline question was whether this calibration approach would produce a clean Category A verdict.

### 1.3 Architectural commitments

The pre-registration committed before any v0.14 code was written (Baker, 2026m). Three architectural commitments shape what v0.14 tests and bound what it can claim.

The first is the trigger. The cell-type transition fires at competency-threshold-reached, where competency is read directly from the inherited mastery-flag dictionary. v0.14 does not introduce a competency representation; it state-checks the mastery layer that v0.11.2 / v0.12 already maintains. The choice was made on Category F grounds: introducing a new competency representation would add an architectural mechanism whose contribution to behaviour would need to be separately characterised. State-checking the inherited dictionary leaves the contribution v0.14 makes localised to the transition rule, the action-selection exception, and the banking rule.

The second is the persistent threat flag's behaviour across the transition. When a hazard cell transitions to a knowledge cell, the threat flag set during the agent's prior encounter does not clear. The cell remains flagged. The architectural reasoning, settled in design conversation: the agent's categorical recognition of the cell as hazardous is information the architecture has earned and should not lose at the moment of transition. The threat flag clears only at knowledge banking — the moment the agent's three-entry engagement with the post-transition cell completes the bounded mastery analogue. This commitment turns out to matter for Finding 3 below, where naive aggregate-metric comparison treats v0.14's flag-cleared-at-banking behaviour as if it were flag-not-set behaviour.

The third is the action-selection hard-gate exception. The hard-gate rule v0.10 introduced — that any action targeting a flagged cell is excluded from the action-selection pipeline before Q-value computation, primitive bias computation, or epsilon-greedy random selection — continues to exclude actions targeting flagged hazard cells. It does not exclude actions targeting flagged knowledge cells. The design reasoning: the gate's purpose is to prevent the agent from re-encountering cells whose costs the agent has learned to avoid. A knowledge cell, post-transition, is no longer the same cell perceptually; the architecture has reason to allow the agent to engage with it. The persistent threat flag remains as the architecture's record of what the cell was; the gate's decision to honour or override that record is now competency-bounded.

The fourth commitment, implicit in the prior three but worth naming, is the amendment to v0.13's end-state activation trigger. v0.13's trigger fired on `all_attractors_mastered`. v0.14's trigger fires on `all_attractors_mastered AND all_hazards_banked_as_knowledge`. The amended trigger is a strict superset of v0.13's: any run reaching v0.14's milestone has, by definition, reached v0.13's. The amendment was committed to ensure the end-state mechanism inherited from v0.13 fires only when the v0.14 architecture's full behavioural envelope has been traversed. The activation-rate consequence of the amendment is the carrier of Finding 1's surprise: a strict superset milestone fires more often in matched-seed comparison.

### 1.4 Findings and their relation to the pre-registration

The paper reports six findings.

The first is the operational-success finding. The pre-registered Category A check set passes in full across both batches: conditional first-entry-after-transition at 160,000 steps reaches 96.2% (original) and 96.6% (replication), exceeding the 85% threshold; three-entry banking at 160,000 steps reaches 59 of 60 in both batches, exceeding 75%; banking at 80,000 steps reaches 51 of 60, exceeding 60%; end-state activation under the strictly-stricter v0.14 trigger reaches 53 and 58 of 60 across the two batches at 160,000 steps, exceeding the 30/60 floor; pre-first-transition behavioural preservation against v0.13 passes at 180 of 180 in both batches. The architectural mechanism the iteration was designed to test does what the iteration was designed to test it could do, at the inheritance-distribution-bounded thresholds the pre-registration specified. The v0.13 calibration discipline held.

The second is the H2 inversion. The hypothesis the architectural decomposition predicted — that competing feature-rewards would delay mastery — is contradicted by the data. v0.14 records higher `attractors_mastered` than v0.13 in matched-seed comparison more often than the reverse, and end-state activation under the strictly-harder milestone fires more often than under v0.13's lighter milestone. The replication confirms the inversion is structural. Section 4 characterises the inversion's behavioural mechanism; Section 5 considers the two readings (mechanistic obstacle-removal versus developmental shift) it remains consistent with.

The third is the flagging-artefact finding. The naive cross-batch comparison on `hazards_flagged` invites the reading that v0.14 flags fewer hazards than v0.13. The decomposition reveals the artefact: v0.14 clears the threat flag at knowledge banking, so banking events appear in the data as flag-not-set. When the comparison is corrected — counting flagged + banked-as-knowledge as the categorical recognition measure — the direction reverses. The corrected reading is recorded here for the methodological record, alongside Finding 6 (per-cell mechanism more diffuse than the single-bottleneck reading suggests), as a reminder that aggregate metrics that span an architectural change moment require decomposition before interpretation.

The fourth is the cross-layer-effects extension. The v0.13 paper named cross-layer behavioural effects as a phenomenon-class across two iterations: v0.11.2's mastery interventions affected threat-side cleanness through post-mastery exploration extension; v0.13's end-state activation affected threat-side encounter rates through post-activation attentional redirection. v0.14 produces the third observation: the competency-gated transition rule bidirectionally couples the threat layer and the mastery layer through the action-selection hard-gate exception, with the activation-rate inversion as the cleanest demonstration. Three observations across three iterations confirms the phenomenon-class as a programme-level pattern; Section 5 returns to the matched-seed comparison apparatus that surfaces these effects and notes the v1.0 integration paper's responsibility to characterise them across the full inheritance chain.

The fifth is the autonomy-signature finding. The finding emerged from analysis after the H2 inversion was identified. Mastery sequences diverge from v0.13's in 81% of matched-seed runs; knowledge banking order matches transition order in only 13% of banking events. The agent autonomously prioritises engagement, routinely overriding the order in which cells became architecturally available. The finding connects directly to the v0.8 feature-aligned preference formation signature, demonstrating it operating at scale through composed inherited capabilities. Category F applies: the autonomy reading is consistent with both autonomous prioritisation operating on inherited drive composition and a deflationary "the drive composition just plays out the architecture's specification." The autonomy reading is empirically stronger because the trajectories visibly diverge from v0.13 in 81% of runs at matched seeds — the agent is doing something architecturally distinct, not running the same trajectory through more cell-type transitions — but the deflationary reading cannot be ruled out from the v0.14 batches alone.

The sixth is the per-cell mechanism finding. The original batch's striking spike at cell (5, 9) at threshold 1 weakens substantially under the v0.14.1 replication's permutation_offset = 1. The full per-(cell × threshold) comparison shows persistence in 20 of 25 conditions, reversal in 3 small-magnitude conditions, weakening in the same direction in 2. Only (14, 13) shows monotone-with-threshold response in the replication. The single-bottleneck reading the original batch invited is partial; the mechanism operates across all five cells under both permutation draws but is not concentrated at one location.

### 1.5 The Category F honesty constraint

The pre-registration's Category F names the v0.14-specific honesty constraint and bounds the architectural claim regardless of the operational result.

What v0.14 contributes is the cell-type transition rule, the action-selection hard-gate exception, the banking rule on knowledge cells, and the amended end-state trigger. Locating — the agent's finding of the post-transition cell — is performed by the inherited Phase 3 dynamics v0.13 demonstrated on the simpler version of the task. State-checking — the architecture's awareness that competency has reached a cell's threshold — is performed by reading the inherited mastery-flag dictionary. The architectural extension v0.14 makes is the rule that combines these inherited capabilities at a transition moment.

The constraint applies most pointedly to Finding 1 (the H2 inversion). The inversion is a substantive empirical finding regardless of which architectural reading explains it. Two readings are consistent with the v0.14 batches: an obstacle-removal mechanistic reading in which the hard-gate exception removes a Phase 2 / Phase 3 routing constraint that previously diverted the agent from cells beyond the hazard clusters, and a developmental reading in which the agent's relationship to the formerly-walled-off cells shifts at the transition moment in a way the inherited drives respond to. The readings make different predictions for an architectural variant in which the hard-gate exception fires at competency-threshold-reached without transitioning the cell type. The variant is reserved for subsequent work and is mentioned in Section 5 for the methodological record without being committed in the present paper.

### 1.6 Connection to the broader programme

v0.14 occupies a specific position in the architectural arc. v0.13 demonstrated that inherited Phase 3 dynamics handle late-appearing targets at random locations through machinery the architecture already had; v0.14 demonstrates that the same machinery handles cells whose perceptual properties change mid-run, where the change is gated by the agent's developmental state. The integration paper (v1.0) reserved at the close of the v0.13 paper has now to audit a longer inheritance chain — v0.10 through v0.14 — and a richer set of cross-layer behavioural effects, three of which the threat-layer programme has now documented. The methodological tooling for the integration audit is the matched-seed comparison apparatus the programme has used since v0.11.2, supplemented by the temporal-scoping discipline v0.13.1 introduced and the v0.14 prospective-calibration discipline that produced the present paper's Category A passes.

The v0.14 single-pass-without-amendment outcome — implementation completed once the pre-registration was committed, with one operational amendment (v0.14.1) scoped to add the replication apparatus rather than modify the architecture — is the empirical answer to a methodological question the v0.13 pre-registration had raised about implementation surfaces larger than v0.12's. The v0.14 implementation surface is substantially larger than v0.13's: a new cell type, a competency-threshold mechanism, an action-selection exception, a banking rule on a previously hazardous cell, and an amended end-state trigger. The single-pass outcome holds despite the larger surface; the discipline recorded in v0.12's and v0.13's methodological notes carries forward.

What the architecture as it now stands does not have, and what subsequent iterations are designed to address, includes: an architectural variant distinguishing the obstacle-removal and developmental readings of the H2 inversion, briefly mentioned in Section 5; the mastery-side equivalent of v0.12's category-level generalisation, identified at the close of the v0.12 paper and still reserved; richer locating mechanisms for distinguishing inherited-machinery-suffices from a v0.13-specific or v0.14-specific locating contribution, reserved at the close of v0.13 and still reserved; and the v1.0 integration paper that audits the cumulative inheritance chain.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Environment

The environment is identical to that used in v0.8 through v0.13 (Baker, 2026a, 2026d, 2026g, 2026i, in preparation, 2026l, in preparation). A 20×20 grid contains four cell types in the v0.13 specification: frame cells form the boundary and are impassable; hazard cells are clustered in two groups, a three-cell cluster at coordinates (5, 8), (5, 9), (6, 8) and a two-cell cluster at (14, 13), (14, 14), passable at scalar cost; attractor cells are six fixed locations at coordinates (3, 3), (16, 3), (9, 10), (4, 15), (15, 16), and (11, 5); neutral cells fill the remainder of the passable interior. v0.13 added the END_STATE cell type, sampled at random from the passable non-attractor scope at run start and remaining perceptually neutral until activation. The agent starts each run at coordinate (1, 1) and operates under the inherited three-phase developmental schedule: prescribed acquisition (Phase 1), drive-based integration (Phase 2), and preference-weighted autonomy (Phase 3), with phase transitions at the deterministic completion of the boustrophedon path and at 60 per cent of total run length respectively.

v0.14 extends the cell type system with a fifth type, KNOWLEDGE, and modifies the environment construction to assign a competency threshold to each of the five hazard cells at run start. The environment's other elements are unchanged from v0.13.

### 2.2 v0.14 architectural specification

The v0.14 architecture inherits v0.13 unchanged except for the addition of one new cell type, one threshold mechanism on hazard cells, one cell-type transition rule, one action-selection hard-gate exception, one banking rule, and one amendment to v0.13's end-state activation trigger. The architectural change is contained at the research-question level — the architecture acquires competency-gated content transformation as a single capability — though the implementation surface touches the world, the agent's perception, the action-selection pipeline, the banking machinery, and the end-state trigger.

**The new cell type.** The constant `KNOWLEDGE = 5` is added to the cell-type constants alongside `FRAME`, `NEUTRAL`, `HAZARD`, `ATTRACTOR`, `END_STATE`. Cells of type KNOWLEDGE are passable, carry feature reward `FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT = 0.15` per visit pre-banking and zero post-banking, and carry primitive attraction bias `ATTRACTION_BONUS = 0.3` for adjacent cells pre-banking and zero post-banking. The reward and bias parameters match the attractor cell parameters and the v0.13 end-state cell parameters exactly. The architectural reasoning, settled in pre-registration: the test is whether the inherited locating machinery re-engages on a cell whose perceptual properties have changed mid-run, and tuning the post-transition cell's signal stronger or weaker would compromise the test.

**Threshold assignment.** At run start, the environment assigns a competency threshold from the set {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} to each of the five hazard cells. The assignment is a permutation over the threshold set, sampled at world construction. Sampling uses the agent's NumPy random state with state-save-and-restore around the draw, parallel to v0.13's `_sample_end_state_cell` implementation. The reasoning is methodological: the pre-registration committed to byte-identical pre-first-transition behaviour against v0.13 at matched seeds, and v0.13 makes no `np.random` calls during world or agent construction beyond the end-state cell sampling. Naive sampling for the threshold permutation would consume RNG draws v0.13 does not, shifting the agent's RNG state at first action selection and breaking the matched-seed guarantee. The implementation saves the state, performs the permutation draw, and restores the state. The permutation is deterministic given the run's seed and the permutation offset (zero in the original batch, one in the v0.14.1 replication batch).

**The cell-type transition.** After every step, the agent checks whether competency — `sum(self.mastery_flag.values())` — has reached or exceeded any unmastered hazard cell's assigned threshold. The check is performed in the agent's main loop, after `update_model` and `update_values` have completed for the current step. When the check first becomes true for a given cell, the environment's `cell_type` dictionary is updated for that cell's coordinate: `self.cell_type[c] = KNOWLEDGE`. The agent records the step at which the transition occurred (`self.transition_step[c] = step`). Subsequent checks on the same cell do not re-fire. Multiple cells can transition at the same step if competency simultaneously reaches multiple thresholds, though in the present batches this circumstance does not arise.

**Persistent threat flag.** The threat flag set during the agent's prior encounter with the (then-hazard) cell does not clear at transition. From the next action selection onward, the agent's `perceive_adjacent` returns KNOWLEDGE for the cell's coordinate, and the action-selection pipeline computes feature reward and primitive attraction bias for the cell as it does for attractor cells — but the threat-flag tracking state continues to record the cell as flagged. The flag clears only at knowledge banking, described below.

**Action-selection hard-gate exception.** The v0.10 hard-gate rule continues to exclude actions targeting flagged hazard cells. v0.14 adds an exception: actions targeting flagged knowledge cells are not excluded. The implementation: the gate predicate, previously `threat_flag[c] == 1`, becomes `threat_flag[c] == 1 AND cell_type[c] == HAZARD`. Knowledge cells, post-transition, pass the gate. The pre-wired aversion bias of −5.0 for frame-adjacent actions is unchanged.

**The banking rule.** Knowledge cells bank after `KNOWLEDGE_THRESHOLD = 3` entries, parallel to attractor cells' `MASTERY_THRESHOLD = 3`. The choice of three-entry banking — rather than v0.13's one-entry end-state banking — was pre-registered on the architectural grounds that knowledge cells, like attractors, carry richer engagement than the v0.13 end-state cell whose research question concerned only locating. When an agent enters a knowledge cell for the third time post-transition, the architecture records the step (`self.knowledge_banked_step[c] = step`), increments the knowledge banking counter, and applies the four mastery interventions parallel to v0.11.2's treatment of mastered attractors: feature reward depletes to zero, primitive attraction bias clears, cell preference resets to zero at this step, and ongoing preference accumulation on the cell is blocked. **Additionally, the persistent threat flag clears.** The cell becomes a banked knowledge cell — passable at no cost, no longer a target for the locating machinery, no longer flagged.

**Amended end-state activation trigger.** v0.13's end-state activation trigger fired on `all_attractors_mastered`. v0.14's trigger fires on `all_attractors_mastered AND all_hazards_banked_as_knowledge`. The amended trigger is a strict superset of v0.13's: any run reaching v0.14's trigger has reached v0.13's. The amendment was committed in the pre-registration to ensure the end-state mechanism inherited from v0.13 fires only when the v0.14 architecture's full behavioural envelope has been traversed.

**Implementation surface.** The architectural change is contained to one research question — does the architecture support content transformation gated by accumulating competency — but the implementation surface is larger than v0.13's was. v0.14 modifies: the cell-type constant set; `StructuredGridWorld.__init__` to permute thresholds across hazard cells; new `StructuredGridWorld.transition_to_knowledge` and `bank_knowledge` methods; the path-planning predicate to recognise KNOWLEDGE as passable; the agent's `__init__` to record transition steps, knowledge banking steps, knowledge visit counts, and banked flags per knowledge cell; the agent's `_primitive_bias` method to give ATTRACTION_BONUS to adjacent unbanked knowledge cells; the agent's `feature_reward` method to deliver FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT pre-banking and zero post-banking on knowledge cells; the agent's hard-gate predicate to admit the cell-type-bounded exception; the agent's `update_model` method to block preference accumulation on banked knowledge cells; the agent's `record_action_outcome` method to handle knowledge cell entry, banking on third post-transition entry, threat-flag clearing at banking, and the competency-threshold check after each step; the end-state activation trigger to require the strictly-stricter superset; the metric set to record the new fields; and the report rendering to display knowledge cell status.

### 2.3 What v0.14 does not change

All architectural elements from v0.13 are preserved unchanged. The threat layer operates as v0.12 specifies: the v0.10 three-entry conversion rule applies to the first hazard cell encountered, and the v0.12 signature-matching rule converts every subsequent hazard on first entry. The hard-gate action-selection rule applies to flagged hazard cells as before; the v0.14 exception extends the rule rather than replacing it. The mastery layer operates as v0.11.2 specifies: the four mastery interventions hold for attractor cells (zero feature reward post-mastery, cleared attraction bias for mastered cells, preference reset at banking, blocked preference accumulation post-banking). MASTERY_THRESHOLD = 3 and MASTERY_BONUS = 1.0 are inherited unchanged. The end-state cell mechanism operates as v0.13 specifies, except for the amended activation trigger; once activation fires, the cell-type transition, primitive bias, feature reward, and one-entry banking all proceed as v0.13. The three-phase developmental schedule, drive composition at each phase, learning rate 0.1, discount factor 0.9, and epsilon 0.1 are inherited unchanged. The pre-wired aversion bias of −5.0 for frame-adjacent actions is retained.

The single-variable-change discipline holds at the research-question level: v0.14 introduces competency-gated content transformation as the singular architectural extension. All prior architectural elements are preserved.

### 2.4 Experimental matrix and matched-seed comparison

The experimental matrix is one architecture (v0.14) crossed with six cost levels (0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0) crossed with three run lengths (20,000, 80,000, 160,000 steps) crossed with ten runs per cell, totalling 180 runs per batch. The pre-registered original batch uses permutation_offset = 0; the v0.14.1 amendment specifies the replication batch uses permutation_offset = 1, producing different threshold assignments while preserving the matched-seed comparison guarantee against v0.13.

For each (cost, run length, run index) triple, the random seed is matched to the v0.13 batch (Baker, 2026l, in preparation) at the same condition. Seeds are loaded from `run_data_v0_13.csv` rather than regenerated, on the technical reasoning named in v0.12's pre-registration Section 5 and inherited by v0.13 and v0.14. The seed chain therefore links v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, v0.13, and v0.14 at every (cost, run length, run index) cell: same seed, same agent RNG state at construction, comparable trajectories up to the divergence each architecture introduces.

The matched-seed comparison produces a within-seed comparison up to the point at which the first cell-type transition fires. v0.14's behaviour in the pre-first-transition period — including all attractor mastery dynamics that occur before the first hazard cell unlocks — is byte-identical to v0.13 at the same seed by construction. Divergence begins at the first transition step, at which point a cell's perceptual properties change and the agent's subsequent action selection differs from v0.13's.

### 2.5 The v0.14.1 replication apparatus

The v0.14.1 amendment was committed before any reanalysis of the original batch's H2 finding was performed. The amendment is operational, not architectural: no code change to the agent module was required. The agent module accepts a `permutation_offset` parameter that is added to the global RNG state before the threshold permutation draw and subtracted after, producing different permutations across batches while preserving the matched-seed guarantee at the agent-RNG level. With offset = 0 the agent's behaviour matches the original batch bit-for-bit; with offset = 1 the threshold permutation is rotated, producing a different (cell × threshold) assignment per run while the seed chain to v0.13 is preserved.

The replication batch's purpose was to test whether the original batch's striking H2 inversion was structural — present under any permutation draw — or artefactual — present only under the particular permutation the original batch happened to draw. The v0.14.1 amendment specified a single replication offset of 1 rather than a full re-randomisation, on the methodological reasoning that one alternative draw is sufficient to distinguish structural-versus-artefact at the inversion's headline-level. If the inversion persists at offset = 1, the structural reading is supported; if it weakens substantially or reverses, the artefact reading is supported. Both batches' results are reported in the present paper; the inversion persists, with the operational metrics in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 showing close agreement across the two batches.

### 2.6 Metrics

All v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, and v0.13 metrics are retained.

Eight metrics are introduced for v0.14. **Competency threshold** records the threshold assigned to each hazard cell at run start. **Transition step** records the step at which each hazard cell transitions to knowledge, or null if the run does not reach the cell's threshold. **Knowledge entries** counts entries to each knowledge cell post-transition. **Knowledge banked step** records the step at which each knowledge cell banks, or null if banking does not occur. **Knowledge banked flag** records whether each knowledge cell has been banked. **All five transitioned** records whether all five hazard cells have transitioned. **All five banked** records whether all five cells have banked as knowledge. **Hazards banked as knowledge** counts the cells that have banked.

The headline metric for v0.14's operational claim is the **conditional first-entry-after-transition rate**: the fraction of (transitioned cells) the agent enters at least once after the cell's transition. The diagnostic metric for the Category A check on banking is the **three-entry banking rate**: the fraction of (transitioned cells) that the agent enters three or more times post-transition. The end-state activation rate measures performance against the strictly-stricter v0.14 activation trigger.

For the cross-layer-effect analysis, two additional derived metrics are reported. **Categorical hazard recognition** is `flagged + banked_as_knowledge` per run, the corrected measure that accounts for v0.14's banking-clears-flag behaviour. **Mastery sequence** is the ordered tuple of attractor banking events per run, used in matched-seed comparison against v0.13.

Per-run numerical data is preserved in CSV format; aggregate findings are reported per cost level and per run length below.

### 2.7 Pre-registered interpretation categories

Six interpretation categories are pre-registered (Baker, 2026m, Section 7), with one operational amendment from v0.14.1 (Baker, 2026n) that adds the replication batch's reporting requirement.

**Category A** specifies successful competency-gated content transformation with full preservation, defined by five checks. **Check 1**: conditional first-entry-after-transition rate ≥ 85% at 160,000 steps. **Check 2**: three-entry banking rate ≥ 75% at 160,000 steps and ≥ 60% at 80,000 steps. **Check 3**: end-state activation rate ≥ 30 of 60 at 160,000 steps under the v0.14 amended trigger. **Check 4a**: pre-first-transition behavioural preservation against v0.13 passes byte-identical at 100% of audited runs. **Check 4b**: post-first-transition cross-layer effects characterised rather than treated as preservation failures. The check structure inherits the v0.13.1 temporal-scoping discipline applied prospectively.

**Categories B through E** follow the v0.10 / v0.12 / v0.13 pattern. Category B is partial transformation (some Category A checks passing but not all). Category C is mechanism failure (substantial below-threshold performance on Check 1 or Check 2). Category D is over-firing (sub-conditions: transition fires before competency threshold reached; cell perceptible-as-knowledge before transition; exception fires for non-knowledge cells; banking on flagged hazard cells). Category E is developmental degradation (pre-first-transition behaviour fails byte-identical against v0.13).

**Category F** is the v0.14-specific honesty constraint named in Section 1.5 of this paper. The constraint applies to architectural claims regardless of the operational result.

The pre-registration document was committed to the public repository at github.com/RancidShack/developmental-agent before any v0.14 code was written. The v0.14.1 amendment was committed before any reanalysis of the H2 finding was performed.

### 3.2 The H2 inversion

The pre-registered H2 hypothesis predicted that competing feature-rewards from transitioned knowledge cells would delay mastery. Specifically, H2 predicted that `time_to_final_mastery` would lengthen relative to v0.13 at matched seeds, and `attractors_mastered` could decrease. The architectural decomposition's reading of its own consequence: more feature-rewarding cells available at a given moment means more candidates for the agent's attention, finite Phase 2 / Phase 3 budget means some candidates will be reached and some will not, so mastery completion should be delayed or reduced.

The data invert this prediction. Across the original batch's 180 matched-seed comparisons, v0.14 records higher `attractors_mastered` than v0.13 in 49 runs and lower in 29; the remaining 102 runs are equal. Across the replication's 180 comparisons, v0.14 is higher in 47 and lower in 20; 113 are equal. The direction is consistent across batches: v0.14 reaches higher attractor mastery more often than v0.13 at matched seeds, and the replication confirms the original batch's direction is structural.

End-state activation, under the strictly-stricter v0.14 trigger, fires more often than v0.13's end-state activation under v0.13's lighter trigger. In the original batch, v0.14 activates while v0.13 does not in 39 runs at matched seeds; v0.13 activates while v0.14 does not in 21. In the replication, the corresponding figures are 35 and 17. The strict-superset milestone (`all_attractors_mastered AND all_hazards_banked_as_knowledge`) fires more often than its subset milestone (`all_attractors_mastered`) at matched seeds. Mastery progresses further under v0.14, and a larger share of the mastery progression is completed within the run length.

The pre-registered hypothesis is contradicted. The mechanism by which the contradiction arises — the cross-layer feedback between mastery accumulation, the cell-type transition, the action-selection hard-gate exception, and subsequent mastery accumulation — is characterised in Section 4 as the third cross-layer effect the threat-layer programme has documented.

### 3.3 The flagging-artefact decomposition

Naive cross-batch comparison on the meta-report's `hazards_flagged` field produces a misleading reading. In the original batch, v0.13 records higher `hazards_flagged` than v0.14 in 154 of 180 matched-seed comparisons; v0.14 is higher in only 10; equal in 16. The headline figure invites the reading that v0.14 flags fewer hazards than v0.13.

The reading is incorrect. v0.14's banking rule clears the persistent threat flag at knowledge banking. A v0.14 run that successfully banks all five cells records, at run end, zero entries in `hazards_flagged` and five entries in `hazards_banked_as_knowledge`. The same run at the same seed under v0.13 records five entries in `hazards_flagged` because v0.13 has no banking mechanism for hazard cells; the flag persists. Both runs reflect the same underlying behaviour at the architectural level: the agent recognised five cells categorically as hazardous. The metric `hazards_flagged` asks "how many cells does the architecture currently record as flagged?", which depends on the architecture's flag-clearing rule rather than on the agent's recognition history.

The corrected metric is `hazards_flagged + hazards_banked_as_knowledge` — categorical hazard recognition, defined as cells the architecture has at any point recorded as flagged. Under this metric, the direction reverses. v0.14 records higher categorical recognition than v0.13 in 147 of 180 runs in the original batch; v0.13 is higher in 6; equal in 27. The same direction holds in the replication. The categorical recognition mechanism is not weakened under v0.14; it is propagated more aggressively, with the agent's competency-bounded engagement with formerly-walled-off cells exposing it to additional hazard recognition opportunities.

The methodological lesson the artefact carries is the temporal-scoping lesson v0.13.1 named, in a different specific form. v0.13.1's lesson concerned aggregate metrics that span the architectural-change moment within a single run. The v0.14 flagging artefact concerns aggregate metrics that span the architectural-change moment between runs — between v0.13 (no banking, flag persists) and v0.14 (banking, flag clears). The two architectures' flag-clearing rules differ; aggregate counts that depend on those rules cannot be compared directly. The corrected comparison requires reconstructing the recognition event count from the underlying records, not the architecture's current-state metric. The lesson generalises forward: any subsequent iteration whose banking rule changes a state field's clearing behaviour requires a corrected comparison metric for cross-iteration analysis.

### 3.4 Mastery preservation: by-the-attractor preservation across the divergent population

Section 3.1's Check 4a passes at 180 of 180: pre-first-transition behaviour matches v0.13 byte-for-byte. The check applies up to first cell-type transition; behaviour after that point is the cross-layer-effect domain Check 4b characterises.

Within the post-first-transition population, mastery dynamics show a different preservation pattern than v0.13's mastery dynamics did against v0.12. Whereas v0.13's mastery sequences were byte-identical to v0.12's in every activated run because all six attractors were banked by v0.13's activation step, v0.14's mastery sequences diverge from v0.13's at matched seeds in 146 of 180 runs (81%). The architectures fork at the second attractor banking — that is, exactly when the first hazard cell unlocks at competency threshold 1 — and diverge progressively from that point.

The divergence is mastery-side, not preservation failure. In every divergent run, the architecture's mastery layer continues to function as v0.11.2 specifies: the four mastery interventions hold for attractor cells, MASTERY_THRESHOLD = 3 is honoured, banking events fire at the third entry, the mastery flag is set, and post-banking attractor cells produce zero feature reward. What differs between v0.13 and v0.14 is the order in which attractor cells are banked and the timing of banking events — driven by the cross-layer feedback Section 4 characterises. The mastery layer's mechanisms are preserved; the trajectory through which those mechanisms operate is divergent.

A finer-grained preservation check applies to the first attractor banked in each run. In every matched-seed comparison across both batches, the first attractor banked under v0.14 is identical to the first attractor banked under v0.13. The fork begins from the second banking onward, by construction: the first hazard cell cannot transition until competency is at least 1, which requires at least one attractor mastered, which requires at least one banking event to have completed. Pre-first-transition behaviour is byte-identical (Check 4a); first attractor banked is identical by-the-attractor (180 of 180); the divergence begins where the architectural mechanism v0.14 introduces begins to fire.

### 3.5 The autonomy signature in mastery sequencing

Section 3.4's 81% mastery-sequence divergence reading invites a second-order question: which sequences does v0.14 prefer, and does the preference reflect the architectural specification (cells become available in the order their thresholds permit) or autonomous prioritisation (the agent engages cells in an order shaped by the inherited drives, not necessarily matching the order of architectural availability)?

The diagnostic metric is the relationship between the order in which cells transition to knowledge and the order in which the agent banks them. If the agent banked cells in the order they transitioned, the inherited drives would be acting as a faithful order-execution mechanism on the architectural availability schedule. If the agent banks cells in a different order — sometimes engaging a cell that transitioned later before completing engagement with one that transitioned earlier — the inherited drives are acting as autonomous prioritisers shaped by feature-aligned preference formation.

Across both batches, the order match rate is 13%. Of 357 banking events recorded in runs where at least one knowledge banking occurred, only 46 follow the transition order. The remaining 311 banking events bank cells in an order different from the order in which those cells transitioned. The agent autonomously prioritises engagement.

The pattern's structural cause is identifiable. When competency reaches threshold 1, the cell with threshold 1 transitions; if competency simultaneously reaches threshold 2 (as it does when competency was 1 at the prior step and just became 2 with the second attractor banking), the cell with threshold 2 transitions at the same step or soon after. Multiple cells become available within a small time window, and the agent's choice among them is shaped by the inherited drives — proximity to current location, novelty, learning progress, primitive attraction — rather than by any architectural ordering. The agent's banking-order reflects what the inherited drives produce on the available choice set, not the architectural availability schedule.

Connection to v0.8: the feature-aligned preference formation signature established in v0.8 (Baker, 2026a) was the v0.8 finding that the agent forms preferences over feature-bearing cells in a way shaped by encounter biography rather than by uniform allocation. v0.14 demonstrates the same signature operating at scale through composed inherited capabilities. The agent's banking order over knowledge cells is the v0.8 signature applied to a richer context: post-transition knowledge cells are feature-bearing, the agent forms preferences over them, and the preferences shape the banking order in a way that overrides architectural availability. The v0.8 signature does not need to be re-implemented for the v0.14 architecture; it appears at scale through the composition v0.14 produces.

The autonomy reading is empirically grounded. The trajectories visibly diverge from v0.13 in 81% of runs at matched seeds, and the banking-order divergence (87% of banking events not matching transition order) shows the divergence operating through cell-by-cell choice rather than through global trajectory drift. The Category F honesty constraint applies (Section 5.2): the autonomy reading is consistent with both autonomous prioritisation operating on inherited drive composition and a deflationary reading in which the drive composition simply plays out the architecture's specification. The empirical strength of the autonomy reading rests on the divergence from v0.13: the agent under v0.14 is doing something architecturally distinct, not running v0.13's trajectory through more cell-type transitions.

### 3.6 Per-cell mechanism: more diffuse than the original batch suggested

The original batch produced a striking spike. Cell (5, 9) at threshold 1 — that is, runs in which the permutation draw assigned threshold 1 to the (5, 9) cell — produced a mean Δ(`attractors_mastered`) against v0.13 of +0.649 across 37 runs. The spike was the strongest single signal in the original batch's per-cell-per-threshold matrix and invited the reading that the H2 inversion was concentrated at this cell: the (5, 9) cell sits in the three-cell hazard cluster between the agent's start position and the (9, 10) attractor, and its threshold-1 unlock at the moment of the first attractor banking might plausibly remove a Phase 2 routing constraint that was diverting the agent away from cells beyond.

The replication batch weakened this reading substantially. Under permutation_offset = 1, the (5, 9) at threshold 1 spike weakens to +0.250. The original spike was real but inflated by the particular permutation draw — that is, by the particular other-cells assignments that accompanied (5, 9)-at-threshold-1 in the original batch. The replication preserves (5, 9)-at-threshold-1 in some runs and reassigns the other cells differently; the spike weakens because the surrounding context was contributing to the original's apparent strength.

Across all 25 (cell × threshold) conditions, the persistence pattern is more even than the original batch's spike suggested. Of 25 conditions, 20 persist (positive Δ in both batches) or strengthen (positive Δ larger in replication). 3 reverse direction at small magnitudes (positive in one batch, negative in the other, both magnitudes < 0.15). 2 weaken in the same direction. Only one cell — (14, 13) — shows a clean monotone-with-threshold pattern in the replication, with Δ decreasing as the cell's threshold increases.

## 4. Cross-layer effects: the third observation, and the phenomenon-class confirmed

This section addresses the cross-layer effect Check 4b characterises and the phenomenon-class observation the cumulative count of three iterations supports. The section is structured to handle two related observations. The first is the v0.14 finding directly: the competency-gated transition rule bidirectionally couples the threat layer and the mastery layer through the action-selection hard-gate exception, and the activation-rate inversion is the cleanest demonstration. The second is the phenomenon-class observation: three observations across three iterations confirms the pattern the v0.13 paper named on the basis of two.

### 4.1 The v0.14 cross-layer effect characterised

v0.14's cell-type transition rule is read by the architectural decomposition as a localised modification: the rule changes a cell's type, which makes the cell perceptible through the same machinery the architecture uses to perceive attractor cells. The decomposition predicts what Section 4.4 names the local prediction: post-transition behaviour around the transitioned cell will differ from v0.13's behaviour in the same period, in a way concentrated near the transitioned cell.

The data support a stronger reading. The transition rule, the action-selection hard-gate exception, and the persistent threat flag form an architectural triple whose joint behavioural effect is bidirectional coupling between the threat and mastery layers. The mechanism:

- Mastery accumulates. The first attractor banks; competency = 1.
- Cell-type transition fires for the cell at threshold 1. The cell becomes perceptible as KNOWLEDGE.
- The hard-gate exception activates: the gate no longer excludes actions targeting this cell, despite the persistent threat flag.
- The agent's action-selection trajectory expands. Cells beyond the transitioned cell — cells the gate previously excluded by routing — become reachable through paths that pass adjacent to or through the transitioned cell.
- The expanded trajectory increases the agent's encounter rate with the remaining attractor cells. Mastery accumulates faster.
- The next attractor banks; competency = 2. The next cell-type transition fires.

The loop is not a property of either the threat layer alone or the mastery layer alone. It is a property of their coupling through the cell-type transition rule. The threat layer's hard-gate decision depends on cell type, which depends on competency, which depends on mastery accumulation. The mastery layer's accumulation depends on which attractors the agent reaches, which depends on the action-selection trajectory, which depends on the gate's exclusion set. Each layer's behaviour at step t feeds the other layer's input at step t + k for some small k.

The activation-rate inversion (Section 3.2) is the cleanest demonstration. The v0.14 end-state activation trigger is a strict superset of v0.13's: any v0.14 activation requires v0.13's milestones plus all-hazards-banked-as-knowledge. Under independent layers the v0.14 trigger could only fire less often than v0.13's. Under coupled layers the v0.14 trigger fires more often: the same architectural extension that makes the milestone harder also opens cells the agent could not previously enter, which enables further mastery accumulation, which enables competency to reach further thresholds, which enables further transitions, which open further cells. The strict-superset milestone is reached more often because the architecture that defines the milestone also constructs the path to it.

The H2 inversion (Section 3.2) and the per-cell diffuseness (Section 3.6) are both expressions of the same coupling. The H2 hypothesis predicted that competing feature-rewards from transitioned cells would delay mastery; the inversion shows the cells acting as accelerators rather than as competitors. The per-cell diffuseness shows the acceleration distributed across all five cells rather than concentrated at one. Both findings are consequences of the bidirectional coupling that the cell-type transition rule introduces.

### 4.2 Distinguishing the v0.14 effect from v0.11.2's and v0.13's effects

The three cross-layer effects the threat-layer programme has now documented share an architectural property — components decomposed cleanly at the architectural level produce coupled behaviour through the shared substrate of action selection — but differ in coupling direction.

**v0.11.2's effect (reported in the v0.12 paper).** The four mastery interventions changed the agent's exploration pattern through a behavioural side-effect, pulling hazard discovery earlier in the run and weakening the time-budget pressure that produced the v0.10 boundary condition. The coupling direction: mastery layer → threat layer. The mastery interventions did not modify the threat layer's mechanisms; they altered the trajectory through which the threat layer's mechanisms operated.

**v0.13's effect.** The activated end-state cell's attentional pull altered the agent's post-activation trajectory, which altered when the agent encountered hazard cells, which altered when threat flags converted and how many hazards were flagged across the run. The coupling direction: end-state mechanism → threat layer. The end-state mechanism did not modify the threat layer's mechanisms; the cell's pull altered the trajectory through which the threat layer's mechanisms operated.

**v0.14's effect.** The cell-type transition rule bidirectionally couples the threat layer and the mastery layer through the action-selection hard-gate exception and the persistent threat flag. The coupling direction: mastery layer ↔ threat layer, with the cell-type transition rule as the shared interface. The prior two effects were observed bidirectionally in the data while operating unidirectionally in mechanism — mastery accumulation feeding into threat-side outcomes by altering trajectory. The v0.14 effect operates bidirectionally in mechanism: mastery accumulation drives transition firing, transition firing opens action-selection paths, opened paths drive further mastery accumulation. Each layer's state at step t feeds the other layer's state at step t + k.

The progression across the three iterations is informative. v0.11.2's effect was unidirectional and surfaced retrospectively. v0.13's effect was unidirectional and surfaced through the v0.13.1-amended audit's deliberate temporal scoping. v0.14's effect is bidirectional and was anticipated, named in the pre-registration as the H2 hypothesis, and then surfaced as inverted from the predicted direction. The phenomenon-class is now visible in three forms: an unidirectional retrospective effect (v0.11.2), an unidirectional prospectively-audited effect (v0.13), and a bidirectional effect with its predicted direction inverted (v0.14).

### 4.3 The phenomenon-class confirmed

The v0.13 paper named cross-layer behavioural effects as a phenomenon-class on the basis of two observations across two iterations. The framing the v0.13 paper used was that two observations is the minimum number that supports the framing; three or more would strengthen it substantially. Three observations is now available, and the framing is confirmed.

The architectural reason is the same in all three cases. The threat layer, the mastery layer, the v0.13 end-state mechanism, and the v0.14 cell-type transition rule are presented in the architecture's design as decomposed components with localised responsibilities. Each component has its own state, its own update rules, its own contribution to behaviour. The architectural decomposition is methodologically clean and supports single-variable-change discipline at the research-question level. The components are not, however, behaviourally independent. They are coupled through the shared substrate of the agent's action-selection trajectory.

What three observations confirm, that two could only suggest, is that the coupling is reliable across architectural extensions of different kinds. The three iterations introduced architectural extensions on three different sides of the architecture: mastery interventions (v0.11.2), end-state activation (v0.13), competency-gated content transformation (v0.14). All three produced cross-layer behavioural effects through the same coupling mechanism. The pattern is not specific to one type of extension or one component pair; it is a property of the architecture's decomposition relative to its decision substrate.

Three implications follow, each extending the corresponding implication the v0.13 paper named.

First, **single-iteration preservation audits are necessary but not sufficient.** The v0.13 paper named this on two observations. The third observation strengthens the implication: the v0.14 batches' Check 4a passes at 180/180 in the period before the architectural-change moment fires, while Check 4b surfaces the cross-layer effect in the post-change period. The temporal-scoping discipline the v0.13.1 amendment introduced is necessary for any iteration whose architectural extension has a within-run firing moment.

Second, **matched-seed comparison is the methodological tool that surfaces cross-layer effects.** The v0.13 paper named this on two observations. The third observation confirms it: the H2 inversion would have been invisible in aggregate-distribution comparison, where v0.13 and v0.14 produce broadly similar batch-level distributions. The inversion appears only when individual runs at identical seeds are compared cell-by-cell. The seed chain that links v0.10 through v0.14 — preserved through the CSV-loading discipline rather than regenerated — is the methodological apparatus that makes the comparison possible.

Third, **the v1.0 integration paper will need to characterise cross-layer effects across the full inheritance chain.** The v0.13 paper raised this. The v0.14 paper extends it: the integration paper has now to characterise three observations rather than two, with the third observation involving bidirectional coupling rather than unidirectional. The audit's tooling — matched-seed comparison plus temporal scoping — is in place; the audit's scope grows with each iteration's contribution. The v1.0 paper is where the phenomenon-class is characterised at full extent; the present paper records the third observation as part of that characterisation's evidence base.

### 4.4 The Category F honesty constraint and Section 4 findings

The Category F constraint applies to Section 4's claims as it applied to Section 4 of the v0.13 paper.

The cross-layer effect Section 4.1 characterises is a behavioural finding: at the level of individual runs, the cell-type transition rule's interaction with the inherited threat and mastery layers produces bidirectional coupling that the architectural decomposition does not capture. The architectural claim associated with this finding — that v0.14's transition rule, hard-gate exception, and persistent threat flag form an architectural triple whose joint behavioural effect is coupling — is consistent with both the substantive reading (the architecture's components are bidirectionally coupled in a way that decomposition does not capture) and a deflationary reading (the agent's trajectory after the transition fires differs from the trajectory it would have taken without the transition, which is true by construction of the architectural extension).

The phenomenon-class observation Section 4.3 makes is more structural and less easily collapsed. Across three iterations, three architectural extensions on three different layers produce cross-layer effects through the same mechanism (the shared substrate of action selection). The fact that three distinct architectural extensions produce structurally similar cross-layer effects suggests that the coupling is a property of the architecture rather than a property of any specific extension. The phenomenon-class claim is bounded by Category F in the same sense as the v0.13 locating-mechanism claim was: the coupling is performed by inherited machinery (the decision process the components share), not by any new architectural mechanism the iterations introduced. An architectural variant in which the components are coupled not through shared action selection but through some explicit cross-layer interface would address this; that variant is a substantively different programme and is not proposed in the present paper.

The local prediction the architectural decomposition would have made — that post-transition behaviour will differ from v0.13 in a way concentrated near the transitioned cell — is empirically incomplete. The H2 inversion shows the difference is global rather than local, distributed across the run rather than concentrated at the transitioned cell. The local prediction is consistent with the data but does not account for the magnitude of the inversion. The bidirectional-coupling reading does account for the magnitude, but does so by claiming a property the architectural decomposition does not directly represent. Category F bounds the claim accordingly: the bidirectional-coupling reading is supported by the data and is not a deflationary restatement of the architectural extension, but is consistent with the data alongside the obstacle-removal reading the next section considers.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 The H2 inversion: substantive and deflationary readings, Category F bounded

The H2 inversion is the empirical anchor of the v0.14 findings. The pre-registered hypothesis predicted delayed mastery; the data inverted the prediction across both batches. The architectural reading the inversion supports is the bidirectional-coupling reading Section 4.1 characterised: the cell-type transition rule, the action-selection hard-gate exception, and the persistent threat flag form an architectural triple whose joint effect couples the threat and mastery layers through their shared decision substrate, with the activation-rate inversion as the cleanest demonstration.

Two readings remain consistent with the v0.14 batches' data and cannot be distinguished from those batches alone.

**The obstacle-removal reading** is mechanistic and structural. Under this reading, the cell-type transition rule matters principally because the action-selection hard-gate exception removes a Phase 2 / Phase 3 routing constraint. Hazard cells, prior to transition, are flagged and gate-excluded; the agent's trajectory is constrained to paths that avoid the hazard clusters. Some attractor cells are reachable only through paths that pass close to hazard clusters; the gate's exclusion of those paths increases the trajectory length to those attractors and makes them less likely to be reached within a finite run length. When a hazard cell transitions to knowledge, the gate's exclusion is lifted for that cell; paths through that cell become available; the previously-distant attractors become reachable through shorter trajectories; mastery accumulates faster. The cell-type transition is, on this reading, principally a gate-exception mechanism. The cell's post-transition feature reward and primitive attraction bias contribute additionally — drawing the agent into the cell, supporting the engagement that produces three-entry banking — but the obstacle removal is the load-bearing element.

**The developmental reading** is structurally different. Under this reading, the agent's relationship to the formerly-walled-off cells shifts at the transition moment in a way the inherited drives respond to beyond the gate-exception. The cell, post-transition, is perceived as a feature-bearing cell of a kind the agent has engaged with before (attractors, end-state); the drives' engagement with the cell mirrors their engagement with attractors at the analogous developmental stage. The persistent threat flag is part of the agent's record of what the cell was; the cell's transition to knowledge is part of the agent's record of what the cell has become. The agent's behaviour around the cell post-transition is shaped by both records. The acceleration of mastery is, on this reading, a consequence of two contributions acting together: the gate exception that opens routing paths, and the cell-as-knowledge engaging the inherited drives on a developmental basis the architecture has accumulated. The developmental contribution is the load-bearing element under this reading; the gate exception is necessary but does not by itself capture what the cell-type transition does.

Both readings are consistent with the v0.14 batches' data. The H2 inversion's magnitude, the per-cell diffuseness, the autonomy signature in mastery sequencing, and the activation-rate inversion are all consistent with both readings. The readings make different predictions for an architectural variant in which the hard-gate exception fires at competency-threshold-reached without transitioning the cell type — that is, a variant in which the cell remains HAZARD (still costly to enter), the threat flag stays set, but the gate no longer excludes it. Under the obstacle-removal reading, the variant should produce a similar inversion magnitude to v0.14: the load-bearing element (the gate exception) is preserved. Under the developmental reading, the variant should weaken the inversion substantially: the cell-type transition the developmental reading depends on is absent.

The variant is reserved for subsequent work and is named here for the methodological record rather than committed in the present paper. The Category F honesty constraint requires that the architectural claim made in the v0.14 paper is bounded by what the v0.14 batches alone can support, and the v0.14 batches alone cannot distinguish the readings. The bidirectional-coupling characterisation in Section 4.1 holds under both readings; what the readings differ on is the architectural significance of the cell-type transition itself, which the variant would test directly.[^obs-removal]

[^obs-removal]: The v0.15 obstacle-removal-isolation variant is the methodological vehicle for distinguishing these readings. It is not pre-registered or committed in the present paper. The empirical question — whether the inversion persists when only the gate exception fires — is a separate decision to be settled in v0.15 pre-registration on its own terms.

### 5.2 The autonomy signature: connection to v0.8, Category F bounded

The autonomy signature in mastery sequencing (Section 3.5) connects directly to the v0.8 feature-aligned preference formation signature (Baker, 2026a). The connection is structural: v0.8 established that the agent forms preferences over feature-bearing cells in a way shaped by encounter biography rather than by uniform allocation; v0.14 shows the same signature operating at scale through composed inherited capabilities. The agent's banking order over knowledge cells overrides the architectural availability schedule in 87% of banking events.

The connection is empirically grounded but Category F bounds the architectural claim. The autonomy reading is consistent with two interpretations.

The substantive interpretation reads the banking-order divergence as autonomous prioritisation operating on the inherited drive composition: the agent's choices among available knowledge cells reflect feature-aligned preference formation in the v0.8 sense, with the choices shaped by encounter biography (which cells the agent has been near, what reward the agent has accumulated from each, what novelty signal each provides) rather than by the architecture's availability ordering. This interpretation reads the 87% override as the v0.8 signature manifesting in the v0.14 context.

The deflationary interpretation reads the divergence as the drive composition simply playing out the architectural specification. Under this interpretation, the inherited drives are a mechanism that produces a particular trajectory given a particular environment state; v0.14 changes the environment state by adding knowledge cells at varying transition moments; the trajectory the drives produce on the changed state happens to differ from the architectural availability ordering. The override is not autonomous prioritisation in any strong sense; it is the drives' deterministic response to the changed state.

The autonomy reading is empirically stronger than the deflationary reading because the trajectories visibly diverge from v0.13 in 81% of runs at matched seeds. Under the deflationary reading, the divergence would reflect only the cells whose perceptual properties differ between v0.13 and v0.14 — the transitioned knowledge cells. Under the substantive reading, the divergence extends to the whole post-transition trajectory: the agent's choices among available targets, including unmastered attractors and not-yet-transitioned hazard cells, are shaped by the drive composition in ways the architecture's specification does not directly determine. The 81% mastery-sequence divergence figure is empirical evidence for the substantive reading, but the deflationary reading cannot be ruled out from the v0.14 batches alone.

The Category F honesty constraint applies. The architectural claim made for the autonomy signature is bounded: v0.14 demonstrates that the v0.8 feature-aligned preference formation signature is present at scale through composed inherited capabilities, with the empirical strength of the autonomy reading resting on the matched-seed divergence from v0.13. Whether this constitutes a substantive autonomy capability or a deflationary drive-composition artefact is not determined by the v0.14 batches alone.

### 5.3 The replication's lesson: per-cell mechanism more complex than single-bottleneck

Section 3.6 reported that the per-cell mechanism is more diffusely distributed than the original batch's striking spike at (5, 9)-at-threshold-1 suggested. The replication's permutation_offset = 1 weakened the spike from +0.649 to +0.250 while preserving positive Δ across most (cell × threshold) conditions. The methodological lesson the spike weakening teaches is general.

A single-batch finding involving a randomly-drawn structural parameter (such as the threshold permutation here) can produce an apparent concentration that is in fact partially artefactual. The original batch's spike was real — (5, 9)-at-threshold-1 produced the largest single Δ in the data — but the spike's apparent concentration was inflated by what other configurations the original draw had not surfaced. The replication's purpose was not to invalidate the original; it was to characterise the original's structural-versus-artefactual decomposition. The weakening of the spike to +0.250 in the replication, while remaining in the same direction across 20 of 25 conditions, separates the structural component (the H2 inversion is real and broadly distributed) from the artefactual component (the original batch's particular permutation made one condition look stronger than its underlying contribution justifies).

The lesson generalises forward. Any iteration whose architectural mechanism involves a per-run random draw — a permutation, a sampled location, a randomly assigned parameter — should report a replication batch at a different draw before drawing inferences about per-(draw-conditioned) findings. The replication's overhead is one batch's compute; the inferential gain is the structural-versus-artefactual decomposition that single-batch reporting cannot provide. The v0.14.1 amendment that introduced the replication batch in the present iteration is an instance of this lesson applied; subsequent iterations that involve random per-run draws should adopt the same discipline at the pre-registration stage rather than at the amendment stage.

The replication's narrow scope — one alternative offset rather than a full re-randomisation — is sufficient for the structural-versus-artefactual decomposition at the headline-level. A full re-randomisation across many offsets would refine the per-condition Δ estimates but would not alter the inversion's directional finding. The narrow replication is the methodological vehicle for distinguishing structural-from-artefactual; the per-condition refinement is a separate question.

### 5.4 Limits of present work

Five limits of the present work are worth naming explicitly, beyond the Category F honesty constraints already discussed at length.

The architecture remains tabular and small-scale. The 20×20 grid is sufficient for testing the competency-gated transformation rule but does not address scale-related questions. Whether the bidirectional coupling Section 4.1 characterised survives migration to environments where representations must be learned rather than tabulated, whether the autonomy signature appears at scale through composed capabilities in non-tabular settings, whether the temporal-scoping discipline Check 4a depends on holds in environments with continuous state — these are questions the present work does not address. The architectural principle is demonstrated in a sandbox.

The cell-type transition rule's perceptual signature is attractor-equivalent. Post-transition knowledge cells carry the same FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT and ATTRACTION_BONUS as attractor cells, on the design principle that the test is whether the inherited locating machinery handles cells whose perceptual properties have changed mid-run. The simplest version of the test uses perceptual properties identical to existing targets. Whether the same machinery handles cells with different post-transition perceptual signatures (graded reward by competency level, beacon-style adjacent signals, learned-feature signatures) is the question the v0.13 paper reserved for v0.15+ and the present paper continues to reserve.

The H2 inversion's two readings cannot be distinguished from the v0.14 batches alone. Section 5.1 discussed this directly. The architectural variant that would distinguish them is reserved for subsequent work.

The phenomenon-class observation in Section 4.3 is based on three cross-layer effects across three iterations. Three is the count at which the v0.13 paper said the framing would be confirmed; the framing is confirmed. Whether the pattern continues to hold across further iterations — particularly iterations that introduce architectural extensions on layers other than threat and mastery — remains to be seen. The v1.0 integration paper's preservation audit will provide one further test.

The autonomy reading rests on the matched-seed divergence from v0.13 in 81% of runs and the banking-order override in 87% of banking events. The deflationary reading cannot be ruled out from the v0.14 batches alone. Section 5.2 discussed this at length.

### 5.5 Architectural questions opened, and the position in the programme arc

Three architectural questions remain open at the close of v0.14, and the present paper's findings sharpen the case for addressing them in subsequent iterations.

The first is the obstacle-removal-versus-developmental question Section 5.1 named. The architectural variant that would distinguish the readings — hard-gate exception fires at competency-threshold-reached without cell-type transition — is the methodological vehicle. The variant is reserved for v0.15 or later as a separate decision; the present paper does not commit it.

The second is the mastery-side category-level generalisation question, identified at the close of v0.12 and still reserved at the close of v0.13. v0.14 does not address it. The question — whether mastery on one attractor accelerates mastery of others through a signature-matching mechanism parallel to v0.12's threat-side mechanism — remains the natural symmetry-completion target. v0.14's autonomy-signature finding is suggestive: the agent's banking order over knowledge cells reflects feature-aligned preference formation, which is the v0.8 signature on which any signature-matching mechanism would build. But the symmetry-completion iteration is a separate architectural commitment.

The third is the v1.0 integration paper. The integration paper's preservation audit was reserved at the close of v0.13 and remains reserved. v0.14's contribution to the integration paper's evidence base is substantial: a third cross-layer-effect observation extending the phenomenon-class confirmed; the v0.13.1 temporal-scoping discipline applied prospectively in v0.14 pre-registration with Check 4a passing at 180/180 in both batches; the v0.13 calibration-discipline lesson applied prospectively with all Category A thresholds passing; and the matched-seed comparison apparatus extended to cover v0.14's amended end-state trigger.

The position in the programme arc, at the close of v0.14: the threat-and-mastery side of the architecture has now traversed v0.9 (problem identification), v0.10 (persistent threat representation), v0.11.2 (bounded mastery), v0.12 (category-level threat-side generalisation), v0.13 (late-target activation), and v0.14 (competency-gated content transformation). The architecture handles persistent categorical knowledge in both layers, generalises across hazard exemplars on first entry, locates late-appearing feature-reward sources at random locations, and supports cell-type transitions tied to developmental state with bidirectional coupling between the contributing layers. Three cross-layer behavioural effects have been documented across three iterations. The v1.0 integration paper is the natural next milestone; v0.15 work, of whatever architectural commitment, follows.

## 6. Conclusion

The v0.14 iteration tested whether the architecture inherited from v0.13 supports cell-type transitions tied to the agent's developmental state, with hazard cells transitioning to knowledge cells when the agent's competency reaches each cell's assigned threshold. Across a pre-registered batch of 180 runs and a v0.14.1-amended replication batch of a further 180 runs at a different permutation offset, with seeds matched to the v0.13 batch (Baker, 2026l, in preparation), v0.14 produces six findings.

The pre-registered Category A check set passes in full. Conditional first-entry-after-transition rate at 160,000 steps reaches 96.2% in the original batch and 96.6% in the replication, exceeding the 85% threshold. Three-entry banking rate at 160,000 steps reaches 59 of 60 in the original batch, exceeding the 75% threshold; banking at 80,000 steps reaches 51 of 60, exceeding the 60% threshold. End-state activation under the strictly-stricter v0.14 trigger reaches 53 of 60 at 160,000 steps in the original batch and 58 of 60 in the replication, exceeding the 30/60 floor. Pre-first-transition behavioural preservation against v0.13 passes byte-identical at 180 of 180 in both batches. The v0.13 calibration discipline applied prospectively in the v0.14 pre-registration produced a clean Category A verdict.

The H2 inversion is the surprise that operational success surfaced. The pre-registered hypothesis predicted that competing feature-rewards from transitioned knowledge cells would delay mastery; the data inverted the prediction. v0.14 records higher `attractors_mastered` than v0.13 in matched-seed comparison more often than the reverse across both batches, and end-state activation under the strictly-stricter v0.14 trigger fires more often than under v0.13's lighter trigger. The replication confirms the inversion is structural rather than a one-permutation artefact. Section 4 characterised the inversion as a behavioural consequence of bidirectional coupling between the threat and mastery layers through the cell-type transition rule, the action-selection hard-gate exception, and the persistent threat flag. Section 5 considered the two architectural readings the v0.14 batches alone cannot distinguish: a mechanistic obstacle-removal reading and a developmental reading. Both readings are consistent with the data; the architectural variant that would distinguish them is reserved for subsequent work.

The flagging-artefact decomposition records a methodological lesson for cross-iteration aggregate-metric comparison. v0.14's banking rule clears the persistent threat flag at knowledge banking, which the meta-report's `hazards_flagged` metric records as flag-not-set. Naive comparison invites the reading that v0.14 flags fewer hazards than v0.13. The corrected metric — `flagged + banked_as_knowledge` — reverses the direction: v0.14 records more categorical hazard recognition than v0.13 in 147 of 180 runs in the original batch versus 6. The categorical recognition mechanism is propagated more aggressively under v0.14, not weakened. The methodological lesson is the cross-iteration analogue of v0.13.1's temporal-scoping lesson.

The cross-layer-effects extension confirms the phenomenon-class on the count of three. The v0.13 paper named the phenomenon-class on two observations. v0.14's bidirectional coupling produces the third. The three observations involve three different architectural extensions on three different sides of the architecture: mastery interventions (v0.11.2), end-state activation (v0.13), competency-gated content transformation (v0.14). All three produce cross-layer behavioural effects through the same mechanism (the shared substrate of action selection). The phenomenon-class is confirmed; the v1.0 integration paper has now to characterise three observations rather than two, with one of them involving bidirectional coupling.

The autonomy signature in mastery sequencing emerged from analysis after the H2 inversion was identified. v0.14's mastery sequences diverge from v0.13's at matched seeds in 146 of 180 runs (81%); the architectures fork at the second attractor banking, the moment the first hazard cell unlocks. Knowledge banking order matches transition order in only 46 of 357 banking events (13%); the agent autonomously prioritises engagement, routinely overriding the order in which cells became architecturally available. The signature connects directly to the feature-aligned preference formation signature established in v0.8 (Baker, 2026a) and demonstrates that signature operating at scale through composed inherited capabilities. Category F applies: the autonomy reading is empirically stronger than the deflationary reading, with the matched-seed divergence and banking-order override carrying the empirical weight, but the deflationary reading cannot be ruled out from the v0.14 batches alone.

The per-cell mechanism finding records that the original batch's striking spike at cell (5, 9) at threshold 1 (mean Δ = +0.649) weakened to +0.250 under the v0.14.1 replication. The full per-(cell × threshold) comparison shows persistence in 20 of 25 conditions, reversal in 3 small-magnitude conditions, weakening in the same direction in 2. Only one cell ((14, 13)) shows a clean monotone-with-threshold pattern in the replication. The mechanism is broadly distributed across the five hazard cells rather than concentrated at a single bottleneck; the "obstacle removal at the bottleneck cell" reading from the original batch is a partial truth.

The methodological discipline that has held throughout the programme held during this iteration. Pre-registration was committed before any v0.14 code was written. Implementation completed in a single pass without architectural amendment, despite the implementation surface being substantially larger than v0.13's was. One amendment was issued (v0.14.1), scoped narrowly to add the replication apparatus rather than modify the architecture. The amendment budget for v0.14 stands at one of three at the close of the iteration.

The threat-layer programme has now moved through six iterations. The natural next milestones are the v1.0 integration paper that audits the cumulative inheritance chain and a v0.15 iteration of separate architectural commitment, neither of which the present paper proposes in detail. v0.14's contribution is the competency-gated content transformation rule, the H2 inversion that overturned the pre-registered hypothesis, the autonomy signature operating at scale, and the third cross-layer-effect observation that confirms the phenomenon-class. The architectural mechanism the iteration was designed to test does what the iteration was designed to test it could do, in a way the iteration's own pre-registration did not predict.
