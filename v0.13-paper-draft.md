# End-State Target Activation in a Small Artificial Learner via Random-Location Cell Appearance on the All-Attractors-Mastered Signal

**Author:** Nicholas P M Baker, Synapstak Ltd
**Date:** 26 April 2026
**Status:** Working draft — Sections 1 through 4

---

## Abstract

A preceding paper (Baker, 2026i, in preparation) established category-level generalisation in the threat layer through cell-type signature-matching. The paper inherits a converged architecture: by 160,000 steps under v0.12 inheritance, mean attractors mastered approaches 5.80 of 6 with 49 of 60 runs reaching full mastery, and the agent's post-mastery exploration concentrates on the closest banked cell to the start position. The architecture has, in this sense, finished what the existing environment offers it. v0.13 is the iteration directed at the question this convergence opens: can the architecture be given something new to find at the moment the existing attractor set is exhausted, and does the architecture's locating machinery handle this new target reliably when its location is not known in advance?

The architectural change is contained. At run start, one neutral cell coordinate is sampled at random from the passable non-attractor scope and designated as the end-state cell location. The cell remains a neutral cell until activation. When the agent banks all six attractors — when `sum(mastery_flag.values()) == len(attractor_cells)` first becomes true — the cell type transitions from NEUTRAL to a new END_STATE type, carrying feature reward and primitive attraction bias parallel to attractor cells. The agent locates the cell (or does not) within the remaining run length. On first entry post-activation the cell banks: the four mastery interventions developed across the v0.11 series apply, the cell is depleted, and the agent has no remaining feature-reward source in the environment.

Across a pre-registered batch of 180 runs spanning six cost levels and three run lengths, with seeds matched to the v0.12 batch (Baker, 2026i, in preparation), v0.13 produces four findings.

The first concerns the locating mechanism. Among runs in which activation fires, the agent locates the end-state cell at a 46.2% rate at 20,000 steps, 82.9% at 80,000, and **93.9% at 160,000** — 46 of 49 activated runs successfully reaching a randomly-placed cell within the remaining post-activation window. The mean post-activation discovery time is 1,036 steps at 20,000 steps total run length, 3,395 at 80,000, and 5,700 at 160,000. The locating mechanism works as the pre-registration's mechanistic prediction specifies, and works at high reliability when the post-activation window is sufficient.

The second finding concerns the activation rate. The all-attractors-mastered signal fires in 13 of 60 runs at 20,000 steps, 35 of 60 at 80,000, and 49 of 60 at 160,000. These figures are exactly the v0.12 batch's 6/6 mastery rates at the same matched seeds. v0.13's activation distribution is the v0.12 inheritance distribution; v0.13 introduces no architectural change to mastery dynamics, and the activation rate is therefore bounded above by the rate at which the v0.12 architecture converges to full mastery within the run length. This bounding is a structural fact about the iteration's place in the inheritance chain, not a result of the v0.13 architectural extension.

The third finding concerns the pre-activation behavioural preservation. The pre-registration committed to byte-identical pre-activation behaviour against v0.12 at matched seeds; the v0.13.1 amendment (Baker, 2026k) clarifies the audit operationalisation, restricting byte-identical comparison to runs in which activation does not fire (where pre-activation behaviour equals full-run behaviour). Under the amended audit, **83 of 83 non-activated runs match v0.12 byte-for-byte** across all 17 audited fields. The architectural extension v0.13 introduces does not contaminate pre-activation behaviour. When the architecture does not activate, it produces exactly v0.12's behaviour.

The fourth finding concerns the post-activation cross-layer effect. Among the 97 runs in which activation fires, 48 produce threat-layer trajectories that diverge from v0.12 at matched seeds. Mastery dynamics are byte-identical to v0.12 in all 97 activated runs. The divergence is exclusively threat-layer: the activated end-state cell's attentional pull modestly biases the agent toward more hazard encounters in some runs, fewer in others, with v0.13 recording higher hazards-flagged in 17 runs versus v0.12 higher in 11. This is a cross-layer effect. It is also, structurally, the second cross-layer effect the threat-layer programme has now documented: the v0.12 paper reported that v0.11.2's mastery interventions affected threat-side cleanness through exploration redistribution; the v0.13 paper reports that the activated end-state cell affects threat-side encounter through attentional redirection. Two observations across two iterations is enough to name the phenomenon-class. Cross-layer behavioural effects, surfaced by matched-seed comparison and invisible to single-iteration architectural decomposition, are an architectural property of this programme.

The pre-registered Category A discovery-rate thresholds (30/60 at 20,000 steps, 50/60 at 80,000, 55/60 at 160,000) fail at all three run lengths because the activation rate is bounded by the v0.12 6/6 distribution and cannot exceed 13, 35, and 49 respectively. The thresholds were calibrated against an implicit assumption of higher convergence rates; the actual ceiling is the inheritance distribution. The paper reports this honestly as a finding about pre-registration calibration discipline rather than as evidence the architectural mechanism fails. The conditional discovery rate — discovery given activation — is the locating-mechanism finding the paper carries forward.

The pre-registered Category F honesty constraint applies. The locating capability operates through inherited Phase 3 dynamics: the novelty drive, the learning-progress drive, the primitive attraction bias, the preference accumulation. v0.13 contributes the cell type, the activation signal, and the cell-type transition; the locating itself is performed by machinery the architecture already had. The architectural claim is bounded accordingly. v0.15 work on richer locating mechanisms (beacon-style perceptual signals, learned end-state-detection, signals not directly tied to existing state) is the necessary follow-on for distinguishing inherited-machinery-suffices from a v0.13-specific locating contribution. The richer-content work that emerged from the v0.12 design conversation as v0.14 / v0.15 (competency-gated content cells transforming based on competency thresholds) inherits v0.13's locating mechanism as a tested precondition: when v0.14 or v0.15 introduces cells whose locations are not known in advance and whose perceptual properties change mid-run, v0.13 has already established that the inherited Phase 3 dynamics handle late-appearing targets at random locations.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Programme to date

A small tabular developmental agent occupying a 20×20 structured environment has, across previous papers, demonstrated five behavioural signatures associated with developmental learning, and has progressively acquired the architectural machinery that makes those signatures stable across extended horizons. v0.8 established the signatures themselves: rule adherence, focused autonomy, feature-aligned preference formation, a specialist-generalist trade-off, and full biographical individuation (Baker, 2026a, 2026b). v0.9 decomposed the v0.8 hazard handling and identified an unbounded-accumulation problem at long horizons (Baker, 2026c). v0.10 closed the gap on the hazard side by introducing experience-driven persistent threat representation (Baker, 2026d). v0.11 closed it on the mastery side through three amendments that decomposed bounded mastery into the four code-path interventions it actually required (Baker, 2026e, 2026f, 2026g). v0.12 supplied category-level generalisation in the threat layer through signature-matching (Baker, 2026h, 2026i, in preparation), eliminating the v0.10 boundary condition while preserving v0.11.2's mastery and individuation findings.

The arc the programme has been tracing through these four iterations has a recognisable shape. v0.9 identified that the v0.8 architecture lacked persistent storage for any learned categorical knowledge. v0.10 supplied it for hazards. v0.11.2 supplied it for mastery. v0.12 added category-level generalisation across hazard exemplars. By the close of v0.12, the architecture's two persistent knowledge layers — threat and mastery — both store categorical information, both bound the unbounded-accumulation problem v0.9 identified, and the threat layer additionally supports first-entry generalisation across cells of the same category. The architecture, by 160,000 steps under v0.12 inheritance, reliably converges toward exhausting the available attractor set: mean attractors mastered approaches 5.80 of 6, with 49 of 60 runs reaching full mastery in the v0.12 batch (Baker, 2026i, in preparation, Table 1).

This convergence is the empirical hook for the v0.13 iteration. The architecture has, by long-horizon standards, finished what the existing environment offers it. The question this opens is whether the architecture can be given something new to find at the moment of exhaustion, and whether the architecture's locating machinery — the inherited novelty drive, learning-progress drive, primitive attraction bias, and preference accumulation that handled the original attractor set — handles a new target whose location is not known in advance.

### 1.2 The convergence finding and what v0.13 was designed to test

The v0.11.2 batch (Baker, 2026g) reported a convergence finding the v0.11.2 paper underweighted at the time: by 160,000 steps, mean attractors mastered approached 5.87 of 6, with 53 of 60 runs reaching full mastery. The v0.12 batch confirmed this with a slight refinement under v0.12's inheritance: 5.80 of 6, with 49 of 60 reaching full mastery (Baker, 2026i, in preparation, Section 3.4). At the same 160,000-step run length, the meta-report's distinct-top-attractor count was 1 of 6 across the batch, and the top attractor was (3, 3) in 60 of 60 runs.

The (3, 3) concentration is structural rather than architectural. With all attractors banked, (3, 3) is the closest banked cell to the agent's start position at (1, 1). The four mastery interventions developed across the v0.11 series have depleted the feature-reward signals that would have drawn the agent elsewhere; (3, 3)'s closeness to the start position makes it the residual destination for whatever Phase 3 attention remains. The architecture is not pulled toward (3, 3) by any signal specific to that cell; it ranges around (3, 3) because no other cell offers a competing pull.

The v0.13 iteration was designed to test whether this convergence opens an architectural follow-on question with a tractable answer. Suppose, at the moment of all-attractors-mastered, a new feature-reward source appears in the environment at a location the agent does not know in advance. Would the architecture's existing locating machinery — the same machinery that originally drew the agent toward attractors during Phase 2 — re-engage on this new source? Would the agent locate it within the remaining run length?

The pre-registration committed before any v0.13 code was written (Baker, 2026j) specified the architectural mechanism. A new cell type, END_STATE, is introduced. At run start, one neutral cell coordinate is sampled at random and designated as the end-state cell location. The cell remains a neutral cell perceptually — invisible to the architecture as anything other than a cell of type NEUTRAL — until activation. When the all-attractors-mastered signal fires, the cell type transitions to END_STATE, carrying feature reward `FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT = 0.15` and primitive attraction bias `ATTRACTION_BONUS = 0.3` parallel to attractor cells. The agent's existing perception, drive composition, and action-selection machinery handle the cell as it would handle any other feature-rewarding cell. On first entry post-activation, the cell banks: the four mastery interventions apply (depleted feature reward, cleared attraction bias, reset preference, blocked accumulation), the cell becomes a banked cell, and the agent has no remaining feature-reward source in the environment for the rest of the run.

The pre-registration's design choices were settled in conversation before drafting (Baker, internal record, 25 April 2026). Three are worth naming explicitly here because they shape what v0.13 tests and what it does not.

The first is the activation signal: all-attractors-mastered, not mastery-rate-decline-window or any combination. The choice was made on the grounds that all-attractors-mastered is a structural fact tied to existing architectural state (mastery flags) and does not introduce a tuning-threshold parameter that would compromise the architectural claim.

The second is the cell location: random-at-run-start, not fixed. A fixed location does not test whether the locating mechanism generalises across coordinates; it tests whether the agent, in its post-mastery exploration concentration, happens to cover the chosen coordinate. Random sampling at run start ensures the architectural claim is about locating capability rather than incidental coverage of a specific cell.

The third is the banking rule: one-entry banking on first post-activation entry, parallel to the four mastery interventions but at one-entry rather than three-entry threshold. The architectural reasoning: the cell is found, not mastered. v0.13's research question is whether the agent locates the cell, not whether the agent learns about the cell across multiple visits.

### 1.3 The pre-registered Category F honesty constraint

The pre-registration's Section 7 named six interpretation categories. Five (A through E) follow the v0.10 / v0.11.2 / v0.12 pattern. Category F is the v0.13-specific honesty constraint and shapes what the paper can claim regardless of the operational result.

The deflationary reading the constraint addresses is structural. The locating capability v0.13 demonstrates is not a v0.13 contribution; it is inherited from v0.11.2 / v0.12 Phase 3 dynamics. The novelty drive that pulls the agent toward cells whose model is uncertain operates without v0.13 modification. The learning-progress drive that rewards reduction-in-prediction-error operates without v0.13 modification. The primitive attraction bias that biases action selection toward adjacent feature-rewarding cells operates without v0.13 modification, with one bookkeeping addition: the v0.13 implementation extends the bias mechanism to recognise END_STATE cells alongside ATTRACTOR cells, but the bias machinery itself is unchanged. The preference accumulation that builds Phase 3 attention concentration operates without v0.13 modification, with the parallel bookkeeping addition that the end-state cell's preference resets at banking and accumulation blocks thereafter.

What v0.13 contributes is the cell type, the activation signal, and the cell-type transition. The locating itself — the agent's finding of the cell once activated — is performed by machinery the architecture already had. If v0.13 succeeds at the operational level, the success is consistent with two architectural readings the v0.13 batch alone cannot distinguish. The first reading is that the architecture's existing locating machinery handles late-appearing targets at random locations: a substantive finding about the inherited dynamics' generality. The second reading is that v0.13 simply added a new cell whose perceptual properties match the existing attractor cells, and the existing attractor-finding machinery did exactly what it was always going to do: a deflationary reading in which no genuine new locating capability is demonstrated. Both readings are consistent with the v0.13 data.

The pre-registration commits in advance to reporting this honestly. The architectural claim made in this paper is bounded by Category F. v0.15 iterations testing richer locating mechanisms (beacon-style perceptual signals around the cell, learned end-state-detection rather than rule-based detection, signals the architecture must construct rather than retrieve from existing state) are the necessary follow-on for distinguishing the readings, and are reserved.

### 1.4 Findings and their relation to the pre-registration

The paper reports four substantive findings.

The first is the locating-mechanism finding. Among activated runs, conditional discovery rate is 93.9% at 160,000 steps, 82.9% at 80,000, and 46.2% at 20,000. The architectural prediction in Section 4 of the pre-registration — that the inherited Phase 3 dynamics would handle the late-appearing target through the same machinery that draws the agent toward attractors during Phase 2 — is supported. Among 49 activated runs at the longest run length, the agent locates a randomly-placed cell in 46. The mean post-activation discovery time at 160,000 steps is 5,700 steps; the minimum is 2 steps (a run in which the agent happened to be adjacent to the end-state cell at the moment of activation). The locating mechanism works.

The second is the activation-rate finding. The all-attractors-mastered signal fires at exactly the rate the v0.12 batch reported for 6/6 mastery convergence: 13 of 60 at 20,000 steps, 35 of 60 at 80,000, 49 of 60 at 160,000. Under matched seeds, v0.13's activation distribution is the v0.12 inheritance distribution to the run. The pre-registration's Category A discovery-rate thresholds (30/60 at 20,000 steps, 50/60 at 80,000, 55/60 at 160,000) cannot be met by any architecture inheriting v0.12's mastery dynamics, because activation cannot exceed 6/6 mastery rate, and the discovery rate cannot exceed activation rate. Even with a perfect locating mechanism, the discovery-rate ceiling at the three run lengths is 13, 35, and 49 respectively. The thresholds were calibrated against an implicit assumption that mastery convergence rates would approach 60/60; the actual inheritance distribution is the data of record. Section 5.4 addresses this calibration mistake directly as a methodological finding.

The third is the pre-activation preservation finding. The pre-registration committed to byte-identical pre-activation behaviour against v0.12 at matched seeds (Section 7, Category A, Check 4). Implementation surfaced an audit operationalisation issue: the original audit compared full-run aggregate metrics, which include post-activation behaviour for activated runs. The v0.13.1 amendment (Baker, 2026k), committed before any reanalysis, splits the audit into Check 4a (byte-identical, restricted to non-activated runs where pre-activation equals full-run) and Check 4b (post-activation cross-layer effect, characterised). Under the amended audit, Check 4a passes at 83 of 83. The architectural extension v0.13 introduces does not contaminate pre-activation behaviour. The byte-identical commitment is honoured in the population where the comparison applies cleanly.

The fourth is the cross-layer effect finding. Among 97 activated runs, 48 produce threat-layer trajectories that diverge from v0.12 at matched seeds. Mastery dynamics are byte-identical to v0.12 in every activated run; the divergence is exclusively threat-layer. The activated end-state cell's attentional pull alters the agent's post-activation Phase 2 / Phase 3 trajectory, which alters when the agent encounters hazard cells, which alters when threat flags convert and how many hazards are flagged across the run. The direction is bidirectional: in 17 of 48 divergent runs v0.13 records higher hazards-flagged than v0.12, in 11 runs v0.12 records higher; on hazard-entries-p2, 16 versus 13; on first-entry-conversions, 17 versus 10. The end-state cell's pull modestly biases the agent toward more hazard encounters in some runs, fewer in others. This is a cross-layer effect — v0.13's end-state mechanism affecting v0.12-inherited threat-layer behaviour — and it is, structurally, the second such effect the threat-layer programme has documented. Section 4 of this paper makes the parallel to the v0.11.2-mastery-affects-threat-side observation reported in the v0.12 paper explicit and treats cross-layer behavioural effects as a phenomenon-class the programme can now name.

### 1.5 Connection to the broader programme

The v0.13 iteration occupies a specific position in the architectural arc. v0.12 closed the threat-side category-level-generalisation question. v0.13 establishes the locating-mechanism precondition for v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content (Baker, internal record, 24 April 2026, design framework). The competency-gated content reframing — in which hazard cells transform to knowledge cells when the agent's competency reaches a pre-registered threshold — assumes the architecture can locate cells whose perceptual properties have changed mid-run and whose post-transition locations are not known to the agent in advance. v0.13 tests precisely that capability on the simpler case of a new attractor appearing at a random location. v0.13's finding that the inherited Phase 3 dynamics handle late-appearing targets at random locations transfers to the competency-gated content case as a tested precondition: v0.14 / v0.15 inherit a locating mechanism that has been verified to work on the simpler version of the problem.

What the architecture as it now stands does not have, and what subsequent iterations are designed to address, includes: richer locating mechanisms for distinguishing inherited-machinery-suffices from a v0.13-specific locating contribution (reserved for v0.15 or later); the mastery-side equivalent of v0.12's category-level generalisation (whether mastery on one attractor accelerates mastery of others, identified in the v0.12 paper Section 5.1 as the natural symmetry-completion question); and the integration paper (v1.0) that audits the cumulative inheritance chain across v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, v0.13 with their full preservation audits.

The methodological discipline that has held throughout the programme held during this iteration in modified form. Pre-registration was committed before any v0.13 code was written. Implementation completed in a single pass without architectural amendment, despite the implementation surface being substantially larger than v0.12's was — the v0.13 architecture introduces a new cell type, an environmental modification, modifications to the agent's perceptual handling of mid-run cell-type transitions, and new metric tracking. The single-pass-without-amendment outcome is the empirical answer to the methodological question the v0.13 pre-registration raised in Section 9: whether settling design considerations before pre-registration drafting permits clean implementation even when the implementation surface is larger than the previous iteration's.

One amendment was issued after the batch: v0.13.1, scoped narrowly to clarify the byte-identical-preservation audit operationalisation rather than to modify the architecture. The amendment is recorded honestly here and in Section 3.5 as a methodological clarification rather than as an architectural change. The amendment budget for v0.13 stands at one of three.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Environment

The environment is identical to that used in v0.8 through v0.12 (Baker, 2026a, 2026d, 2026g, 2026i, in preparation). A 20×20 grid contains four cell types in the original specification: frame cells form the environmental boundary and are impassable; hazard cells are clustered in two groups (a three-cell cluster at coordinates (5, 8), (5, 9), (6, 8) and a two-cell cluster at (14, 13), (14, 14)) and are passable at scalar cost; attractor cells are six fixed locations at coordinates (3, 3), (16, 3), (9, 10), (4, 15), (15, 16), and (11, 5), at which the agent receives feature reward on entry and which the v0.11 mastery mechanism handles; neutral cells fill the remainder of the passable interior. The agent starts each run at coordinate (1, 1) and operates under the three-phase developmental schedule inherited unchanged: prescribed acquisition (Phase 1), drive-based integration (Phase 2), and preference-weighted autonomy (Phase 3), with phase transitions at the deterministic completion of the boustrophedon path and at 60 per cent of total run length respectively.

v0.13 extends the cell type system with one new type, END_STATE = 4, and modifies the environment construction to sample one end-state cell location at run start. The cell remains a neutral cell perceptually until the activation signal fires. The environment's other elements are unchanged from v0.12.

### 2.2 v0.13 architectural specification

The v0.13 architecture inherits v0.12 unchanged except for the addition of one new cell type, one new activation signal, one cell-type transition, and one banking rule specific to v0.13's end-state cell. The architectural change is contained at the research-question level — the architecture acquires end-state target activation as a single capability — though the implementation surface touches the world, the agent's perception, the action-selection bias machinery, the feature-reward computation, the preference-accumulation logic, and the metric set.

**The new cell type.** The constant `END_STATE = 4` is added to the cell-type constants alongside `FRAME`, `NEUTRAL`, `HAZARD`, `ATTRACTOR`. Cells of type END_STATE are passable, carry feature reward `FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT = 0.15` per visit pre-banking and zero post-banking, and carry primitive attraction bias `ATTRACTION_BONUS = 0.3` for adjacent cells pre-banking and zero post-banking. The reward and bias parameters match the attractor cell parameters exactly. The architectural reasoning, settled in the design conversation: the test is whether the existing attractor-finding machinery re-engages on a new feature-reward source, and tuning the end-state cell's signal stronger would contaminate the test.

**End-state cell sampling.** At run start, the environment selects one neutral cell coordinate at random from `world.cell_type[c] == NEUTRAL` and designates it as `world.end_state_cell`. The selection uses the agent's NumPy random state, but with state-save-and-restore around the sampling draw so that the global RNG state is preserved. The reasoning is methodological: the pre-registration committed to byte-identical pre-activation behaviour against v0.12 at matched seeds, and v0.12 makes no `np.random` calls during world or agent construction. A naive implementation of `_sample_end_state_cell` using `np.random.randint` would consume one RNG draw that v0.12 does not, shifting the agent's RNG state at first action selection and breaking the matched-seed guarantee. The implementation saves the RNG state, performs the sampling draw, and restores the state. The cell selection is deterministic given the run's seed (because the saved state is exactly what `np.random.seed(seed)` produced) but the draw does not advance the global stream.

**The activation signal.** After every step, the agent checks whether `sum(self.mastery_flag.values()) == len(self.world.attractor_cells)` — that is, whether all six attractor cells have been banked. The check is performed in the agent's main loop, after `update_model` and `update_values` have completed for the current step. When the check first becomes true, the agent records the step at which activation occurred (`self.activation_step = step`) and triggers the cell-type transition specified below. Subsequent checks within the same run do not retrigger activation.

**The cell-type transition.** At the activation step, the environment's `cell_type` dictionary is updated for the end-state cell coordinate: `self.cell_type[self.end_state_cell] = END_STATE`. The transition is performed by `world.activate_end_state()`, called by the agent on first activation; the method is idempotent. From the next action selection onward, the agent's `perceive_adjacent` returns END_STATE for adjacent cells at the end-state location, and the action-selection pipeline computes feature reward and primitive attraction bias for the end-state cell as it does for attractor cells.

**The banking rule.** Unlike attractor cells, which bank after `MASTERY_THRESHOLD = 3` entries, the end-state cell banks on first entry after activation. When the agent enters the end-state cell for the first time after activation, the architecture records the step (`self.end_state_found_step = step`), increments the end-state visit counter, and applies the four mastery interventions to the end-state cell parallel to v0.11.2's treatment of mastered attractors: feature reward depletes to zero, primitive attraction bias clears, cell preference resets to zero at this step, and ongoing preference accumulation on the cell is blocked. After the end-state cell is banked, the agent has no remaining feature-reward source in the environment.

**Implementation surface.** The architectural change is contained to one research question — does the architecture support late-appearing target location through the existing locating machinery — but the implementation surface is larger than v0.12's was. v0.13 modifies: the cell-type constant set; `StructuredGridWorld.__init__` to sample the end-state cell coordinate; a new `StructuredGridWorld.activate_end_state` method; the path-planning predicate to recognise END_STATE as passable; the agent's `__init__` to record the activation step, end-state cell coordinate, found step, visit count, and banked flag; the agent's `_primitive_bias` method to give ATTRACTION_BONUS to adjacent unbanked end-state cells; the agent's `feature_reward` method to deliver FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT pre-banking and zero post-banking; the agent's `update_model` method to block preference accumulation on the banked end-state cell; the agent's `record_action_outcome` method to handle end-state cell entry, banking on first post-activation entry, and the activation check after each step; the metric set to record the new fields; and the report and plot rendering to display the end-state cell and its banking status.

### 2.3 What v0.13 does not change

All architectural elements from v0.12 are preserved unchanged. The threat layer operates as v0.12 specifies: the v0.10 three-entry conversion rule applies to the first hazard cell encountered, and the v0.12 signature-matching rule converts every subsequent hazard on first entry. The hard-gate action-selection rule remains: any action targeting a flagged cell is excluded from the action-selection pipeline before Q-value computation, primitive bias computation, or epsilon-greedy random selection. The mastery layer operates as v0.11.2 specifies: the four mastery interventions hold for attractor cells (zero feature reward post-mastery, cleared attraction bias for mastered cells, preference reset at banking, blocked preference accumulation post-banking). MASTERY_THRESHOLD = 3 and MASTERY_BONUS = 1.0 are inherited unchanged. The three-phase developmental schedule, drive composition at each phase, learning rate 0.1, discount factor 0.9, and epsilon 0.1 are inherited unchanged. The pre-wired aversion bias of −5.0 for frame-adjacent actions is retained.

The single-variable-change discipline holds at the research-question level: v0.13 introduces end-state target activation as the singular architectural extension. All prior architectural elements are preserved.

### 2.4 Experimental matrix and matched-seed comparison

The experimental matrix is one architecture (v0.13) crossed with six cost levels (0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0) crossed with three run lengths (20,000, 80,000, 160,000 steps) crossed with ten runs per cell, totalling 180 runs.

For each (cost, run length, run index) triple, the random seed is matched to the v0.12 batch (Baker, 2026i, in preparation) at the same condition. Seeds are loaded from `run_data_v0_12.csv` rather than regenerated, on the technical reasoning named in v0.12's pre-registration Section 5 (Python's hash randomisation per session prevents deterministic seed regeneration; the recorded seeds in the CSV are the data of record). The v0.12 batch's seeds were themselves loaded from the v0.11.2 CSV, which contains both v0.10 and v0.11.2 architectures at matched seeds. The seed chain therefore links v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, and v0.13 at every (cost, run length, run index) cell: same seed, same agent RNG state at construction, comparable trajectories up to the divergence each architecture introduces.

The matched-seed comparison produces a within-seed comparison up to the point at which the activation signal fires. v0.13's behaviour in the pre-activation period — including all attractor mastery dynamics and all threat-layer dynamics that occur before the agent banks the sixth attractor — is byte-identical to v0.12 at the same seed by construction. Divergence begins at the activation step, at which point the end-state cell's perceptual properties change and the agent's subsequent action selection differs from v0.12's.

### 2.5 Metrics

All v0.10, v0.11.2, and v0.12 metrics are retained.

Five metrics are introduced for v0.13. **End-state cell location** records the coordinate of the cell sampled at run start; the location is recorded for analysis of locating-mechanism performance as a function of cell location, distance from start position, and adjacency to other landmarks. **Activation step** records the step at which the all-attractors-mastered signal first fires, or null if the run does not reach all-attractors-mastered. **End-state found step** records the step at which the agent first enters the end-state cell after activation, or null if the agent does not enter the cell within the run length. **End-state visits** counts entries to the end-state cell post-activation. **End-state banked** records whether the cell has been banked.

Two derived metrics are computed for analytical convenience. **Post-activation window** is `num_steps - activation_step` for activated runs (the number of steps remaining after activation). **Discovery time** is `end_state_found_step - activation_step` for runs in which both metrics are non-null (the post-activation discovery time, which is the diagnostic metric for locating-mechanism efficiency).

The headline metric for v0.13's operational claim is the **discovery rate**: the fraction of runs in which the agent enters the end-state cell at least once after activation. The diagnostic metric is the conditional discovery rate: discovery rate among activated runs only, conditioning on the precondition that activation has actually fired. Per-run numerical data is preserved in CSV format; aggregate findings are reported per cost level and per run length below.

### 2.6 Pre-registered interpretation categories

Six interpretation categories are pre-registered in the v0.13 pre-registration (Baker, 2026j), Section 7, with one amendment from the v0.13.1 amendment (Baker, 2026k) that splits the pre-activation byte-identical audit into Check 4a (restricted to non-activated runs) and Check 4b (cross-layer effect characterisation, activated runs).

**Category A** specifies successful late-target location with full preservation, defined by four checks: discovery rate ≥ 30 of 60 at 20,000 steps; ≥ 50 of 60 at 80,000 steps; ≥ 55 of 60 at 160,000 steps; and Check 4a (byte-identical pre-activation behaviour, restricted to non-activated runs) passing at 100 per cent of audited runs.

**Categories B through E** follow the v0.10 / v0.12 pattern. Category B is partial location (at least one Category A discovery-rate threshold passing but not all three). Category C is locating-mechanism failure (substantial below-threshold performance). Category D is over-activation (sub-conditions: activation fires before all six attractors are banked; end-state cell perceptible-as-end-state before activation; end-state cell sampled at a coordinate not in the passable non-attractor scope; agent enters the end-state cell pre-activation and the cell banks). Category E is developmental degradation (pre-activation behaviour fails the byte-identical preservation audit at the matched-seed level, where pre-activation is now restricted to non-activated runs per the v0.13.1 amendment).

**Category F** is the v0.13-specific honesty constraint named in Section 1.3 of this paper. It applies whenever the operational result demonstrates the locating capability, regardless of whether that capability meets Category A discovery-rate thresholds. The architectural claim made in any v0.13 paper is bounded by Category F: the locating capability operates through inherited Phase 3 dynamics rather than v0.13-specific addition.

The pre-registration document was committed to the public repository at github.com/RancidShack/developmental-agent before any v0.13 code was written. The v0.13.1 amendment was committed before any reanalysis of the v0.13 batch was performed.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 The locating mechanism works at high conditional rates

Among activated runs, conditional discovery rate is 46.2 per cent at 20,000 steps (6 of 13 activated runs locate the cell), 82.9 per cent at 80,000 (29 of 35), and 93.9 per cent at 160,000 (46 of 49). The locating-mechanism finding the iteration was designed to test is supported at the longest run length tested.

The mean post-activation discovery time grows with run length: 1,036 steps at 20,000 steps total, 3,395 at 80,000, 5,700 at 160,000. The minimum discovery time at all three run lengths is 2 steps — a run in which the agent happened to be adjacent to the end-state cell at the moment of activation, with the cell-type transition propagating to the agent's next action selection on the very next step. The maximum discovery times are 2,744 steps at 20,000, 14,343 at 80,000, and 46,615 at 160,000. The architecture takes longer at longer run lengths because the post-activation window itself is longer and the agent's exploration distribution covers more of the environment before drawing close to the cell.

### 3.2 Activation rate is bounded by the v0.12 inheritance distribution

The activation rate at each run length matches the v0.12 batch's 6/6 mastery rate at the same matched seeds exactly. The v0.12 batch reported (Baker, 2026i, in preparation, Section 3.4) 13 of 60 runs reaching 6/6 mastery at 20,000 steps, 35 of 60 at 80,000, and 49 of 60 at 160,000. The v0.13 batch reports 13/60, 35/60, and 49/60 activation respectively. The match is to the run.

This bounding is structural rather than coincidental. v0.13 inherits v0.12's mastery layer unchanged. Under matched seeds and identical mastery dynamics, every run that reaches 6/6 mastery in v0.12 also reaches it in v0.13, and every run that does not reach 6/6 in v0.12 also does not reach it in v0.13. The architectural extension v0.13 introduces (the end-state cell and its activation) cannot affect mastery dynamics, because the cell remains perceptually neutral until activation, and activation cannot occur until 6/6 mastery is reached. The only way v0.13 could affect mastery convergence would be if pre-activation behaviour diverged from v0.12 at matched seeds — and Section 3.3 below confirms that it does not.

The implication for v0.13's operational success is direct. Discovery rate is bounded above by activation rate, which is bounded above by the v0.12 6/6 mastery distribution. Even with a perfect locating mechanism (100 per cent conditional discovery), v0.13's discovery rate at the three run lengths is capped at 13/60, 35/60, and 49/60. The pre-registered Category A discovery-rate thresholds of 30/60, 50/60, and 55/60 are above the inheritance ceiling at all three run lengths. They cannot be met by any architecture inheriting v0.12's mastery dynamics. Section 3.4 addresses the threshold-calibration question this raises.

### 3.3 Pre-activation preservation: Check 4a passes

Under the v0.13.1-amended audit logic (Section 3.5), Check 4a tests byte-identical preservation against v0.12 in the population where pre-activation behaviour equals full-run behaviour: runs in which the activation signal does not fire. Across 83 non-activated runs in the batch, the audit comparison covers 17 fields: phase boundaries, frame attempts by phase, hazard entries by phase, hazards flagged, all four threat-layer timing metrics, first-entry conversions, attractors mastered, mastery timing, first banked attractor, and mastery sequence.

**The audit passes at 83 of 83.** Every non-activated v0.13 run produces values matching v0.12's recorded values byte-for-byte across all 17 audited fields. The architectural extension v0.13 introduces — the end-state cell sampling at world construction, the new cell type in the type system, the additional code paths in `_primitive_bias` and `feature_reward` and `update_model` and `record_action_outcome` — does not contaminate behaviour in runs that do not activate. The pre-registration's commitment is honoured in the population where the comparison applies cleanly.

The preservation finding rests on two implementation choices recorded transparently for the methodological record. The first is the global RNG state-save-and-restore around `_sample_end_state_cell`, which preserves the agent's RNG state at first action selection. The second is the gating logic that ensures all v0.13-specific code paths reduce to v0.12 behaviour pre-activation: `_primitive_bias` returns ATTRACTION_BONUS for END_STATE cells only, and the cell type is NEUTRAL pre-activation; `feature_reward` returns FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT for END_STATE cells only, and similarly; `update_model` blocks preference accumulation only on the banked end-state cell, and the cell cannot be banked until activation has fired. Each code path that v0.13 adds is conditional on cell-type or banked-flag state that does not become true until activation. The pre-activation behaviour reduces to v0.12's behaviour by construction.

Mastery dynamics are also preserved across the activated population. Section 3.6 below characterises this.

### 3.4 Discovery rate against pre-registered thresholds: Category A fails

The pre-registered Category A thresholds were 30 of 60 discovery at 20,000 steps, 50 of 60 at 80,000, 55 of 60 at 160,000. The v0.13 batch produces 6, 29, and 46 respectively. All three thresholds fail.

The failure is not in the locating mechanism. As Section 3.1 reported, the mechanism works at 93.9 per cent conditional rate at 160,000 steps. The failure is in the threshold calibration. The thresholds were set against an assumption that v0.13's activation rate would approach 60 of 60 at 80,000 and 160,000 steps; the actual inheritance ceiling is 35 and 49. With a near-perfect locating mechanism, discovery rate at 160,000 steps would still be 47 or 48 — close to the 49-cap of the inheritance distribution but well below the 55-of-60 threshold. The threshold set the bar above the population it applied to.

The honest accounting is that the pre-registration's Section 4 acknowledged this risk (named there as the run-length-dependence prediction), but Section 7's Category A thresholds did not encode the bound the inheritance distribution imposes. The 30/60 threshold at 20,000 steps was calibrated lower than 80k and 160k thresholds in recognition of post-activation-window-budget pressure, but the threshold values themselves were estimates rather than strict derivations from the v0.12 batch's 6/6 distribution. Had the calibration been performed against that distribution directly, the thresholds would have read approximately 6 of 13 (Category A trigger if conditional rate > 45 per cent at 20k), 25 of 35 (> 70 per cent at 80k), 40 of 49 (> 80 per cent at 160k) — and the v0.13 batch produces 6/13, 29/35, and 46/49 conditional discoveries, satisfying all three on a conditional-rate basis.

The paper does not ask the reader to accept post-hoc threshold revision. Section 5.4 addresses the calibration mistake directly as a methodological finding worth recording for future iterations. The pre-registered Category A as stated is not met. The architectural mechanism that Category A was designed to test does work, at the conditional rate the diagnostic metric measures. Both observations are recorded honestly here.

The other interpretation categories are not triggered. Category B (partial location) does not apply because no discovery-rate threshold passes. Category C (locating mechanism failure) is not triggered because the mechanism succeeds when given the chance to operate. Category D (over-activation) is firmly not triggered: zero runs in the batch activated before all six attractors were banked, zero runs found the end-state cell before activation. Category E (developmental degradation) is not triggered because Check 4a passes at 83/83 and mastery dynamics are byte-identical in all 97 activated runs (Section 3.6). Category F (the honesty constraint) applies: the locating capability is performed by inherited Phase 3 dynamics, and the architectural claim is bounded accordingly.

### 3.5 Methodological note: the v0.13.1 amendment

A methodological clarification was applied to the v0.13 audit logic after the batch ran. The original Section 7 Check 4 of the pre-registration named "byte-identical pre-activation behaviour" as the audit goal but operationalised the audit as full-run aggregate metric comparison, which produced 132 of 180 matches and surfaced 48 divergent runs as a Category E (developmental degradation) signal. Investigation of the divergence pattern (audit script in lab record, 25 April 2026) revealed that the audit was confounding two distinct populations.

In runs where the activation signal does not fire — where the architectural extension v0.13 introduces had no behavioural effect — full-run aggregate metrics equal pre-activation metrics by definition. The audit comparison applies cleanly. In runs where activation fires, full-run aggregate metrics include post-activation behaviour, and divergence from v0.12 is expected by design: the activated end-state cell is a new feature-reward source whose attentional pull legitimately alters the agent's subsequent trajectory, including any remaining Phase 2 / Phase 3 hazard encounters. The original audit's full-run comparison treated activated runs as if they were pre-activation runs, surfacing post-activation divergence as a preservation failure when it was instead the expected behavioural consequence of the architectural mechanism.

The v0.13.1 amendment (Baker, 2026k), committed before any reanalysis was performed, splits the audit. **Check 4a** restricts byte-identical comparison to non-activated runs, where pre-activation behaviour equals full-run behaviour and the comparison applies as the original Check 4 intended. **Check 4b** characterises the activated-runs population as a cross-layer effect (Section 4 below) rather than testing for byte-identity. Under the amended logic, the audit produces two separate verdicts: Check 4a passes at 83 of 83, and Check 4b reports 48 of 97 activated runs diverging in threat-layer metrics with mastery metrics byte-identical across all activated runs.

The amendment is operational, not architectural: no code change to `curiosity_agent_v0_13.py` was required, and no batch re-run was performed. The audit logic in `curiosity_agent_v0_13_batch.py` was modified to operationalise the split, and a standalone regeneration utility was provided to apply the amended audit to the existing CSV. The methodological lesson named in the amendment is recorded explicitly here: aggregate metrics that span the architectural-change moment (the activation step) cannot be used as byte-identical-preservation evidence; they must be split into pre-change and post-change components, or the comparison must be restricted to runs in which the architectural change does not fire. The lesson generalises forward: v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content (Baker, internal record, 24 April 2026) will involve mid-run cell-type transitions and will need the same temporal-scoping discipline.

The v0.13.1 amendment is the only amendment issued during the v0.13 iteration. The amendment budget per the pre-registration's Section 10 stopping rule stands at one of three.

### 3.6 Mastery preservation across activated runs

Mastery dynamics are byte-identical to v0.12 in all 97 activated runs. Of the 48 runs that diverge from v0.12 on any threat-layer metric, zero diverge on `attractors_mastered`, zero on `time_to_first_mastery`, zero on `time_to_final_mastery`, zero on `first_banked`, and zero on `mastery_sequence`. The full mastery layer's behavioural signature is preserved in every activated v0.13 run.

This is a substantive preservation finding. The activated end-state cell — a new feature-reward source appearing in Phase 2 or Phase 3 of these runs — does affect the agent's post-activation trajectory enough to alter threat-layer encounter rates in 48 of 97 runs. It does not, however, affect the order or timing of attractor mastery in any run. The architectural reason is direct: by the time activation fires, all six attractors have already been banked. The mastery layer's recording of the run's mastery trajectory is, by activation step, complete. The end-state cell's pull operates only on subsequent steps, by which point the mastery sequence is fixed.

The implication for the paper's preservation claim is that mastery dynamics are preserved at full strength: in the 83 non-activated runs Check 4a confirms byte-identical behaviour, and in the 97 activated runs the mastery-layer fields specifically remain byte-identical. Across the full 180-run batch, mastery preservation holds to the byte. The v0.11.2 individuation findings, the v0.12 first-banked distribution and mastery sequence diversity — all are preserved in the v0.13 batch at the resolution the metrics measure.

## 4. The post-activation cross-layer effect, and cross-layer effects as a programme-level pattern

This section addresses the cross-layer effect Check 4b characterises: the 48 of 97 activated runs in which the end-state cell's attentional pull alters threat-layer behaviour. The section is structured to handle two related observations. The first is the v0.13 finding directly: the cell affects threat-layer encounter rates through attentional redirection. The second is structural: this is the second cross-layer effect the threat-layer programme has documented across two iterations, and the second observation is enough to name the phenomenon-class. Cross-layer behavioural effects, surfaced by matched-seed comparison and invisible to single-iteration architectural decomposition, are an architectural property of this programme.

### 4.1 The v0.13 cross-layer effect characterised

Among 97 activated runs, 48 produce threat-layer trajectories that diverge from v0.12 at matched seeds. The divergence is exclusively threat-layer: zero of the 48 runs show any mastery-layer field divergence. The divergence is concentrated in the threat-layer timing and counting fields: `time_to_final_flag` diverges in all 48 runs, `hazard_entries_p2` in 29, `hazards_flagged` in 28, `first_entry_conversions` in 27, `time_to_second_flag` in 14, `time_to_first_flag` in 11.

Direction analysis on three diagnostic fields is shown in Table 2.

**Table 2.** Direction of cross-layer divergence in activated runs that diverge from v0.12 at matched seeds. Counts indicate runs in which v0.13's recorded value is higher than v0.12's, and vice versa.

| Field | v0.13 higher | v0.12 higher |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| hazards_flagged | 17 | 11 |
| hazard_entries_p2 | 16 | 13 |
| first_entry_conversions | 17 | 10 |

The pattern is bidirectional with a modest bias toward v0.13 recording more hazard activity. The interpretation is structural rather than directional. The activated end-state cell appears at a randomly-sampled neutral coordinate. Depending on where the cell sits relative to the agent's pre-activation Phase 2 / Phase 3 attention concentration, the cell's pull can either draw the agent through hazard regions that v0.12 (without the cell) would have avoided, or pull the agent away from regions where v0.12 would have encountered hazards. In runs where the cell happens to sit in a region the agent would already have explored, the divergence is small. In runs where the cell sits in an unfamiliar region, the agent's trajectory toward the cell may pass through hazard clusters the agent had not previously encountered, increasing flag count and Phase 2 entries. In runs where the cell sits in a familiar region but pulls attention away from a region where the agent would otherwise have wandered into hazards, the divergence is in the opposite direction.

The cross-layer effect is therefore a property of the architecture's coupling: the end-state cell mechanism (v0.13) and the threat-layer dynamics (v0.10 / v0.12 inheritance) interact through the agent's exploration trajectory rather than through any direct architectural connection. The threat layer does not know about the end-state cell; the end-state cell does not modify the threat layer. The two layers are coupled only through the shared substrate of the agent's action selection, which both depend on. Where v0.12 paid x amount of cumulative hazard cost and y number of flag conversions, v0.13 at the same matched seed pays a different amount because the agent took a different path during the post-activation period.

Mastery dynamics, by contrast, are not coupled to v0.13 in the same way. Section 3.6 noted that all six attractors are banked by activation step in every activated run by definition; the mastery sequence is fixed before the end-state cell becomes perceivable. The cross-layer coupling acts only on dynamics that continue after activation, which excludes mastery and includes threat-layer encounter rates.

### 4.2 The v0.12 cross-layer effect: structural parallel

The v0.12 paper (Baker, 2026i, in preparation) reported a cross-layer effect with the same architectural structure as the v0.13 effect characterised above. The v0.10 outlier finding — 5 of 10 Phase 3 cleanness at the cost-5.0-at-20,000-steps cell of the v0.10 batch — was eliminated in the v0.11.2 batch under matched seeds, before the v0.12 architectural extension was applied. The v0.11.2 paper had not surfaced this finding; matched-seed comparison performed during the v0.12 batch did. The mechanism, identified post-hoc in the v0.12 paper Section 4.3: v0.11.2's four mastery interventions changed the agent's exploration pattern through a behavioural side-effect, pulling hazard discovery earlier in the run and weakening the time-budget pressure that produced the v0.10 boundary condition.

The structural parallel to the v0.13 effect is exact. In both iterations, an architectural extension on one side of the architecture (mastery in v0.11.2; end-state activation in v0.13) affects threat-layer behaviour through the shared substrate of the agent's action-selection trajectory, without any direct architectural connection between the modified layer and the threat layer. In both iterations, the effect is invisible to single-iteration architectural decomposition: the v0.11 paper did not report the threat-side cleanness improvement because it was looking at mastery-side findings; the v0.13 paper would not have identified the threat-side divergence except through the matched-seed audit. In both iterations, the effect is bidirectional in detail (v0.11.2's mastery interventions did not uniformly improve threat-side cleanness across all runs; v0.13's end-state cell does not uniformly increase or decrease hazard encounters across activated runs) but biased in aggregate (v0.11.2 improved the worst-case condition; v0.13 modestly biases toward more hazard activity).

### 4.3 Cross-layer behavioural effects as a phenomenon-class

Two observations across two iterations is enough to name the phenomenon-class explicitly. Cross-layer behavioural effects, surfaced by matched-seed comparison and invisible to single-iteration architectural decomposition, are an architectural property of this programme. The two observations the programme has now documented:

- **v0.11.2's mastery interventions affecting threat-side cleanness via post-mastery exploration extension.** Reported in the v0.12 paper as a finding about inheritance dynamics surfaced retrospectively by matched-seed comparison.
- **v0.13's end-state cell activation affecting threat-side encounter rates via post-activation attentional redirection.** Reported in the present paper as a finding directly verified by the v0.13.1-amended Check 4b audit.

The architectural reason is the same in both cases. The threat layer, the mastery layer, and the v0.13 end-state mechanism are presented in the architecture's design as decomposed components with localised responsibilities: the threat layer handles persistent threat representation; the mastery layer handles persistent mastery representation; the end-state mechanism handles late-target activation. Each component has its own state, its own update rules, its own contribution to behaviour. The architectural decomposition is methodologically clean and supports single-variable-change discipline at the research-question level: each iteration changes one component, and the others are preserved.

The components are not, however, behaviourally independent. They are coupled through the shared substrate of the agent's action-selection trajectory. Each component's contribution to behaviour depends on the same underlying decision process (the drive-weighted softmax over Q-values plus primitive bias), and changes in one component's contribution alter what that decision process produces, which alters the cells the agent visits, which alters the experiences other components receive. The decomposition is clean at the architectural level; the behaviour the architecture produces is not decomposable in the same way.

The phenomenon-class observation has three implications for the broader programme.

First, **single-iteration preservation audits are necessary but not sufficient.** v0.11.2's preserved-signatures audit confirmed the four mastery interventions did not disturb v0.10's threat-layer findings at the level of the audit's headline metrics — Phase 3 cleanness counts, hazard flagging rates, and so on. The audit was correct as far as it went; the cross-layer effect on the v0.10 outlier was below the resolution of the headline metrics and surfaced only when the v0.12 batch performed cell-by-cell matched-seed comparison. v0.13's pre-activation audit similarly passes Check 4a at 83/83 in non-activated runs while Check 4b surfaces the cross-layer effect in activated runs only after the audit was operationalised correctly to capture the temporal structure of the architectural change.

Second, **matched-seed comparison is the methodological tool that surfaces cross-layer effects.** The two effects this programme has documented were both identified by matched-seed comparison rather than by aggregate-distribution comparison. In aggregate, v0.10 / v0.11.2 / v0.12 / v0.13 produce broadly similar batch-level distributions; the cross-layer effects are visible only when individual runs at identical seeds are compared cell-by-cell. The programme's methodological commitment to matched-seed comparison across iterations — beginning with the v0.11.2 batch's loading of v0.10 seeds from CSV rather than regeneration — has made cross-layer effects detectable in a way that within-iteration analysis alone does not.

Third, **the v1.0 integration paper will need to characterise cross-layer effects as part of the full-architecture audit.** The v1.0 paper's preservation audit cannot consist solely of pairwise iteration-vs-baseline comparisons. The integration paper must characterise behaviour under the full inheritance chain in matched-seed comparison against each preceding iteration, with explicit attention to fields where cross-layer effects would surface. v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content (Baker, internal record, 24 April 2026) is likely to produce additional cross-layer effects, given that the competency-gated mechanism modifies cells that the threat and mastery layers also handle. The methodological tooling for surfacing these effects is now established; the v1.0 audit can use it deliberately.

### 4.4 The Category F honesty constraint and Section 4 findings

The Category F constraint applies to the architectural claim made in Section 4 in a specific way that is worth naming.

The cross-layer effect Section 4.1 characterises is a behavioural finding: at the level of individual runs, the activated end-state cell's pull alters threat-layer encounter rates. The architectural claim associated with this finding — that end-state activation produces cross-layer effects through the shared substrate of action selection — is consistent with both the substantive reading (the architecture's components are behaviourally coupled in a way that decomposition does not capture) and the deflationary reading (the agent's trajectory after the cell appears differs from the trajectory it would have taken without the cell, which is true by construction and produces no architectural insight beyond restating what activation means).

The phenomenon-class observation Section 4.3 makes is more structural and less easily collapsed. Across two iterations, two architectural extensions on different layers (mastery in v0.11.2, end-state in v0.13) produce cross-layer effects on the threat layer through the same mechanism (the shared substrate of action selection). The fact that two distinct architectural extensions produce structurally similar cross-layer effects suggests that the coupling is a property of the architecture rather than a property of either specific extension. The phenomenon-class claim is bounded by Category F in the same sense as the v0.13 locating-mechanism claim is bounded: the coupling is performed by inherited machinery (the decision process the components share), not by any new architectural mechanism the iterations introduced. v0.15+ work on signature-matching with richer signals would not address this; what would address it is an architectural variant in which the components are coupled not through shared action selection but through some explicit cross-layer interface, which is a substantively different programme.

The v0.13 paper does not propose this variant. The phenomenon-class observation is recorded as an architectural property of the present programme, not as a question whose answer the present paper provides.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Late-target location through inherited machinery

The architectural principle the v0.13 result demonstrates is bounded but identifiable. The architecture's existing locating machinery — the novelty drive, the learning-progress drive, the primitive attraction bias, the preference accumulation, all inherited from v0.11.2 / v0.12 unchanged — handles late-appearing targets at random locations when the target is given a perceptual signature equivalent to the existing attractor cells. At 160,000 steps, 46 of 49 activated runs locate a randomly-placed cell within a post-activation window averaging 123,082 steps. The locating mechanism the iteration was designed to test works at the highest reliability the inheritance ceiling allows.

The principle is identifiable in part because the architecture's drive composition is generic at the cell-type level. The novelty drive operates on cells whose model is uncertain, not specifically on cells of any given type; the learning-progress drive rewards reduction-in-prediction-error on any state-action pair; the primitive attraction bias and preference accumulation are computed per cell at the action-selection step, not as type-specific subroutines. Adding a new cell type that carries the same perceptual properties as attractors (`FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT` and `ATTRACTION_BONUS`) means the existing drives engage with the new cell as they engage with any other feature-rewarding cell. The architecture does not need new locating machinery because its locating machinery is general.

This generality is itself a finding worth naming. The v0.10 / v0.11 / v0.12 iterations have each modified the architecture's cell-handling in specific ways: persistent threat representation for hazards, four mastery interventions for attractors, signature-matching across hazard cells. Each modification is type-specific. The locating machinery underneath these modifications is not. v0.13 demonstrates that the underlying drive composition supports late-appearing target acquisition without architectural extension; what v0.13 contributes is the bookkeeping (a new cell type, the activation signal, the cell-type transition, the banking rule), and the inherited drives do the work.

The principle generalises beyond the threat-layer programme in one specific direction worth flagging. Any architectural extension that introduces a new cell type with attractor-equivalent perceptual properties can presumably be located through the same machinery, regardless of whether the cell appears at run start, mid-run, or in some more complex temporal pattern. v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content (Baker, internal record, 24 April 2026) inherits this principle directly: when a hazard cell transforms to a knowledge cell at the moment competency thresholds are met, the agent's existing locating machinery should engage with the knowledge cell as it would engage with any other late-appearing feature-rewarding cell. v0.13 establishes this transfer; v0.14 / v0.15 will test it on the harder case where the transformation depends on agent state (competency) rather than on a structural fact (all-attractors-mastered).

### 5.2 The Category F honesty constraint applied

Section 1.3 named Category F as the pre-registered honesty constraint on v0.13's architectural claim. Section 4.4 applied it to the cross-layer effect findings. This subsection considers the constraint's application to the locating-mechanism finding directly.

The substantive reading is that the architecture's existing locating machinery handles late-appearing targets at random locations: a finding about the inherited dynamics' generality. The deflationary reading is that v0.13 added a new cell whose perceptual properties match the existing attractor cells, and the existing attractor-finding machinery did exactly what it was always going to do. Both readings are consistent with the v0.13 batch's 93.9 per cent conditional discovery rate at 160,000 steps.

The two readings differ in what they predict for variants the v0.13 batch did not test. If the substantive reading is correct, the inherited machinery should also handle late-appearing targets whose perceptual signature differs from existing attractors — for instance, a cell with a distinctive feature structure that the agent has not previously encountered, or a cell whose location is signalled by perceptual cues at adjacent cells (a beacon variant). If the deflationary reading is correct, these variants would require additional architectural machinery to handle, because the cell's perceptual signature would not engage the existing drives directly.

v0.15 work testing richer locating mechanisms is the methodological vehicle for the distinction. The natural sequence:

- A v0.15 variant in which the end-state cell carries a distinctive feature signal not equivalent to attractor feature reward — for instance, a graded reward that scales with proximity to mastery completion. If the inherited machinery handles this variant at comparable rates, the substantive reading gains support. If conditional discovery rate drops substantially, the architecture's locating capability is more narrowly tied to attractor-equivalent signatures than v0.13 alone can show.

- A subsequent variant in which the end-state cell is signalled by perceptual properties at adjacent cells (a beacon) rather than at the cell itself. If the inherited machinery handles this variant — perhaps through the novelty drive responding to the unfamiliar adjacent-cell pattern — the substantive reading gains further support. If not, the deflationary reading is supported and a new architectural mechanism (something like adjacency-pattern matching, parallel to v0.12's signature-matching but on a different signature space) would be required for richer locating.

These variants are reserved for v0.15 or later. The present paper's architectural claim is bounded: v0.13 demonstrates that the inherited machinery handles late-appearing targets at random locations when the target's perceptual signature matches existing attractor cells. Whether this constitutes a substantive locating capability or a deflationary cell-type-matching artefact is not determined by the v0.13 batch alone.

### 5.3 Cross-layer effects as an emerging architectural pattern

Section 4 of this paper documented the v0.13 cross-layer effect (the activated end-state cell affecting threat-layer encounter rates) and made the structural parallel to the v0.12 paper's v0.11.2-mastery-affects-threat-side observation explicit. This subsection considers what the phenomenon-class observation implies for the broader programme.

The two cross-layer effects share an architectural mechanism. In both cases, an extension on one component of the architecture (mastery interventions in v0.11.2; end-state activation in v0.13) affects another component's behaviour (threat-layer cleanness; threat-layer encounter rates) through the shared substrate of the agent's action-selection trajectory. The components are decomposed cleanly at the architectural level — the mastery layer does not modify the threat layer; the end-state mechanism does not modify the threat layer — but their behavioural consequences are coupled through what the agent does, which depends on all the components together.

Three observations follow.

The first concerns what cross-layer effects are *not*. They are not implementation bugs, architectural inconsistencies, or violations of the single-variable-change discipline. The single-variable-change discipline operates at the research-question level: each iteration changes one architectural component, and the others are preserved at the architectural-state level. The behavioural consequences of the change need not be confined to the modified component, because the agent's behaviour is produced by all components acting on a shared decision substrate. Single-variable-change discipline is satisfied; cross-layer behavioural coupling is the architectural property the discipline reveals when matched-seed comparison surfaces it.

The second concerns the relationship between cross-layer effects and developmental degradation. Category E in the pre-registration framework names "developmental degradation" as a failure mode in which preservation audits show the modified iteration disturbing inherited dynamics. The cross-layer effects this programme has documented are not Category E. v0.11.2's mastery interventions did not disturb threat-layer dynamics in the sense that v0.10's threat layer continued to function as specified — flag conversion, hard-gate action selection, and persistent threat representation all operated unchanged. What changed was the agent's exploration trajectory, which altered when the threat layer's mechanisms encountered hazards. Similarly, v0.13's end-state cell does not disturb the threat layer's mechanisms; it alters when those mechanisms operate on the agent's trajectory. The mechanisms themselves are preserved; the trajectories on which they operate are coupled.

The third concerns methodological tooling. The two cross-layer effects this programme has documented were both surfaced by matched-seed comparison rather than by aggregate-distribution comparison. The v0.12 paper noted this in passing; the v0.13 paper makes it explicit (Section 4.3). The methodological commitment to matched-seed comparison across iterations — the seed chain that links v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, v0.13 through CSV-loaded seeds rather than regenerated formulas — is the tool that makes cross-layer effects visible. Future iterations should preserve this commitment as a primary methodological discipline. The v1.0 integration paper's preservation audit will need to characterise cross-layer effects across the full inheritance chain (Section 4.3), and the audit's tooling is the matched-seed comparison machinery already in place.

### 5.4 The pre-registration's threshold-calibration mistake

This subsection addresses the Category A threshold-calibration mistake directly as a methodological finding worth recording. It is placed standalone rather than folded into earlier discussion because the lesson it carries is general — applicable to any future iteration that pre-registers operational thresholds against architectural mechanisms whose ceilings are constrained by inheritance distributions.

The pre-registration's Section 7 specified Category A discovery-rate thresholds of 30 of 60 at 20,000 steps, 50 of 60 at 80,000 steps, and 55 of 60 at 160,000 steps. The v0.13 batch produced 6, 29, and 46 respectively. All three thresholds fail. The conditional discovery rate — discovery given activation — is 46.2 per cent, 82.9 per cent, and 93.9 per cent, satisfying any reasonable threshold on the population where the locating mechanism actually operates.

The mistake is identifiable post-hoc. The pre-registration's Section 4 anticipated run-length-dependent variation in the post-activation window distribution: it named that some 20,000-step runs would activate too late for any locating mechanism to succeed within the remaining budget, and the 30/60 threshold at 20,000 steps was calibrated lower in recognition. But the calibration did not extend to 80,000 and 160,000 steps. The 50/60 and 55/60 thresholds were estimates of what the locating mechanism should achieve given a sufficient post-activation window, not derivations from the v0.12 batch's 6/6 mastery distribution. The v0.12 batch's 6/6 rates (35 of 60 at 80,000 steps, 49 of 60 at 160,000) were available data at the time the pre-registration was drafted, and the bounding implication — that v0.13's discovery rate is bounded above by the v0.12 mastery rate — was direct enough that the pre-registration should have encoded it.

What the threshold should have been, derivable from the v0.12 distribution, is straightforward. v0.13's discovery rate is bounded above by v0.12's 6/6 mastery rate at the same matched seeds. A reasonable Category A threshold against an inheritance-distribution-bounded mechanism would be a fraction of the inheritance ceiling rather than an absolute count. For instance: discovery rate ≥ 80 per cent of v0.12's 6/6 rate. At 20,000 steps this gives ≥ 11 of 13 (the v0.13 batch produced 6 of 13, failing); at 80,000 steps ≥ 28 of 35 (the batch produced 29 of 35, passing); at 160,000 steps ≥ 40 of 49 (the batch produced 46 of 49, passing). Two of three conditional thresholds pass; the third (20k) fails for the structural reason the pre-registration anticipated, namely that short post-activation windows do not give the locating mechanism reliable opportunity to operate.

The lesson the mistake teaches is methodological. **Pre-registered thresholds for an iteration's operational success should be tied to the inheritance distribution where the iteration's mechanism is bounded by inherited dynamics.** v0.13 inherits its activation-rate distribution from v0.12; the discovery-rate ceiling is therefore set by that distribution rather than by an architectural ideal. Setting thresholds without this bounding produces false-negative Category A verdicts that misread the architectural finding.

The lesson generalises forward. v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content will inherit dynamics from v0.13 and earlier iterations. Any operational threshold for v0.14 / v0.15 should be calibrated against the v0.13 batch's distributions on the relevant metrics — for instance, against v0.13's locating-mechanism conditional rates if v0.14's mechanism depends on locating cells whose perceptual properties have changed mid-run. Pre-registration discipline in those iterations should encode the inheritance bounds explicitly. The same applies to the v1.0 integration paper's preservation audit, which should test cross-iteration distributions for behavioural preservation rather than against external operational thresholds.

The paper does not propose post-hoc threshold revision for v0.13. The pre-registered Category A thresholds as stated are not met; the architectural mechanism the thresholds were designed to test does work, at the conditional rate the diagnostic metric measures. Both observations stand on the public record.

### 5.5 Limits of the present work

Three limits of the present work are worth naming explicitly, beyond the Category F honesty constraint already discussed at length.

The architecture remains tabular and small-scale. The 20×20 grid is sufficient for testing the locating mechanism but does not address scale-related questions. Whether the inherited drives' locating capability scales to environments with thousands or millions of states, whether it generalises to non-tabular representations, whether it interacts with function approximators in ways that introduce drift or catastrophic interference — these are questions the present work does not address. The architectural principle is demonstrated in a sandbox; whether it survives migration to environments where representations must be learned rather than tabulated is a separate research question.

The end-state cell's perceptual signature in v0.13 is attractor-equivalent. The cell carries the same `FEATURE_DRIVE_WEIGHT` and `ATTRACTION_BONUS` as attractor cells, on the design principle that the test is whether the existing attractor-finding machinery re-engages on a new feature-reward source. This choice was deliberate single-variable simplification: the simplest possible test of locating on a late-appearing target is to use perceptual properties identical to existing targets. Whether the same machinery handles targets with different perceptual signatures (graded rewards, beacon-style adjacent signals, learned features) is the question Section 5.2 reserved for v0.15+.

The phenomenon-class observation in Section 4.3 is based on two cross-layer effects across two iterations. Two observations is the minimum number that supports the framing; three or more would strengthen it substantially, and four or more would establish cross-layer behavioural coupling as a robust architectural property of the programme rather than a pattern observed twice. The v0.14 / v0.15 work — whose competency-gated content mechanism is presumably going to interact with the threat layer through similar shared-substrate coupling — provides the next observation, but the present paper does not anticipate its findings. The phenomenon-class claim is bounded by the small number of observations it currently rests on.

### 5.6 Architectural questions opened

Three architectural questions are opened or sharpened by the v0.13 findings, each of which is plausibly addressable through a separate iteration.

The first is the locating-mechanism richness question Section 5.2 named: distinguishing the substantive reading (inherited machinery is general) from the deflationary reading (cell-type-matching artefact) requires testing locating on perceptual signatures the architecture does not directly represent. v0.15 iterations testing graded feature signals, beacon-style adjacency cues, or learned categorical signatures are the methodological vehicle.

The second is the mastery-side equivalent of v0.12's category-level generalisation, which the v0.12 paper Section 5.1 identified as the natural symmetry-completion question. v0.11.2 demonstrates persistent categorical mastery representation; whether mastery on one attractor propagates to other attractors of the same category — an experience of mastering one feature-bearing cell accelerating mastery of others — remains untested. v0.13's success on the threat-side analogue (signature-matching propagation) is suggestive that the principle could work on the mastery side, but the v0.13 batch does not test it. A subsequent iteration extending signature-matching to the mastery layer would close the symmetry across the two persistent layers.

The third is the competency-gated content question Section 1.5 referenced: the v0.14 / v0.15 work emerging from the v0.12 design conversation (Baker, internal record, 24 April 2026), in which hazard cells transform to knowledge cells when competency thresholds are met. v0.13 establishes the locating-mechanism precondition for this work — the architecture can find late-appearing cells at random locations through inherited dynamics — and the cross-layer-effects phenomenon-class observation in Section 4 establishes the methodological tooling the audit will require. v0.14 / v0.15 implementation can begin from this evidence base, with both the architectural mechanism (locating) and the methodological apparatus (matched-seed audit, Check 4a / Check 4b temporal scoping) in place.

### 5.7 The architectural arc through v0.13

The v0.9, v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, and v0.13 papers together trace a five-step architectural arc on the threat-and-mastery side of the architecture. v0.9 identified that the v0.8 architecture's hazard handling lacked persistent categorical knowledge. v0.10 supplied persistent threat representation. v0.11.2 supplied bounded mastery through the four mastery interventions. v0.12 supplied category-level threat-side generalisation through signature-matching. v0.13 supplied late-target activation, demonstrating that the architecture's existing locating machinery handles new feature-reward sources at random locations.

What remains, at the close of the v0.13 iteration, is the v1.0 integration paper that audits the cumulative inheritance chain across v0.10, v0.11.2, v0.12, v0.13. The integration paper's preservation audit will need to test behaviour under the full inheritance chain in matched-seed comparison against each preceding iteration, with explicit attention to fields where cross-layer effects would surface (Section 4.3). The v1.0 paper is reserved for subsequent work.

Beyond v1.0, the v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content is the natural architectural extension. v0.13 has established the locating-mechanism precondition; the cross-layer-effect tooling is in place; the methodological discipline carries forward. The threat-and-mastery programme through v0.13 is, in this sense, complete on its own terms. What follows is integration and extension.

## 6. Conclusion

The v0.13 iteration tested whether the architecture's inherited locating machinery — the novelty drive, learning-progress drive, primitive attraction bias, and preference accumulation that originally drew the agent toward attractor cells — would handle a new feature-reward source appearing at a random location at the moment of all-attractors-mastered. Across a pre-registered batch of 180 runs spanning six cost levels and three run lengths, with seeds matched to the v0.12 batch (Baker, 2026i, in preparation), v0.13 produces four findings.

The locating mechanism works at high conditional rates. Among activated runs, conditional discovery rate is 93.9 per cent at 160,000 steps, 82.9 per cent at 80,000, and 46.2 per cent at 20,000. The architectural prediction the iteration was designed to test is supported when the precondition for v0.13's mechanism is met. The mean post-activation discovery time scales with run length (1,036 steps at 20,000 total, 5,700 at 160,000), and the architecture takes longer at longer run lengths because its post-activation exploration distribution covers more of the environment before drawing close to the cell.

The activation rate is bounded by the v0.12 inheritance distribution. The all-attractors-mastered signal fires at exactly the rate the v0.12 batch reported for 6/6 mastery convergence: 13 of 60 at 20,000 steps, 35 of 60 at 80,000, 49 of 60 at 160,000. v0.13's discovery rate is therefore bounded above by the v0.12 6/6 mastery rate, which falls below the pre-registered Category A discovery-rate thresholds at all three run lengths. The thresholds were calibrated without encoding the inheritance ceiling; Section 5.4 of this paper records the calibration mistake as a methodological finding for future iterations.

The pre-activation behavioural preservation passes the v0.13.1-amended audit at 83 of 83. In runs where the architectural extension v0.13 introduces had no behavioural effect — runs in which the activation signal does not fire — full-run metrics match v0.12's recorded values byte-for-byte across all 17 audited fields. The architectural extension does not contaminate pre-activation behaviour. The v0.13.1 amendment, committed before any reanalysis was performed, clarifies that the byte-identical commitment applies cleanly only in the population where pre-activation equals full-run; the activated-runs population shows post-activation behavioural coupling that is expected by design.

The post-activation cross-layer effect is documented as a substantive empirical finding. Among 97 activated runs, 48 produce threat-layer trajectories that diverge from v0.12 at matched seeds; mastery dynamics are byte-identical in all 97. The activated end-state cell's attentional pull alters the agent's post-activation trajectory, which alters threat-layer encounter rates without modifying any threat-layer mechanism. This is, structurally, the second cross-layer effect the threat-layer programme has documented: the v0.12 paper reported that v0.11.2's mastery interventions affected threat-side cleanness through exploration redistribution; v0.13 reports that the activated end-state cell affects threat-side encounter through attentional redirection. Section 4 of this paper makes the parallel explicit and treats cross-layer behavioural effects as an architectural property the programme can name.

The architectural claim made in this paper is bounded by the pre-registered Category F honesty constraint. The locating capability operates through inherited Phase 3 dynamics; v0.13 contributes the cell type, the activation signal, and the cell-type transition. Whether the inherited machinery's success on this iteration's task constitutes a substantive locating capability or a deflationary cell-type-matching artefact is not determined by the v0.13 batch alone. v0.15 iterations testing richer locating mechanisms are the methodological vehicle for distinguishing the readings, and are reserved.

The methodological discipline that has held throughout the programme held during this iteration with one clarification on the public record. Pre-registration was committed before any v0.13 code was written. Implementation completed in a single pass without architectural amendment, despite the implementation surface being substantially larger than v0.12's was. One amendment was issued after the batch (v0.13.1), scoped narrowly to clarify the byte-identical-preservation audit operationalisation rather than to modify the architecture. The amendment budget for v0.13 stands at one of three at the close of the iteration.

The threat-layer programme has now moved through five iterations: v0.9 identified the unbounded-accumulation problem, v0.10 supplied persistent threat representation, v0.11 closed the equivalent gap on the mastery side, v0.12 supplied category-level generalisation, v0.13 supplied late-target activation. The natural next iteration is the v1.0 integration paper that audits the cumulative inheritance chain. v0.14 / v0.15 work on competency-gated content (Baker, internal record, 24 April 2026) is the architectural extension reserved for subsequent work, with v0.13's locating-mechanism precondition and the cross-layer-effect methodological tooling now in place.
